Music as Poetry
A Talk on My Fourteen Little 
Piano Pieces
This talk was delivered to the composers at Eastman in a composition forum 
in 2004 . It is a talk with didactic aspects about a piece that was written both to 
teach and to please. I spend some time revealing many intracompositional refer-ences. In this I have ambivalence, since I’d prefer the listener or performer to discover such references on his or her own. The references are to other twelve-tone pieces by other composers. References to tonal music would have to be translations across different musical languages, except in the case of quotation, which rarely occurs in the music I have written since 1976 , when I completed my 
trilogy of acculturation pieces. Yet, intercompositional references also abound in my music, due to the nature of the twelve-tone system and other contempo-rary tonal languages that have arisen or been invented in the last eighty or so years. Such systems permit communality.
At the talk’s end, I mention a function for music that has been lost in the 
sociological changes attending music as it has become mediated by recordings, television, and the World Wide Web. Before these media were available, many people played music in their homes for their own pleasure, and there was a considerable market for piano pieces, especially, that were designed for ama-teurs and young people to play. Schubert, Chopin, and Brahms wrote waltzes and other occasional pieces for this audience, and in the twentieth century we have Bartók’s For Children , his set of arrangements of Hungarian folk music, and 
other pieces based on all kinds of Eastern European folk music.
Many of my piano pieces have an intimate character, even if they are quite dif-
ficult to play. Such intimacy may be stirring, even radical, but it is devoid of any rhetorical passages that are mainly designed to impress the listener or show off the performer. I assume Morton Feldman’s attempt to purge his own music of what he called “compositional rhetoric” was a similar move. I might even go as far as to say that this music of intimacy is the opposite of some film music, which, as Aaron Copland once said, works best when one is not attending to it. But the

In the previous talk, I spoke about music as creating worlds, using my song cycle 
Cold Mountain Songs  as an example. That piece concerned the world of the poet 
Han-shan—the world of Buddhist monks and hermits, the world of nature and enlightenment. I showed how the music’s form and arrangement of songs pro-vided a narrative describing Han-shan’s progress on the path to enlightenment.
The idea that music in its content and form should mirror the world is called 
mimesis . Mimesis is one of four concerns or modes of inquiry in literary theory as 
set forth by M. H. Abrams.

In this talk, I’ll be taking a pragmatic stance about a recent piece of mine: 
Fourteen Little Piano Pieces , written in the fall of 2002 .
Literary pragmatism asks what the work is for and about its use. In the face 
of either sophisticated or vulgar censure, many literary critics feel obliged to defend poetry’s relevance to their culture. To the extent that they address what poetry is for, they are pragmatists. In addition, a critic may attempt to counteract 
or forestall a perceived decline in aesthetic values by recreating and maintaining standards. A typical strategy is to evoke older, established—that is, classical—definitions and practices to support and preserve the literary values of one’s time. This is generally known as neoclassicism .
The early neoclassicists were critics such as Horace ( 65–8 BCE) and Longinus 
(213–73 CE), who lived during the Roman Empire. They bolstered their 
arguments for and about poetry by returning to various contrasting aesthetic

In Western concert music, we use the term “neoclassic” to refer to twentieth-
century music between the world wars. This was a music that returned to the classical values of Mozart, as well as to the values of composers such as Bach, who did not contribute to what we call the Classical period in music. Musical neoclassicism was a reaction to a perceived decadence in Western culture that had purportedly led to World War I. However, Stravinsky was well aware that the locus classicus  of literary neoclassicism was in the ancient Greek texts, and 
he often borrowed Greek themes, images, and poetic names for use in his music.
In many ways, any music that reflects on past practices in its own tradition 
is neoclassic, for previous values reassert themselves in current music. In this way, we can think of a given composition as engaged in conversation with other pieces, some of which are exemplars, others of which are on an equal footing with the work at hand. The idea of musical quotation and musical eclecticism are aspects of this ongoing concern of composers who see their work in the context of other musics of their time or before. My Eastman composer colleague David Liptak made this point vividly a discussion of influences on his music from Giya Kancheli and Eastern Orthodox Christian chant. And Carlos Sánchez-Gutiérrez, also a colleague of mine at Eastman, once quipped that every new work is an homage to previous work.
This concern with reference can be a conscious motivation for composition. 
Sometimes the work in question is designed to function pedagogically as it points to older established practices and works. Of course, it must also have aesthetic value for it to be worth listening to. So there is a challenge to make works that exemplify the pragmatic desiderata to teach and to please.
Many works of Bach fit neatly in this category. For instance, Bach titled his 
published keyboard works Clavier-Übung  (Keyboard Practice), which puns on the 
ideas of performance/compositional practice and practicing music. Or consider the written statement in the front matter of the manuscript of Bach’s Inventions and Sinfonias:
Straightforward Instruction, in which amateurs of the keyboard, and especially the 
eager ones, are shown a clear way not only ( 1) of learning to play cleanly in two voices, 
but also, after further progress, ( 2) of dealing correctly and satisfactorily with three 
obbligato  parts; at the same time not only getting good inventiones , but developing the 
same satisfactorily, and above all arriving at a cantabile  manner in playing, all the while 
acquiring a strong foretaste of composition.
Incidentally, the word inventione originally was borrowed by Bach from its use 
in rhetoric; for instance, Cicero ( 106–43 BCE), in his De Inventione , listed five 
stages in creating a speech, namely invention ( inventio ), arrangement ( disposi-
tio), style ( elocutio ), memory ( memoria ), and delivery ( pronuntiatio ). Of course,

Is Bach’s collection of inventions and sinfonias in all common keys, consist-
ing of short contrapuntal pieces, a precursor of my Fourteen Little Piano Pieces ? 
Certainly, but not directly, for not all my pieces are contrapuntal studies, although I try to seek a variety of utterances amid a set of compositional procedures, and many of them involve a cantabile manner of playing.
A much more direct influence on the Fourteen Little Piano Pieces  are the Six 
Little Piano Pieces  of Schoenberg, whose epigrammatic atmosphere I evoke and 
develop. Even more pertinent is the suite of relatively easy twelve-tone piano pieces by Luigi Dallapiccola entitled Quaderno Musicale di Annalibera . This work 
was composed in 1952  for the Pittsburgh International Contemporary Music 
Festival and is dedicated to the composer’s daughter, Annalibera, on her eighth birthday. The English translation of the title, Musical Notebook for Annalibera , con-
nects to the Notebook for Anna Magdalena by J. S. Bach, from which some of the 
inventions were borrowed. Dallapiccola’s piece is in eleven short movements. Each movement of the Quaderno  is based on the same inventione —that is, its 
row—and many different row techniques are employed; thus the piece can be viewed as an introduction to twelve-tone composition. My set of piano pieces resembles Dallapiccola’s in a number of ways; for 
instance, it has a similar scope and lyrical tone, and it is based on a row. In fact, I actually make a direct reference to one movement of the Quaderno . The 
Fourteen  pieces were also written with a pedagogical purpose in mind. Somehow 
I was able to write music that was easy enough for amateurs and piano students to play, which has always been very difficult for me, as my other music is usually quite demanding of its performer. And I definitely want to promote these pieces as offering young, gifted composers “a strong foretaste of composition.” But I also make references to older twelve-tone and atonal pieces by Schoenberg and Webern, as well as to passages in my Cold Mountain song cycle of 1993 .
As in the Bach, Schoenberg, and Dallapiccola works, the pieces from the 
Fourteen can be played separately. However, in my piece I have stipulated a few 
rules for the ordering of an abridged performance.

Intertexuality is secured by another property of the row of Fourteen Little Piano 
Pieces . It can be used to produce self-derived combinatorial arrays. These arrays

“Return to Cold Mountain” also illustrates a patterning device I have used in 
my music since I was a student at Eastman. Here it is employed rhythmically. It involves the permutation of elements according to what is called a “plain bob” in church-bell change ringing. An ordered set—a series—of elements is subject to two transformations in alternation. We label the  n elements of the series with 
index numbers from 0 to n–1. The first transformation exchanges the elements 
of all adjacent pairs that begin with even index numbers; the second transfor-mation exchanges the elements of pairs that begin with odd index numbers. If there are unpaired elements at the beginning and/or end of the series, they remain the same under the two transformations. So given the index numbers 0, 1, 2, 3, the first transformation changes the series into 1, 0, 3, 2; the second 
transformation starting with the same series ( 0, 1, 2, 3) changes it into 0, 2, 1, 
3. The alternation of the two transformations yields the retrograde of the series 
after n transformations, and the original permutation after 2n transformations. 
Another interesting feature of the plain bob patterning is that elements x posi-
tions apart never get closer than x–1 position, or further than x+1 position as the 
transformations work on the series.

I started this talk out by referring to four concerns of criticism, saying that my 
orientation would begin with the pragmatic—that is, to discuss the relation of the piece to its audience. I include in the audience the players and listeners who would study and interpret the music, as well as those who might listen to it in concert. Here and there I have spent some time discussing connections between my musical aims and those of poets. Indeed, I consider the Fourteen pieces to be 
musical poems, and as such, they refer and allude—just as poems do—to other pieces, by other composers and by me, as well as to each other and internally. Thus my discussion moved gradually from external to internal relations, moving from pragmatism (and its connection to neoclassicism) to objectivism, the study of what makes a work work.
But if there is one feature of poetry I would like music most to emulate, it is 
that one can read poetry anywhere, not only at poetry readings. I would there-fore hope that people would play pieces like my Fourteen Little Piano Pieces , not 
only in concert for others, but at home for themselves, for solace and enjoyment. After all, music is not only performance, but intimacy, and all that goes with it.
