Addendum: The Array and the Unit
The reader familiar with serial composition, especially with arrays—what I have 
called “compositional designs”—will note that in this paper I distinguish between an array column and a compositional unit.
25 The array column is an abstract 
entity ready to be composed into music, and the unit is the result of that process. Nonetheless, we often speak of passages of serial pieces as instantiating or even being identical to arrays in locutions such as “the array begins at m. X.” Certainly it is clear that the array is not the piece but contributes to it in some (hopefully) meaningful way, for the relation of an array to a piece is prospective, and as such—like a pitch-class to a pitch—it is one-to-many, not a deterministic map-ping (like one-to-one or many-to-one). This simple point can be forgotten when doing analysis or writing music so that the properties of the array are assumed to be manifest in the music just because the array can be shown to have such prop-erties. Writing music with a combinatorial twelve-tone array doesn’t mean the listener will automatically be able to hear the local aggregate saturation or the rows that make up the array as such (or anything); or, if one determines that a hodge-podge of notes are rows based on an array, the passage does not suddenly become audibly or conceptually clear.
I therefore find it useful to make a distinction between parts of an array (or 
any other abstract compositional object) and a compositional unit. When com-posing Each Time , I decided that the array columns were to be composed into 
units. Yet, another piece might not be based on this correspondence; the unit might transgress the array boundaries of column and row and connect different parts of the array together, or not be determined by the array’s structure (alone or at all). Or one might not articulate units by measures in the first place. In fact, compositional units need have nothing to do with arrays or other sorts of com-positional entities or methodologies—nor does a composition have to be based on discrete or overlapping compositional units, either.

After studying each of the twelve units in figure 5.23, the reader may note 
how, despite their differences, they all sound alike. This is no mystery; the same ordered sets in the same six registers underlie each unit. Because of their simi-larity, if all of the units of figures 5.23 were found in different locations in the 
same piece, would one unfailingly note their various appearances?
Yes and no. For even though it is useful to compose and hear a composition 
as a succession of units, given a single unit out of context, we do not know what might precede or follow it. Would the composition and partition of surrounding units be similar or different? Would horizontal weighting tie—even literally—notes from past or to future units? What kinds of textures and continuities would frame a unit’s musical actions? After all, context changes how we hear a unit; this is one of the meanings of the Buddhist concept of pratityasamutpada  (dependent 
origination) I discuss in the essay on musical time.  If we were to replace unit 53 
of Each Time  with one of the units in figure 5.23, the whole composition would 
ultimately be affected—links between the former unit 53 and others would be 
broken and the replacement would associate with the rest of the piece in new and perhaps unforeseen ways. (Of course, any experienced composer has more

As I have said, there are a great number of legal orderings of an aggregate of 
seven registers, because pitch-classes in one register can be aligned (alone or in “chords”) with the pitch-classes (alone or in chords) in any other register. This fact alone shows that composing (so-called) tonal music is much more struc-turally constrained than composing with arrays. Of course, working with arrays might seem more constrained because one has previously made music adhering 
to some variety of tonal syntax, such as that found in jazz or popular music.

However, as one becomes accustomed to array composition, it becomes clear that the opportunities for implementing long-range musical associations that do not compromise compositional individuality are vast. Imagination is what counts, not the contingencies of chains and aggregates in arrays.
