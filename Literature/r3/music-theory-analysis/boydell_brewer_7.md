Deeply affected is the way nature lets me know what I need to know 
about the mysteries of form. I sense this in the way the trail takes me places, sometimes knowingly, other times by surprise. But always, at the end, there is an inevitable moment where nature takes over, where the trail is no longer humanly contrived, but, instead, and because of its contrivance (or in spite of it), I find myself overwhelmed.

I must also mention Henry Brant’s antiphonal music, which certainly partakes of a spatial sensibility akin to outdoor music. Other contempo-rary composers who have written music to be played outside include Karlheinz Stockhausen, John Cage, and most notably Alvin Curran.

As a child I always enjoyed walking outdoors in natural settings such as forests 
and parks, but in the 1990 s I developed a passion for hiking. I’ve especially 
enjoyed walking in the deserts of the southwestern U.S., in Arizona and Utah. Spending time out-of-doors led to a change in my compositional thinking.
In the summer of 1996 , I had been hiking for fun near Rochester, New York, 
on an intricate web of ski trails in Webster Park. I was so fascinated by these trails that I made a map of them. I felt a connection between these trails and the music I had just been composing, so I thought it would be interesting to bring them together in a musical composition. This music would not be concert music per se, but something like ambient music, where the music would merge with the environment—in this case, with the sounds and sights of the fields and forests of the park.
The result was my first major outdoor piece, Playing Outside , composed from 
January to August 2001 .

Many of the experiences we have with natural events out-of-doors are without 
self. We are not witnessing nature; we are nature, not simply within it. Unlike 
some of the nature poetry of Wordsworth and other English Romantics, where traces of the self exist within an attendant mental contemplation that connects with moral and religious sentiments, there are other genres of nature poetry in which the ego is absent. I’m thinking of Japanese haiku and earlier Chinese poems.
5 An example is Basho’s poem:

orientation.

Haiku interested me exactly because it reflected so well what I had felt when 
looking at the clouds, or stars at night, or walking in the woods or countryside. I experienced a freedom from the self with all its cares and worries, something I also encountered in other activities, such as playing the piano or listening to music.
To his credit, Emerson recognizes all this and more. Let me put the above 
quote in context:
In the woods, we return to reason and faith. There I feel that nothing can befall me 
in life,—no disgrace, no calamity, (leaving me my eyes,) which nature cannot repair. Standing on the bare ground,—my head bathed by the blithe air and uplifted into infi-nite space,—all mean egotism vanishes. I become a transparent eyeball; I am nothing; I see all; the currents of the Universal Being circulate through me; I am part or parcel of God. . . . I am the lover of uncontained and immortal beauty. In the wilderness, I find something more dear and connate than in streets or villages. In the tranquil landscape, and especially in the distant line of the horizon, man beholds somewhat as beautiful as his own nature.
7
Emerson is not talking about the country (or suburbs) versus the city—or 
as he puts it, “streets and villages.” His experience of the woods is egoless, but not an out-of-body experience, where the self flies free of the body. Perhaps Emerson is really refining or redefining what has been called “the sublime” in Western thought.
The sublime has been a topic in literary criticism since Longinus’s treatise on 
the subject, written sometime in the first centuries of the Common Era. More recently, the word “sublime” not only points to something that is so awe-inspir-ingly beautiful it seems divine, but it also can indicate something that is threat-ening or scary, such as a fierce thunderstorm or earthquake. The word is from the Latin sublimis , meaning “looking up from.” This implies that the place of the 
perceiver is below or inferior to that which is sublime.
Edmund Burke thought of the sublime as opposite to “the beautiful.”
12 Beauty 
was something that aroused affection in the perceiver, while the sublime could be inherently ugly or deformed, without fixed or stable boundaries. However, Burke thought that both the beautiful and the sublime could arouse pleasurable feelings—hence, the combination of fear and attraction in one’s encounters with the sublime.
Immanuel Kant thought of the sublime as “the name given to what is abso-
lutely great.”
13 He noted that beauty is dependent on the nature of the form of 
an object, whereas the sublime may be without form or boundaries. He further distinguished between the mathematical and dynamical sublime. The mathe-matical sublime has to do with the limitlessness of the infinite, as in this pseudo-definition of the extent of the natural numbers: the highest number is one higher than the highest number you can think of. Kant states that the dynamical sublime is like “nature, considered as in an aesthetic judgement as might [i.e., power] that has no dominion over us.”
14

Eastern cultures tend not to separate the beautiful from the sublime. And the 
contemplation of nature may not be the path to release. In India, at the time of the first Upanishads, monks lived in the forest as a form of asceticism, for these forests were often jungles, or other forms of wilderness, infused with a sense of danger and alienation.
Early Buddhist writings in the Pali Canon record encounters with nature that 
stimulate a desire for transcendence. In her book Buddha , Karen Armstrong 
cites a passage from the Majjhima Nikaya :
A king in a state of acute depression took a drive one day through a park filled with 
huge tropical trees. He dismounted from his carriage and walked among their great roots, which were themselves as tall as an ordinary man, and noticed the way that they “inspired truth and confidence.” “They were quiet; no discordant voices disturbed their peace; they gave out a sense of being apart from the ordinary world, a place where one could take refuge from people” and find a retreat from the cruelties of life.
17
Here nature is a refuge inspiring one to seek something similar in one’s own life. 
In later Mahayana sutras, the presence of the Buddha transforms nature itself into something supernatural.
When the Buddha had finished preaching the sutra, he sat with his legs crossed in 
lotus position and entered into the samadhi of the place of immeasurable meanings, his body and mind never moving. At that time heaven rained down mandarava flowers, great mandarava flowers, manjushaka flowers, and great manjushaka flowers, scattering them over the Buddha and over the great assembly, and everywhere the Buddha world quaked and trembled in six different ways.
18
Even today in India and Indian-influenced cultures, nature is graded in its 
auspiciousness. The tops of mountains are the purest places, also often consid-ered holy, as in the West. Rivers carry this purity into the lower lands, locations of dubious purity. The ocean is considered dangerous and is the most polluted form of nature. Certain rituals are performed on the beach to cleanse oneself, so the spiritual refuse flows away into the sea.

During the T’ang dynasty, writings and art began to reflect a great veneration 
of nature that continues up to the twentieth century.19 Paintings, poetry, and 
travel journals were devoted to depicting nature, to the exclusion of images or objects associated with social realms. Paintings are typically of mountains and water, with at most a few isolated people, often monks, insignificantly dotting the landscape. Paintings and ink drawing were often executed on long scrolls that could be unrolled in segments to show various scenes. The geometry underly-ing such depictions has a different sense of perspective than Western painting after the Renaissance. There is no center or single vanishing point. The scroll is more like a musical score. And it captures an aspect of our consciousness: as we encounter new sensations and perceptions, the old ones fade away in a con-tinuous process of initiation and decay. In this way the scroll represents not only nature itself, but also how we perceive nature or anything else. In any case, the lack of people in Chinese nature pictures and poems also allows room for one to merge into the scene without self-consciousness.

As is well known, Beethoven titled his sixth symphony Pastoral Symphony or 
Recollection of Life in the Countryside . The phrase “recollection of life” indicates 
that he was just as concerned with feeling and memory as with depiction. To this end, he gave titles to each movement such as “The awakening of emotions upon arriving in the village” and “Pastoral song—feelings of joy and gratitude after the storm.” Yet this work is no mere story of the trials and tribulations of shepherds and shepherdesses. Beethoven attempts not only to express in music a sense of the countryside, but also to give voice to the power and depth he felt out-of-doors. While harmonic movement is often slow in other Beethoven pieces written at the time of the symphony, the pacing of events in this work is even slower and more relaxed, with much repetition of themes and motives. Modulations are not subtle, but often juxtapose keys—really extended chords—with an abruptness that evokes the sudden changes in some natural features and processes. The second movement’s attempt to picture a brook in sound involved Beethoven in some interesting experiments in orchestration. There is some-thing both earnest and delightful in this music, which is not self-expression, but a composer’s attempt to express what there is to see and hear outdoors and how that feels, not what is “significant” about these scenes to him or his time. (The use of birdsong is not just cute, but one aspect of Beethoven’s desire to be true to what is out there and needs no mediation.)
A number of more recent composers have also overtly communicated their 
appreciation of nature in music. Charles Ives’s Central Park in the Dark  and the 
third movement of Three Places in New England , “The Housatonic at Stockbridge,” 
resemble Beethoven’s attempts to be true to the outdoors as he felt it, but in more literal ways. We hear mist and river-flow made into music wherein the music sound and the natural sound coincide without a degradation of musical value. Béla Bartók’s various “night music” pieces also participate in this mix of nature and art. It’s easy to hear the sounds of insects and birds and other things in Bartók’s woods, including humans singing from afar. Again the music that supports the sonic reference is admirably crafted and written. George Crumb has expanded on Bartók’s night music in many of his own pieces. Even more literal are the works of Olivier Messiaen that employ birdsong written out and even labeled as to species in the score. Such catalogues of things, including modal scales, Hindu rhythms, letter codes for notes, play an important role in Messiaen’s goal to express religious sentiment and mysticism in his music.

But perhaps Cage was the first composer to be influenced by non-Western 
conceptions of nature. In his “An Autobiographical Statement,” written in the late 1980 s, he remarks:
I could not accept the academic idea that the purpose of music was communication,

I determined to give up composition unless I could find a bet-ter reason for doing it than communication. I found this answer from Gita Sarabhai, an Indian singer and tabla player: The purpose of music is to sober and quiet the mind, thus making it susceptible to divine influences. I also found in the writings of Ananda K. Coomaraswamy that the responsibility of the artist is to imitate nature in her manner of operation. I became less disturbed and went back to work.

Cage soon found further inspiration in Zen Buddhism, a belief system, as we

For me, the importance of Cage’s idea to “imitate nature in her manner of 
operation” is that he understood that a “natural” music isn’t required to literally state, imitate, or symbolically code what we see or hear in nature. Rather, such music could be a form of nature, not as music had been previously conceived, separate from nature yet “expressing” it. This meant to me that my music would eventually have to leave the concert hall, and that I should encourage listeners to hear my concert music in the way we take in the world of nature, as process and flow.
I cannot say exactly when this idea became clear to me, but by 1996  I began to 
think about having some of my concert music played outside. Shortly thereafter I saw that an out-of-doors music would have to be expressly composed for an outdoor occasion or venue—that performing a concert piece outside was not only impractical, but perhaps inappropriate, and not only for acoustic reasons. Nevertheless, as I look back I can now see a direct line leading to my first out-door piece, Playing Outside . For many years, I was only dimly aware of a connec-

Hiking in nature has other connections with music. One walks outside, in, 
within natural surroundings, visiting places not everyone can get to. When one meets other people on the trail, there is an immediate bond, for only those who share similar values will be out there in the first place. People push themselves, a bit or a lot, to get to interesting places. The challenge of driving oneself to new heights of endurance, without getting lost or hurt, is often a motivation. The need to be careful and prudent amid potentially dangerous surroundings helps to keep a one-pointed mind. In addition, there’s a keen beauty in just walking in a place without urban signs or conveniences; this promotes a sense of what one might call natural elegance, of being in tune with one’s surroundings. One soon understands that all parts of the trail are the goal—not just the vista at its so-called end. The result is a loss of self-consciousness; you are no longer other than the landscape; you are simply your perceptions. Thus, we return to Emerson and his transparent eyeball.
Playing Outside
So, in retrospect, it was inevitable I would eventually compose music to be liter-ally played outside. I will therefore now describe my first concerted attempt to make this happen, my composition of 2001 , Playing Outside . This composition is 
not concert music played outdoors, even though it is written for musicians who ordinarily play concert music. The music is less formal than concert music and employs special notations to permit various degrees of improvisation. The pac-ing of the piece is often relaxed, spacious, and sometimes ritualistic, in contrast to the social rhythms of music in most cultural settings.
I chose the title Playing Outside  since it so pertinently suggested my musical con-
ception. In jazz, “playing outside” denotes the practice of playing without chords, either modally or atonally, and without clear meter or even beat; the emphasis is on gesture and timbre. The allusion of the title to sports such as baseball suggests a conception of time where there is a distinction between “time in” and “time out.” A baseball game, for instance, has times during which the game action takes place versus times between these actions, between innings, batters, pitches, etc. The one-hundred-minute span of the piece contains people making music (time in), but also people moving from one place to another, enjoying the natural set-ting, sharing all the sights and sounds in the park (time out).
The implementation of the composition and the performance took some

Why should I add my music and sounds to those in the woods?
Cities have parks. What are they for? Recreation. That is, re-creation, returning to

Certainly, cities have architectures that articulate space in the same way forests, fields, and hills do in the country. In jungles, people live as closely as in apartment houses. (This can be of course perverted into conquering, exploiting, and colonizing nature—for the puta-tive good of “mankind,” or worse, in the name of a definition of “man” that excludes other men, women, animals, plants, terrain, and space itself.) People who live close to the land have so-called “traditional” or “folk” music, often

What kinds of sounds should I use? What forms, what processes?It appears that the sounds of nature are not aesthetically arranged; they are purely

So how can my music “harmonize” with the woods’ sounds, rather than imitate those sounds, drown them out, or impose others?
I take my cue from the forms in the woods, where nothing is exactly uniform or sym-

So this music has no “themes” or “accompaniments,” no clearly articulated voices, 
no sections, nothing exactly repeated. Its form is flow. Not the fixed forms or formulas of some traditional music, but formation and transformation, multiple processes of growth and decay, continuously developing, not without points of repose or abrupt change. Flux again and again.

Since 2001 , I have composed four more outdoor pieces: Coming Down to Earth 
(2002 ), Oracle  (2005 ), Sound/Path/Field  (2006 ), and Arboretum  (2008 ). Each of 
these pieces differs from Playing Outside  in theme, formality, place, ensemble,

pitches and time divisions of the minute are guided by a chain or cycle of notes, containing all instances of a set of harmonies (different set-classes). They are all notated in a similar way.

In many ways, Coming Down to Earth  resembles a Japanese or Chinese garden. In such a 
garden, natural objects such as rocks, plants, soil, conifers are shaped and arranged to produce an inviting atmosphere to be explored by visitors. There are many paths and views, and the garden’s boundaries are unclear. My piece was designed to have many of these features. The sounds are often like those found outdoors, slowly evolving, or having the characteristics of animal cries and birdcalls. The experience of the music is like watching sunsets, clouds passing, or sea changes. 
Sometimes there are abrupt changes, like a sudden gust of wind or a startling animal sound. Musical attention is akin to noticing and enjoying the subtle differences among flowers, leaves, plant morphology, birds, animals, and insects and their sounds.

ancient Chinese oracular book still used for prediction and guidance.
In keeping with Oracle ’s leisurely character, I will elaborate on the theme of 
this piece and what it has to do with its musical structure. In China, oracles used various techniques of divination to help foretell the future. The Chinese thought of the world in flux, always changing—in fact, the title I Ching  is usually 
translated as The Book of Changes . Therefore, to get insight into one’s future or 
to get advice on how to meet challenges, one performed a ritual, which func-tioned to free the question asked from any particular human intervention or desire. This deliberate impersonality would help make contact with the state of change at the time of the ritual to determine what to do and how to do it. This is very much the way we treat weather reports (which might not be any more reliable than consulting an oracle). In the case of the I Ching, one threw sticks whose various patterns were interpreted in terms of whether they formed solid or gapped (or broken) lines. These lines came in two groups of three; so there were two systems, two pairs of eight possible states with three solid or unbroken lines, and sixty-four states with six solid or unbroken lines. Each of these sixty-four line patterns was identified by a little diagram of six lines called a hexa-gram, and each hexagram was accompanied by brief text, which was consulted when a particular pattern came up in the ritual. These texts were highly esoteric; therefore commentaries were written on the original texts to pass on the tradi-tion of interpretation; as a result, when the I Ching  was compiled, there were 
many texts that were used to interpret what a pattern of lines meant. Today the I Ching  has been translated into many languages and consists of a chapter for 
each of sixty-four hexagram texts.
John Cage was fond of using the I Ching  to determine structural choices in his 
so-called chance or indeterminate compositions. I do not use the book in this way, but it did determine the form of Oracle .
As I mentioned before, the composition is in sixty-four sections, each lasting 
a minute, with each section based on a different hexagram. Each section has a nuclear or toniclike note and a chord that goes with it. However, two notes do not occur in the sequence of sixty-four nuclear notes: B and E ♭. All the different 
types of tetrachords are used for the chords, exactly twice, arranged in a retro-grade pattern. (This is shown in figure 9.5.)

These three categories were helpful when I was designing the structure and flow of the piece.

I began composing Sound/Path/Field  in the winter of 2006 . First I visited the 
quad several times, to assess the structural potentials of the space. I felt that this was best done in the absence of foliage and people enjoying themselves outside. The design of the entire piece often specifies that a section have certain charac-teristics, such as busy or sparse, high or low, quantized or continuous. Such musi-cal contrasts animate any piece or music, but in this case, they were intended to heighten an awareness of space and time. After designing the structure, which included matrices of the time it takes to walk from one performance location to another, I began writing the indoor pieces, reinterpreting the structural plan at different time ratios, as mentioned above. I therefore built into these pieces exact correlations with the whole work, ones which can be heard and enjoyed.
I composed the indoor pieces in my normal way, at a desk and piano. But 
the outdoor portions of the work were composed outside, at various waterfalls in western New York State. Some of the falls were small, others quite tall; many were in parks, a few in uncultivated woods; some were accessible by road, some were difficult to reach.
My compositional practice for writing the outdoor music involved a cycle of 
three activities. First, the night before, I would prepare music notebooks, one for each ensemble, with information from the charts for the units I would com-pose in the following morning. I would then go to bed and awake with ideas about what I wanted to write and how to write it. The next day, I would travel to a trail. As I entered the woods, many ideas and sound images would present themselves. I would hike to a waterfall, settle down, and write the music. Then I would return home and copy everything into the computer to make the score. This would take usually a day and a half, so the compositional cycle was two days long.
