Figure 6.17a shows a passage based on an eight-row combinatorial array. This 
music occurs late in the piano solo. The array following the example shows the rows lined up forming primarily dyads with a few trichords. All of these small sets are members of set-classes found within the row, but can of be of any two-pc set-class since the row is an all-interval series. Still, no tritones occur in an array cell. In composing this combinatoriality into music, any correspondences between the local configurations of notes and the longer set-classes in the row are a matter of composition, not inherent in the array of rows. The improvised drum solo leads to the return of the head. Whether or not there is a climax at the return of the head is up to the drummer, but in all performances I have heard, the drummer always builds up his solo to a furious conclusion that breaks at the entrance of the B section of the head.
Despite the complexity—and at the time of composition, novelty—of the 
compositional thinking that created Not Lilacs , the composition was completed 
in about a month, with sporadic interruptions by other projects and academic duties. The process was continually exhilarating and often felt improvisational as I discovered new interrelations and expansions of the rows and arrays. I under-stood firsthand what Schoenberg meant when he said “craft is imagination.” At the end, I realized that the serial techniques I had developed were hardly exhausted in the composition of this piece, so I went on to compose other serial works and have not found an end to this fruitful land even to this day. What started out as a jazz island turned out to be a continent.
