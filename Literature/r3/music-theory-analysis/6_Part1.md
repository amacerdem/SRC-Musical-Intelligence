Why Not Lilacs
This text is a written version of a talk I’ve delivered on at least ten occasions in 
various forms and venues since 1974  when I first presented it to my graduate 
class on twelve-tone music at Yale University.1 While I reveal some of my experi-
ences and knowledge of modern and free jazz in the talk, here I will recount a few more personal experiences that greatly affected my appreciation of jazz as a young man.
As a teenager in the late 1950 s, I took piano lessons in New York City in both 
Steinway Hall and Carnegie Hall. I would travel from Yonkers to Manhattan every week and before and after lessons spend time visiting music and record stores and attending concerts. One of my friends was interested in jazz and introduced me to the music of various jazz artists such as Gerry Mulligan, Bob Brookmeyer, Thelonious Monk, and Ornette Coleman. Coleman was completely controver-sial at the time, played a plastic saxophone, and was considered to be a genius by Gunther Schuller, the then-young composer, conductor, and jazz scholar. When I heard Coleman’s first album, The Shape of Jazz to Come , and his now-classic com-
position Lonely Woman  I was astounded and saw how his music was akin to the 
progressive contemporary music of Varèse and others I was getting to know at the time.
When I went to the Eastman School to study composition, I found that most 
of the other freshman composers were jazz players and of such high quality that they were able to pay for their tuition by taking “society gigs” in town and playing at resorts in the Catskills in the summer. Nevertheless, jazz was not yet an estab-lished program of study at Eastman, and my friends had to learn what they could on their own. They founded a jazz band, but it was only permitted to rehearse on Saturday afternoon, with warnings from the brass department that playing jazz could ruin one’s lip for playing orchestral music. The only other outlet for playing real jazz—opposed to the popular music they were paid for—was in bars and coffeehouses in town. After finishing up a gig on Saturday at one a.m., my composer colleagues would gather at a coffeehouse and play bebop and even free jazz until dawn. I attended many of these informal sessions after which we all went out for breakfast. It was here that I heard many bebop compositions and the wild and exciting improvisations that ensued. When I began thinking about writing music that explored the ways different

I had some introduction to scholarly research on these non-Western musics when I studied ethnomusicology in graduate school. All this Asian music was in my musical consciousness when I came to teach, first in 1968  at the University of

junior colleagues were jazz musicians, and so I found myself among jazz people again.
My Acculturation Trilogy , written from 1971  to 1976 , was heralded with a

the piece; it seemed exactly right for the piece, even though I had no idea what it meant, and it reminded me of the whimsical titles of various bebop composi-tions such as “Ko-ko,” “Klactoveedsedstene,” and “Moose the Mooche.” In any case, it laconically announced that the composition was not going to be in the vein of Tchaikovsky’s Lilac Fairy or Rachmaninov’s song “Lilacs.” A few years later it dawned on me that the title referred to Rochester, New York. The year 1973  was an important one for me as a composer. I had begun a com-
positional project based on the anthropological concept of acculturation—how one learns a foreign culture—that brought together many of my stray musical interests in older Western classical music, non-Western musics, and various for-mal ways of allowing vastly different musics to “talk to each other.” I had also begun a deep study of the twelve-tone system as enunciated by Milton Babbitt and Donald Martino, and how that kind of musical thought was related to what was then called “set-theory” at Yale. These two musical vectors met head-on in two of the chamber pieces I wrote for the acculturation project: Motet on Doo-
dah, for flute, bass, and piano; and Not Lilacs , for alto sax, trumpet, drums, and 
piano. Both use combinatorial pitch structures, but in combination with musi-cal features found in other forms and genres of music. Motet on Doo-dah  com-
bines a twelve-tone canon based on Stephen Foster’s “Camptown Races” with the isorhythmic structures of ars nova  motets and the ornamental vocabularies of 
ancient Korean art music (called Ah-ak ). Not Lilacs  combined serial procedures 
with jazz.

I did not read these program notes until after I had completed Not Lilacs ; 
however, if I had, I would have noted that Babbitt’s ideas about his piece were not mine about Not Lilacs .
6 I was trying to make my piece be as much it could 
be as both jazz and serial music. (But I should say jazz and serial music are not the same kind of thing. Jazz is a multifaceted repertoire and serial music is a dubious category of music, where compositional technique takes precedence over style.) In any case, unlike Babbitt, I was interested in composing a piece that could be “heard” as either jazz or new music, or even as both at once. Ironically, as I will describe later, it was my researches into atonal and serial structure that made this possible, not my musical experiences with jazz. Another contrast with Babbitt’s All Set  is that if All Set  refers to a “jazz instrumental,” 
it most likely alludes to the ensemble music of perhaps Stan Kenton or Duke Ellington, whereas the jazz that was in my mind was the progressive bebop and free jazz I had heard as a teenager on disc and in Manhattan and Rochester, NY, of artists such as Miles Davis, Charlie Parker, Thelonious Monk, Charles Mingus, and Ornette Coleman. And in my twenties I became aware of the even more far-reaching music of Cecil Taylor, John Coltrane, Anthony Braxton, and other new developments in jazz.
The question of whether Not Lilacs  can be received as jazz without question 
was answered positively on its first performance at Yale and subsequently. The premiere was part of a special concert that members of the composition depart-ment—faculty and graduate students—called “Music under the Influence of Jazz.” Unlike me, many of my colleagues, such as David Mott and Robert Moore, were also practicing jazz musicians,. They provided bona fide jazz compositions, as well as jazz-influenced compositions to the concert. We anticipated that this concert would be a great success since there was large and appreciative audi-ence for both jazz and new music in New Haven, and at the performance the hall was sold out. Not Lilacs  was first on the program, and it was very enthusiasti-
cally received. However, most of the other pieces on the program were not so well liked, which left me thinking about the nature of jazz and the audience. I soon found out that my piece had been taken as an extended bebop piece (as I intended), but after the “head,” what I had written out was heard as improvi-sation. Moreover, the audience took the more progressive and certifiable jazz pieces on the concert to be experimental—as one person said, “too far out.” So it was surprisingly ironic that an essentially conservative audience appreci-ated my piece, when it was for me the unprecedented realization of a new musi-cal concept. I can’t say I was altogether disappointed by the reception, but it did make me think long and hard about my interest in acculturative music. Of course, the serial aspects of the work were not perceived, and thus the work failed to be heard as jazz and serial at once. Moreover, it was exactly those unno-ticed inner workings that would lead me away from directly combining musics from diverse times and places to a more self-contained musical language devel-oping new semantic reach and reference.

The idea of the piece occurred to me when I was looking over the printout of 
a computer run of all-interval series, something Dan Starr and I were studying in 1973 . Row number 44 jumped off the page as an inherently jazzy row, and I 
immediately thought I should do a jazz/serial piece as part of the acculturation project. I put this idea aside until I learned from a technical article by David Lewin on order relations in serial music that the row happened to be famous at Princeton. This row was known as the Mallalieu row, named after its creator (or discoverer), Pohlman Mallalieu. It has some unique and remarkable properties that I realized would serve my compositional purposes. So I got to work, studied the row further and was amazed to find out how perfectly the row would work in a jazz context. As I worked on the piece, I developed some new types of combi-natorialities that eventually were included in the long two-part article that Starr and I wrote in 1974 , and which eventually appeared in Perspectives of New Music .
