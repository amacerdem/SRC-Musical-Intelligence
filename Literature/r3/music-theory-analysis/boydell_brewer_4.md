More Thoughts on Stefan Wolpe’s Music
It’s interesting to know that in the 1950 s John Cage and Stefan Wolpe spent 
time together exchanging ideas at Black Mountain College and in New York City. Milton Babbitt and Wolpe also interacted in New York during the same period. In some ways Wolpe was more traditional than Babbitt or Cage, in others he was surely more eclectic. In any case, Wolpe taught and influenced many of the most important American composers of the next generation—composers of many different stripes: Charles Wuorinen, Ursula Mamlok, Morton Feldman, M. William Karlins, Ralph Shapey, and Harvey Sollberger, to name only a few.
From his many writings on his music, one finds that Wolpe had the sensibility 
of a poet. More than almost any other composer of his time, he was able to find ways to bridge the gap between qualitative and quantitative ways of describing composition and the music he cared about.
My first encounter with Wolpe was via the 1961  broadcast of his Symphony 
played by the New York Philharmonic under the baton of Stefan Bauer-Mengelberg. The originality and power of this and other pieces have continued to inspire me—so much so that I’ve written about Wolpe’s music on three occa-sions, and I will likely do so again.
The present text is a response to a keynote address by Christopher Hasty at 
the 1999  Wolpe Conference at Northwestern University.

Let me begin by repeating something I’ve frequently overheard: that Wolpe’s 
music, especially the late music, in its gestural and kinetic character, suggests that Wolpe is performing his musical thoughts and feelings in addition to merely making music out of them. I find this suggestion apt and insightful, but also elusive, for Wolpe’s musical thought-actions often appear discursive, indicating that we don’t really have a handle on what’s going on. If we care to describe and therefore understand something about how Wolpe’s music goes, we may need to construct a new type of music theory—as if we didn’t have enough—and in doing so, expand the limits of what music analysis can address and discover. This has been the case with the music of Schoenberg, which inspired Babbitt, Forte, and Lewin, among others, to invent and consider new ways to explore twentieth-century music. Similarly, study of the music of Bach, Mozart, Beethoven, and other common-practice composers allowed Schenker to discover principles of organization that raised and answered questions about the nature of tonality and tonal forms. Certainly, important steps have been taken in this direction by the authors of these papers on Wolpe, and I will add my voice to imagine some other theoretic orientations that Wolpe’s music might engender.
When I listen to Wolpe’s music, I am often struck by the way the musical ges-
tures seem to be motivated autogenetically, not by the composer’s invisible hand, but as if the music were a play of agents creating form through a process of mutual engagement and interaction, something Wolpe called “the panorama of activities.” It is as if the distinction between form and content—logic and rheto-ric—has been suspended; as if every action contributes to a web of dynamic pres-ence that has no other in the quiet eye of musical experience. We may feel this in traditional music as well, but with much of Wolpe there seems to be no way to untangle the musical flow into the usual phonological, syntactic, and semantic spheres available to more traditional music analysis. Thus, we may turn to poetic and visual imagery to help us describe our musical perceptions and reactions.
The problem is, of course, not in the music or our experience, but with the 
models of traditional music analysis through which we may hear music. We may not be very aware of these models when we listen, but the musical habits we have overlearned in performance and analysis embody them. Moreover, a model that fits hand to glove will tend to be transparent, in the same way that eyeglasses are transparent to their owners, so if the hand doesn’t fit the glove, it’s the hand’s fault. Traditional models of tonal music are dualistic; binary oppositions such as dissonance and consonance, tension and resolution, movement and stasis, transformation and object, deviation and norm, prolongation and harmony are aligned to privilege the second over the first. Thus logic is taken above rhetoric, and being over becoming. Some modern theories invert these dualisms so music can be described as processive, dissonant, tense, antinomian, etc., or subsume the dualisms in some sort of Hegelian synthesis. This puts a wedge between our musical experience and our ability to compre-hend it. Thus, we need to find more appropriate ways of thinking about Wolpe’s music if we want to repair the rift between representation and experience.
So what kinds of models will illuminate the music? I will propose two, both of 
which are monistic and radical. The first banishes the concept of time as a con-tainer of experience and substitutes the processes of memory in the here and now. “Before” and “after” become echoes and reflections in the time-space of a performance and even beyond it. Experience is a matter of attention to what’s going on. Prediction and reminiscence are but mental impositions, essentially interruptions in an all-encompassing attention beyond notions of self and other, presence and absence, appearance and reality, being and becoming.
This model of musical experience may seem otherworldly and frightening to 
those caught up in the dualities cited above, for, without a certain discipline, the fluxy present of Wolpe’s music will simply jostle and disconcert. Things will seem out of control, and we will desire stasis when the music seems turbulent, or interest when the music sits still. However, the performing musician and atten-tive listener—is there really a difference in kind?—will be able to find a fixed point, that equanimity from which—in which—the music’s flow is simply that, no more, no less. We will be able to hear the ways in which the music forms and decays, producing momentary boundaries and continuities, constructing and deconstructing multidimensional paths of association and resonance. Music discourse will no longer serve to hierarchize and control experience, but will allow us to enrich our experiences by sharing memory with others. Listening to tonal music in this way will free it, also, from the constraints of duality. What were harmony and prolongation will be appreciated as different kinds and rates of processes; dissonances will be heard as parts of processes that are reified and taken out of context. In the end, the distinction between tonality and atonality will only be a matter of style.
My other model can, but need not, go hand in hand with the first. As I said 
before, Wolpe’s music seems to me a play of independent agents engaged in a process of musical transformation. I have deliberately avoided using the term scenario, for what I hear is story, not plot. It is as if Wolpe had planned a party to see what would happen when guests of various inclinations and experience come together and interact. Hence the often wild and unpredictable exchanges and affiliations in his music. From a traditional point of view, the music seems to be rhetorically unbound, expressionistic, and manneristic, without manners or logic. However, careful listening reveals that the tumult is not arbitrary, but an outcome of musical materials whose potentials are realized as the music flows on. under the sway of another dualism. In traditional accounts of common-practice 
music, musical continuity is provided by tonal syntax (logic), while expressive content depends on accent and salience via intensity, duration, and other articu-lative dimensions (rhetoric). Theories of other kinds of music model continuity by patterns of accent, thereby conflating logic with rhetoric. But there’s the rub, for the independence of logic and rhetoric allows greater richness and reach of reference, since continuity and accent can operate independently as well as in concert. Composers and theorists might look far and wide to find or construct a musical system that functions independently of accent and salience. (The twelve-tone system is one example.) But we can ask if this duality of logic and rhetoric is a necessary feature of musical experience.
Making a rough analogy with the duality of signal and noise in physics can 
provide insight. For instance, the underlying laws of Newtonian physics are pos-tulated in an ideal state (signal); their applications to the real world have to be adjusted to account for friction, adhesion, and random behavior (noise). It is “noise” that gives rise to difference and unpredictability. In this view, the natural world appears the way it does because underlying regularities are bur-ied in noise. As a result, particularity, character, and difference are local devia-tions from regularities that underlie them. The beauties of mountain ranges and meandering rivers are the results of conflict between order and chaos. We can now line up some more dualities: accent/structure, rhetoric/logic, noise/signal, and appearance/reality. Is there a way out?
Some new developments in science and mathematics suggest a resolution. 
Various kinds of recursive mathematical functions, long used to simulate ran-domness in the deterministic context of digital computers, produce unpredicta-ble outcomes even though one can specify the function in a few lines of math. By unpredictable, I mean that there is no way to predict what values will appear on the nth iteration of the function, aside from performing the function n times. 
This kind of mathematical chaos is closely related to developments in complexity theory where small changes in the relation of simultaneously evolving processes interact to effect larger changes in the character of the entire system. Such mod-els can be used to describe complex systems such as biological selection, stock markets, and the weather. While these models won’t be of much use in predict-ing these systems’ futures, they answer a deeper question: how can these systems be the way they are? For instance, standard economic theories find Wall Street utterly incomprehensible. Moreover, traditional methods of modeling these systems are incredibly difficult, involving huge systems of nonlinear differential equations that defy solution. Such models are based on the assumption that a system will remain in a state of balance and equilibrium unless it is perturbed by outside forces. Again, we encounter the distinction between signal and noise. In the new models, however, noise and signal are not distinct, but are inextricably united, so there’s no reason to posit an ontological distinction between an ideal and its imperfect incarnation in nature.

Returning to Wolpe’s music, I do not mean to imply we must construct com-
plex mathematical models to understand this music. Rather, I want to say that if we did have such models—and one could try to construct them—their imple-mentation would promote comprehension but not prediction. So the course of a Wolpe piece might be determined more or less by his choice of materi-als, processes, and a starting point, and we might feel that determinism as the piece unfolds. This would mean that at any moment, we will not know where the piece might go next, except that when it makes its moves, those moves, however disorderly, will seem part of the flow, not arbitrary or out of place. Notice how different this kind of experience is from the goal-oriented models of tonality. There we may feel a pull toward certain tonal goals and other norms that may be resisted but eventually will be fulfilled. Wolpe’s music is more like a path in the woods; we don’t know where the path may take us, but we always know when we are on the path. So listening to Wolpe’s music might be like watching the stock market or following the changes in the weather. One day we delight in our good fortunes; on another we say, “I should have known . . .”
I’ve proposed these two models to help elucidate my perception that Wolpe’s 
music does not happen  in time or some concept of musical form, but that it creates  
time and form as it comes and goes. Although I’m sure other models could be proposed (such as those included in other Wolpe essays), these two models are not so far apart despite the idealist ontology of the first versus the realism of the second, and not only because I’ve used them to flesh out my sense of Wolpe’s music. One connection might be suggested: their apparent pessimism, very rem-iniscent of the initial reaction of Western thinkers to Indian philosophy when translations became available in the nineteenth century. (Incidentally, reference to non-Western thought in this context is not gratuitous, for both models share affinities with Buddhist and Taoist worldviews; and Wolpe, via his connections with Johannes Itten and John Cage, would have at least overheard such lines of thought.) This pessimism can probably be traced to a sense of loss—the loss of the authorial composer and of musical teleology. Of course, at the beginning of the millennium we are more comfortable with transience, and have come to terms with the “death of the author” and the essentialism that goes along with it. What is gained is freedom—the open optimism I hear in this music—a freedom not only from authority, predetermination, and Platonic shadows, but a freedom to create and explore worlds, musical and otherwise.
