Not Only Rows in Richard Swift’s 
Roses Only
Although I had encountered the music of John Cage, Milton Babbitt, and Stefan 
Wolpe in my late teens, I didn’t know of Richard Swift until after I finished graduate school, and I didn’t listen to his music until the late 1970 s. In the 
early 1990 s, we became friends, and I got to know a good deal of his music, 
which shared some compositional principles with Babbitt’s and mine. I chose to include my article on Swift in this book not only because I’d like to introduce his music to those who have not yet encountered it, but because it documents a process of getting to know a serial piece.
1 This complements my essay on Babbitt, 
which deals with serial thought from a compositional point of view.
Alas, Richard Swift died in 2003 , leaving behind a wealth of beautiful music 
that deserves to be performed and studied much more that it has been. Dick preferred a scholarly life to one of artistic promotion, even though his musical gifts were clearly superior to those of most scholars or composers. If this essay will interest musicians and listeners in his music, I will be greatly gratified.
*****
In late May of 1993 , I received a package from Richard Swift including a letter, 
the score of his song cycle, Roses Only , and a tape of the premiere performance by 
Susan Narucki, soprano, and D. Kern Holoman conducting the UCD Symphony Orchestra.
2 Swift and I had been corresponding, mainly by mail for about two 
years, occasionally exchanging tapes and scores. As with much of the beautiful music I received from him, his song cycle immediately engaged my interest and admiration. In addition, and for reasons I will register later, I happen to remem-ber vividly my initial impressions of Roses Only  and especially its last song. Over 
many rehearings and thoughts during a period of about three years, I learned a good deal about this piece, which has greatly enhanced my appreciation. For this reason alone I have decided to write up an account of the last song. 3
Let me begin by stating the obvious: appreciation of music is intensified and 
deepened by repeated listening. Of course I mean active listening; for we are doing  something when we attend to music in a completely wholehearted way. 
Such listening is also interactive. The listener is not merely the recipient of an experience designed by a composer and implemented by a performer. Indeed, as Ben Boretz and others have remarked, listening is a form of composition.
But beyond sheer listening, we also experience music when it is not being 
performed or sounded. John Roeder states in one of his articles: “Music is an object for contemplation, not simply immediate experience.”
4 Indeed, the study 
of music out-of-time is vitally important to our understanding of and pleasure in its performance in so-called real time.” We not only remember our in-time experiences of a piece of music as such, but we also remember and consider our expectations, discoveries, and other observations from previous auditions. We get the score and study it, both while listening and between listenings, which sharpens our discrimination and understanding. We sit down at the piano and play passages in our own time. We attempt to imagine and remember the sound of the music and consider many different ways of hearing the relations of its parts to the whole, and the whole and parts in various contexts. So when we lis-ten again, what we have learned through musical study and engagement affects our in-time experience. To the degree we can eventually (re)play or recall the piece “in our heads,” we actually can unite our in- and out-of-time music experi-ences into an interpretation or analysis.
As a result, our investment in a particular music is deeply transformative; as we 
learn more and more, the structure of our understanding of a particular musical experience changes and deepens. Indeed, there is not only a drama in the way a work unfolds in performance, but in the way a work grows on you as you listen and reflect time and time again, as you become imbued with it, as it reaches and triggers modes of consciousness and novel emotions that you never knew existed within you. In fact, we end up regarding a piece of music in the same way that we cherish those people and things we love and respect; we also delight in reflect-ing on the traits and relations of a valued person or thing and sharing what we know with others. So it is with the music we value.

obviously apt as suggested by Swift’s program notes for Roses Only .

Roses Only , for soprano and small orchestra, was composed between August and early 
October 1991 . The orchestra consists of single wind and brass instruments, percussion, 
marimba, and strings. The music was conceived as a memorial for my wife, Dorothy Swift ( 1928 –90), who was, among much else, a poet, a devoted rosarian, and an accom-
plished musician. The full work is a setting of three poems, each of which refers to roses; 
the title is taken from a poem of Marianne Moore. A pair of pitches—G
♯ and B—serves 
as the central focus of much of the melodic and harmonic aspects of the music. It pro-vides a point toward which the aggregate progressions tend. The second song refers to the syncopations and textures of the first movement of Beethoven’s Piano Sonata, op. 31, no. 1, although here tonally and rhythmically much transformed.
Bouquets and wreaths of flowers mark rituals of passage with a sense of inti-
macy. They express both the overt gravity and tender intimacy attending birth, courtship, marriage, worship, and death. Roses add a romantic, corporeal dimen-sion. The fragility of flowers suggests the transience of all aspects of existence, and all the more for flowers that have been cut and arranged for celebration or memorial. But even if I had no knowledge of the memorial purpose of the songs, I am sure that this elegantly lined and deeply felt music and the qualities of Dorothy Swift’s texts would have made a similar impression of reflection and reminiscence.
I never met Dorothy Swift; in fact, she was already ill when I began to corre-
spond with Swift. The texts of the last two songs were my first encounters with her poetry; a year later, I was able to read many other of her poems.
8 In subse-
quent years, Swift wrote to me about Dorothy a number of times. In one letter he says, “The garden, even though I could do only minimal work, has occupied part of my time. April marks the height of rose bloom, also my late wife’s birthday and the anniversary of her death. A bittersweet time but laved with the myriad colors, textures and scents of the roses. . . . This year the ‘heritage’ (read ‘old’) rose group that Dorothy founded in this area had its 10th anniversary, with our 
garden open to all those who attended, as usual.”
9
Rose and garden resonate as metaphors for many of my own aesthetic tenets. 
I often say that I especially admire the kind of composer who creates gardens of music, gardens open to the listener to explore at leisure—a musical architecture that is open and temporally multifarious.
10 The great abundance of different 
strains of roses that have been bred and cross-bred resonates with the deep rich-ness and diversity that I admire in my favorite pieces of music. Such “reach of reference” is available only to those who make pieces with the same care and patience that informs the rose gardener. The formal and informal societies of rosarians correspond to the community of composers who have studied, mas-tered, and extended the vast resource of pitch-class relations that underlies so much of Western twentieth-century art music.

One other important percep-tion was apparent from the start: the entire polyphony of musical lines was not aggregate-forming. So if the piece was twelve-tone, which seemed likely due to its sound and texture—not to mention that all the other pieces of Swift’s that I knew were twelve-tone—it would either be not combinatorially aggregate-form-ing (as in Webern or Berg) or it would use a combination of weighted aggregates and superarrays (as in Babbitt).

The entrance of the voice startled me, not so much because it entered without 
fanfare and with some new pitch configurations, but because the words were in German. I had supposed the text of the first movement (by Rilke) would be translated into English since the two Swift poems were (I supposed) in English. I wished I had looked over the texts beforehand.
Figure 3.1 shows the first vocal phrase. The declamation is typical of the whole 
work. The setting is syllabic with occasional melismas of two or more notes. The overall lyric continuity in the voice contrasts with the more tentative discursive instrumental music. The tessitura is wide, from low A ♭ to high B ♭. The figure 
shows an emphasis on scalar orderings of set-class 3-6[024], what then seemed 
a new element (so far) in the piece. I remember immediately hearing how the lines formed two whole-tone scales at the minor third (<D E F ♯ G♯ A♯ C> and 
<F G A B C ♯>), which helped me detect the lack of a (concluding) D ♯ in the 
phrase. (In later listenings, I heard that the missing pc 3 was being played in the 
flute.)
12
And so my listening progressed: noting details and local features, attending 
to changes of progression or material, enjoying the harmonies, lines and tim-bres, following the overall motion of the whole. Later listening filled in details, pointed up relations (especially of the text to the music), offered new observa-tions, raised new questions of continuity and coherence, and thereby recontex-tualized the global musical experience.

Here is the text of the third song.
   A Variation, after Gunnar Ekelöf
So alien to me this rose, this new unfolding this far-distanced thought-fulfilling or like light across an altered cheekbone . . .Like a day of springtide when you’re given something and then hold it there an eyelid’s flash, an instant, irrevocable: something to never become summer      Dorothy Swift ( 1978 )
This text adapts the poem “Så främmande för mig” by Gunnar Ekelöf, the 
twentieth-century Swedish poet, known for his interest in Eastern culture and lit-erature and for his keen appreciation of music. It is indeed a variation, not sim-ply a translation. While its basic structure and theme of transience is retained, the connotations of the Swedish words are not followed up, and new associations are forged in English.
