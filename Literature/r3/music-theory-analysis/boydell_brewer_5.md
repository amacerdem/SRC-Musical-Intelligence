As I say in my talk on nature and my music later in this part, I encountered haiku 
poetry in the late 1950 s. It had a purity that attracted me because it was not 
about people or ideas but about the act of perception itself. It was wonderful to find out a little later that Mel Powell had set a group of haiku in his Haiku Settings 
(1960 ), sung marvelously by Bethany Beardslee on a Son-Nova LP recording, 
along with his Filigree Setting  for string quartet and works of Milton Babbitt. Over 
the years I returned to haiku in my compositional output. I worked on what became a compositional experiment, producing Haiku Mix  for human speak-
ers and tape ( 1971 , retracted); a more successful outcome was my Haiku Cycle  
(1978 ) for soprano and piano dedicated to Lynn Webber, who was the first to 
sing Babbitt’s Phonemena  in both the instrumental and tape version and recorded 
it on New Word Records in the mid 1970 s.
In the 1980 s I began to study Buddhist religious and philosophic texts seri-
ously; this stimulated an appreciation of classical Chinese poetry and art that has continued until today. I found in the poetry of Wang Wei and others the roots of haiku, which pleased me greatly. This led me to become interested in the relation between poetry and landscape, so well merged in Chinese and Japanese poems. Eventually two compositions for soprano with instruments emerged. In 1985  I 
composed Wang River Cycle  for soprano and seven instruments, an extended set-
ting of Wang Wei’s famous collection of twelve occasional poems written about various locations on his estate on the Wang River and correlated to his painted scroll of the same place. The other piece was Cold Mountain Songs  for soprano 
and piano of 1993 , which I discuss here. Since then I have composed four other 
pieces for the voice on natural themes. Inscape  (1996 ) is based on five poems 
by Gerard Manley Hopkins, written for Thomas Paul, basso, and the Eastman Philharmonia Orchestra. “Sung Song” ( 2002 ) sets four Zen koans from the 
Sung Dynasty and is an unaccompanied song composed especially for soprano Heather Gardner, and Wye: Lynes for Tintern Abbey  for soprano and computer-
generated sounds ( 2003 ) was written for Deborah Norin-Kuehn, who also pre-
miered the Cold Mountain Songs . Wye sets William Wordsworth’s poem “Tintern 
Abbey” or “Lines Composed a Few Miles above Tintern Abbey on Revisiting the Banks of the Wye during a Tour. July 13, 1798 .” A recent project ( 2008 ) is “This

My song cycle Cold Mountain Songs , written in 1993 , represents a way I often 
think about music, especially my own music. I conceive of a musical composition as a world—a world to be discovered, cultivated, lived in, enjoyed. Gone is what I consider to be the outmoded idea of unidirectional musical communication as embodied in the roles of sender (composer), transmitter (performer) and receiver (listener). Rather, composition is the act of creating musical worlds of novel design and coherence; the composer, performer, and listener all explore this world and find their own way within it. Perhaps the operas of Wagner or the symphonies of Mahler are the most literal examples of this conception of music, but there are other approaches, perhaps not as grand, but just as attrac-tive. Think of the minute, but incredibly intense works of Webern or the slowly unrolling musical scrolls of Feldman. Of course, there are critical questions: how rich, multifaceted, deep, and compelling are these worlds? Certainly, such worlds are not be closed or sealed off from other worlds; they are intrinsically open to other worlds of music and other arts and discourse; if this were not the case, we couldn’t recognize a composition as a world among worlds.
Cold Mountain Songs  is such a world. I set poems by Han-shan, a Chinese poet—
also a Taoist and Buddhist hermit—who flourished probably in the ninth cen-tury CE. Who was Han-shan and what was his world? He took his name, which means “cold mountain,” from the place where he lived, a common practice of Zen Buddhist masters.  Anyone who meets Cold Mountain
 Will say he’s touched. A plain face, not attractive to the eye, A cloth robe wrapped about him. What he says, no one knows, Nor does he grasp their words. He responds to those who happen by, “Can you come to Cold Mountain?”

From these words we can get an idea of Han-shan’s world: cold, far, difficult to 
reach, beautiful but dangerous, natural, lonely, and most important, free.

Han-shan and his poems influenced earlier pieces of mine, too. In the mid 
1970 s I first came across Burton Watson’s translations of one hundred of the 
approximately 400 extant poems of Han-shan. Later, in 1984 , in conjunction 
with my computer composition, Night Sky Scroll , I reread the poems and found 
more translations by Gary Snyder, Arthur Waley, and others scattered through-out the literature. Night Sky Scroll was inspired in part by Han-shan, whose dual 
nature as enlightened being and eccentric recluse accords with the character of that composition. Consequently, I developed a taste for Chinese poetry which resulted in my song cycle Wang River Cycle  (1986 ), based on translations of 
poems by another famous poet of the Tang, Wang Wei.
My interest in setting Buddhist poetry was rekindled in 1990 , when I noticed 
that the complete poems of Han-shan had been published in 1983 , translated by 
Red Pine. From my previous studies, I knew how much is transformed in trans-lating Chinese poetry into English. Not only are the rhyme and tone schemes totally lost, but the connotations and allusions that drive a Tang dynasty poem articulated by single (usually monosyllabic) words have to be fleshed out into prosy phrases. Thus, translated poems have a very different feel from their sources. My answer to this problem was to learn enough Chinese to do a transla-tion myself, but not with the aim of making an English poem. Instead, I would be producing a text to be sung, rather than read. So in the winter of 1993 , I learned 
a bit of classical Chinese and read up on Tang dynasty lyric theory. In the end, I was surprised that my translations turned out to be somewhat readable, if hardly as elegant as those done by the experts. In this connection, I am grateful for the help of Su Yin Mak,

A particular problem that I faced was to set these poems for soprano, rather 
than for a male voice. While most of the poems do not reflect gender, I was obliged explicitly to change Han-shan into a woman in the domestic scenes of “Home” and “A Dream.” The gender change helps enliven Han-shan and puts a new spin on the poems “Girls Playing” and “A Critic.” I felt justified in this transformation since the Indian Mahayana Buddhist Avalokitesvara, a male bodhisattva, when imported to China in the first millennium AD ended up as the female Kwan Yin, the bodhisattva of compassion. In addition, when a person has become enlightened, he (or she) takes his (or her) name from the immediate locale, usually a mountain. The form and arrangement of my translation—fourteen poems interspersed 
with five piano interludes—was designed to provide a picture of Han-shan in his or her many guises and disguises. The first lines of the opening poem, printed above, “Anyone who meets Cold Mountain / Will say he’s touched,” presents Han-shan to the uninitiated—alien to the outsider’s concern. After a brief piano interlude, we hear from Han-shan himself. We learn that Han-shan can be both laconic and effusive. In either style the poet is direct, totally at one with the moment.
Another piano interlude leads to three poems, describing the three stages of 
Han-shan’s life. Here is the first poem depicting Han-shan’s youth as an indigent scholar and householder.
 Thatched rafters, a rustic home,
 Horses, carts: few pass the gate. Deep woods, birds roost, Wide streams, spawning fish. Father and son pick mountain fruit, Mother tills the field, And what is inside the house? Just one bed with books.
The second poem of the three follows: Han-shan’s dream of return after separa-
tion from his or her family.
 Last night, I dreamt I went home,
 I saw my spouse at the loom, Stopping the shuttle in thought Then raising it as if without strength. I called, he turned his face. He did not know me, So many years had passed, The hair on my temples without color.
The third poem, quoted above, is about Han-shan’s later years after enlighten-
ment, “a white-haired man, mumbling sacred texts.”
The next poem in the cycle is the longest, set off by interludes. Its first verse 
describes the enlightened person as an empty, open house—a well-known Buddhist metaphor for the self. The second verse is addressed to the unenlight-ened outsider concerned with getting ahead in mundane affairs.
The next five poems form a group. Each presents a different manifestation of 
Han-shan’s personality/environment modulated by the concerns and intensions of those who seek her/him/it: “dark and eerie,” “no one there,” “picking flow-ers,” “struggling,” “hostile,” and so on. The last of the five poems (“A Critic”)

Having described the narrative trajectory of the work, I now turn to its musical 
implementation. See figure 7.1 for the plan of the entire work. This plan took 
some time to develop. I had to choose the texts, translate them, arrange them in the right order, decide on the musical materials and create the compositional design (the sequence and type of arrays described later). This took four months of work, including the writing of computer programs.
The plan lists the nineteen sections of the piece, fourteen songs and five piano 
interludes. The interludes were placed in the whole to give the singer an occa-sional rest and group the poems according to the narrative. These interludes might even more directly express what Han-shan is pointing to in his poetry, especially in the interlude following “Cold Mountain is a House.”
As the figure shows, each song is based on a twelve-tone combinatorial array. 
But before we can go further, we must examine the row of the piece. See  figures 
7.2a through f.  The row has special properties that support the sound and struc-
ture of the music and the text. It provides the infrastructure that enables the world of the piece to exist.
Figure 7.2a presents the row-table listing all the rows in the row-class of the 
Cold Mountain row. I list it twice, first with pc numbers, then with pc note-names. Figure 7.2b presents the successive intervals in the row. We see that the 
row has many instances of interval 1 and B, members of ic 1. Most of the other 
intervals are small or inversions of small intervals. There are no tritones (interval 6) and only one instance each of ic 4 and 5. These intervals make the row easy to

Now we return to the plan in figure 7.1. The bottom row of the chart named 
“registers” shows the registral plan for the 19 sections, that is, which ranges of 
the piano and voice are used in which sections. All combinations of two or more registers are used, with the first and last song using all registers. Sometimes the registers change in a section, like section IX (a piano interlude), which starts low and ascends.
The plan (in figure 7.1) also summarizes the kinds of arrays used in the songs 
and interludes. Arrays are two-dimensional musical structures; each voice (or line) of the array contains one related row or more. Each column holds 12 pcs, but in this piece these pcs don’t have to be distinct. That is, while some of the arrays are “normal,”—their columns include all twelve tones without duplication—others are weighted, with some of the 12 pcs duplicated and others omitted.
Figure 7.3 lists and describes the arrays in the song cycle. Cold Mountain 
arrays have three modalities. They can be of three or four voices, all-partition or even, and normal or weighted. “All-partition” arrays have columns with the pcs distributed in all the ways 12 pcs can be arranged into n parts; in these arrays,  
n equals 3 or 4. “Even” arrays have the same partition of pcs in each column 
and the number of pcs in each part is the same. So the rows are parceled out in groups of 3 or 4 pcs. I described the distinction between normal and weighted 
arrays above, but an additional requirement is that the set-class represented in each array column has to be the same; this set-class is called the norm  of the 
array. (The norm of a normal array is the twelve-tone aggregate or set-class 12-1.) 
Figure 7.3 provides further information.

The musical effect of the progression from complete aggregates to norms of 
eight distinct pcs is the increasing profusion of doubling between the soprano and piano and within the piano parts as we reach the center of the composition. Song VII represents the most extreme case where octaves predominate in the piano part. This is the song in which Han-shan realizes that her/his spouse and the world of society and mundane affairs have forgotten her/him.
In Cold Mountain Songs , I’ve tried to create a musical whole that reflects on 
the world of Han-shan, following the course of his or her life as I imagine it. By utilizing all the ways to interpret arrays of aggregates of all even and all-partition types in all weightings from 12 to 8 pcs, for 3 and 4 voices, and in all registers, I 
have produced something like a mandala—a symmetric plan, chart, or geomet-ric pattern used in Hindu and Buddhist ritual and meditation that represents “reality” as a microcosm of the world from a human perspective. In any case, in Cold Mountain Songs , nothing is repeated exactly, but everything makes sense.
