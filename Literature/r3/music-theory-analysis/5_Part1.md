Chapter Five
Composing Each Time
I delivered this talk to the composition students and faculty at Eastman in the 
winter of 2006 . It is the most recent of the talks in this section of this book, but 
I’ve put it first since it gives a somewhat rounded description of my composition practice. It also provides a more leisurely introduction to many concepts and aesthetic principles that inform the rest of the talks and my work as a whole.
At the outset, I submit that I rarely describe my work to others to this degree 
of specificity; in fact, I tend not to reveal such things except on special occa-sions when I feel it is useful or appropriate to go into detail. Even when I teach composition, in either class or private tutorial, I rarely speak of my own music. I believe a composition teacher’s first obligation is to help students find their own way into composition.
1 The beginning stages of composition instruction 
certainly involve studying the literature of music—contemporary music, as well as the works of composers of past centuries. The basic elements of composi-tional craft have also to be undertaken, but without bias for one compositional direction over another. Compositional survey courses are good for imparting this kind of knowledge, with workshops in which students try out various com-positional techniques. In one-on-one composition lessons, a student’s current and usually unfinished work is shared with the teacher who must be careful to offer only positive, constructive commentary, without underrating or undermin-ing the student for lack of imagination or knowledge. Ultimately, the teacher’s main function is to inspire the student to compose and sometimes to ensure the student is composing as opposed to—or in addition to—thinking about compos-ing or worrying about it. From this point of view, there is little reason for the teacher to introduce her own compositions as models. First of all, such models may enforce the composer’s own style at the expense of the student’s musical needs and desires, and this often breeds resentment. Besides, a seasoned com-poser’s working methods will probably not be useful to the student, since such compositional practice is often hard won and while relatively effortless for the teacher, almost impossible for the student to manage, not to mention master.
For these reasons, I do not introduce my own music in composition lessons 
unless the student asks me about a particular piece, or if, in a rare case, the student is composing in a way that is very close to mine—which happens very infrequently.

Nevertheless, when I am asked to introduce my music at symposiums and 
forums, usually when I am a guest composer, I will hold forth on my music. I feel, since I am talking to fellow composers about what I do, I can indulge in shoptalk and explain my compositional methods in reference to particular pieces.
2 Nevertheless, problems can develop, some of which I mention in this 
talk. Here, I’ll elaborate on a common misconception that often arises in the minds of younger composers, or those who have little need for theory when they compose. The issue has to do with the relation between compositional tech-nique and the semantics of one’s music, between means and end.
I’ve found there is a latent dualism that gets in the way of showing that method 
and result interpenetrate each other in creative work. From one side, the means is considered the end, so that one is accused of writing music that is no more than its structure, or can be reduced to structural descriptions—the limit case of the art-for-art’s-sake position. But the merger of means and end can also be mis-understood from the other side, to imply that music is no more than its function or use; whatever is necessary to ensure that the music does its job is the bottom line of compositional action. In any case, the problem stems from a misunder-standing of how method functions in composition, and, I would generalize, to the arts and sciences as well.
Perhaps the greatest confusions about method occur in talk about twentieth-
century music. We can get some clarity if we take for granted that composi-tion, along with other kinds of musicality, is a form of knowledge, or at least is supported by knowledge. But what we call knowledge in music theory and in the practice of musicians is not the same thing. The Greeks called these two aspects of knowledge theoria  and praxis . In more recent philosophy, the distinc-
tion has often been couched as “knowing that” and “knowing how.” Knowing what makes it possible to ride a bicycle is theoria; knowing how to ride one is praxis, Obviously there is a gap between theoria and praxis. Knowing your theory doesn’t turn you automatically into a composer or performer. Being able to write or play music doesn’t mean you have an idea about how music is structured.
Fortunately, Aristotle had a better way to classify knowledge. He postulated 
four types: doxa, episteme , techne , and phronesis . Doxa  is belief, opinion, and point 
of view. There is no guarantee that this kind of knowledge is sound or correct; however, for many musicians, it may appear that doxa is all we can know when it comes to making music. Episteme  is knowledge of which we can be absolutely 
sure; logic and the facts of a case fall into this category. Music theory aspires to this ideal. Techne  is how-to knowledge. The word “technique” is derived from 
techne. It is often based on rules that can be automatically or habitually applied. The instructions that come with the parts from which one is to assemble a model airplane or a piece of furniture are one sort of techne. Such plans need support from another type of techne. This techne involves the various skills needed to carry out plans and instructions: how to use tools such as a screwdriver, hammer,

plane and level, etc., and how to glue pieces of wood together (knowing the

Phronesis  is the last form of knowledge. It is knowing how to get things done 
in a particular context and to particular ends. For Aristotle, phronesis was asso-ciated with politics, so that diplomacy is perhaps its paradigm case. Phronesis is rule-based to some extent, but the rules are fuzzy and change in pertinence and application in different cases.
3 Phronesis is learned by practicing. It involves 
being aware of all aspects of a situation and considering the results of different alternatives. Phronesis is never the result of automatically applying rules; one has to consider which rules apply and to what degree, and how to use them in a given case. Therefore, phronesis and creativity go hand in hand.
In the case of furniture, we see that one chair is adequately constructed and 
functions as a chair, but that another is beautifully built, with care and craft. Building both chairs requires techne, but the beautiful chair also requires phro-nesis. This means that phronesis supervenes over the other forms of knowledge. Excellence in constructing and proving a theory, doing science (episteme), expounding a doctrine (doxa), or carrying out rules (techne) depends on the application of phronesis to problems in each kind of knowledge. (In short, those who have techne are called experts and are deemed clever, but those who have phronesis are called masters and are deemed wise.)
It should be clear that musical activities such as composition, performance, 
analysis—and even listening, itself—are forms of phronesis. In what is often called art, techne, episteme, and doxa alone will not do.
I mentioned confusion about method in twentieth-century music. The char-
acter of musical revolution in that century was often characterized as matter of technique, and changes of technique were considered to be necessary if one were to write significant pieces of music. Therefore, certain types of music were characterized by their technique rather than their style. Examples of such types are minimalism, spectralism, complexity, serialism, and chance. This empha-sis on techne was aided and abetted by a requirement that the composer also construct a theory or context for composition; that significant composition also involves episteme. Today, techne and episteme are no longer seen as necessary or sufficient in the creation of meaningful music, if they ever were. However, this does not mean that to include techne and episteme as a part of the creative process is to be avoided at all costs. Rather, the main problem of the discussion of method in new music was that it ignored phronesis. Phronesis captures the way in which a composer coordinates music theory, compositional techniques, and issues in a musical and cultural environment, in the service of expressing a musical point of view that counts as art. That is why there can be irreconcilable differences between composers, and yet one cannot doubt the authenticity of their musics.

So what is presented in this talk and all the others on my music should be 
understood as an attempt to convey the phronesis in my compositional prac-tice—even when I seem to lapse into technicalities and abstraction.

Introduction
Each time I finish a piece, there is little time to rest or reflect; something else 
demands my attention, and I attend to it. Of course, finishing a piece is not really abrupt; after the first draft, there is revision, computer engraving, and editing. During these tasks, new ideas and conceptions begin to enter conscious-ness, so the experience of writing a particular piece fades even before all the work is done. Later, when I return to a work—to coach a performance or lecture on it—I try to reconstruct the process of composition and return to the way it felt to write it, but many times much of the passion and clarity that brought the work into being is lost. With some thought of addressing this problem, I decided immediately after completing my piano piece Each Time  to write about it while 
the experience of its composition was still fresh in my memory. In this way, I hope to express as vividly as possible what I try to put into a piece and how and why—something that might be useful to future performers or listeners.
In some respects,  Each Time  is not very different from my other piano pieces; 
however, I wanted to express in music some new insights I had into the phenom-enology of time derived from Buddhist philosophy. Rather than spelling this out in this talk, I’ll just say that I composed the piece so it might suggest to an attentive listener that impermanence is at the root of experience and that time is both flowing and discontinuous at once.
4
During composition, much is clear but not said. Musical ideas come and go, 
and on a good day, one knows just what to do with and about them. From study-ing the scores of past composers, it is evident that they possessed great composi-tional knowledge and skill. Whether they could have spoken about these facets of their music is doubtful, for there was little or no technical language avail-able—not to mention a conceptual base—to help frame their compositional ideas in words or other nonmusical symbols. It has even been suggested that past composers knew how to compose, but not what they were doing. Nevertheless, 
the wealth of incredibly skillful and elaborate musical associations and transfor-mations in earlier music contradicts that assumption.
By contrast, today’s composers have access to sophisticated technical language, 
concepts, and techniques. last forty years, American music theory has transformed and added to these com-positional ideas, refined and generalized them, and put them to use in musical analysis. Thanks to these developments, the composer of the twenty-first-century can choose  whether or not she wants to be articulate about her compositional 
practice and that of others. But even with such knowledge, there is reticence about “talking shop” in public. Some of this undoubtedly derives from the unfor-tunate split in our intellectual discourse between science and the humanities, called the “two cultures” by C. P. Snow. Be that as it may, words about music tend to be distrusted by the general public, who like their composers to be “intuitive” and nonverbal. Sometimes one even hears it said that composers who can talk about their music are not as talented as those who cannot (or will not).
Being able to speak about one’s work may not make you a better composer, 
but, contrary to superstition, it doesn’t hurt. Cognitive style is an important fac-tor, for negotiating the gap between the qualitative and the quantitative isn’t altogether natural for many people. Even so, the act of writing using musical notation involves and encourages such translations in both directions. We write down a musical symbol that stands for a musical object like a note or a process like a crescendo. Both the object and process are meant to be experienced as, or contribute to, a musical event, which, despite its notation, is transient—often immediately transient—and retained only in memory. From the other side, when we hear a musical event, we possess a way to grasp and retain it, exactly because we can notate it (even if we do not do so on a particular occasion). So in the following discussion, I will freely make use of musical notation to make points about the features of Each Time  and its musical structure. At the same time, I’ll 
try to keep away from technical terminology unless it is absolutely necessary. I will assume the reader has only scant knowledge of serial composition; I hope those who do have such knowledge will appreciate my attempts to advance serial music in innovative and unprecedented ways.
When presenting my work to, say, a group of composers in a workshop or 
forum, depending on the time available, I follow a more or less standard proce-dure. After a few introductory comments, I play a recording of the work; describe its basic compositional features, form, and structure; illustrate some of these with musical or written examples; play the work again; and ask for questions. I believe that this top-down method helps introduce listeners to a challenging or unfamiliar composition; but from the questions, I often notice people want to know how to relate what they hear in the music to what I have said about it. They want to know if it is all right to have had reactions or interpretations other than the ones I might have explicitly or implicitly presented. Sometimes they ask if or even assert that what I have said might not be relevant or understandable to the listener or performer who doesn’t compose, and especially to the audience at large. Or the question is framed in another way: who is the ideal listener? I have no definitive answers to such questions, since I don’t believe there are any.

I can only say that music takes time to learn and that musical experience is not 
confined to the period of time when one is listening to a performance—that musical study is part of that experience, that what one can hear is based on what one already knows, and so forth. From another standpoint, these questioners ask: what would I say if I were asked to teach them how to listen to my music? I take this question not as an invitation to legislate or demand that my music be heard in only certified ways and contexts, but to ask about what I have put into the work that I believe can be heard by others. It is frustrating to be asked such a good question at the end of a talk on my music since there is no time to answer it. I might bring up a few caveats, such as: when I say X can be heard, I need to also specify what it is heard as, and in what contexts. Or I might say that a very close look at the particularity of some passage from a piece might help one get an idea of what is there to hear and appreciate. In any case, this talk is an attempt to answer that question.
First Hearing
To begin, I need to think about what a serious listener might pick up from a first encounter with Each Time . Not surprisingly, composers want to know what the 
presumably “unbiased” and “objective” listener hears in their music, if not out of narcissism or to assuage doubts about their music’s worth, then to know if they have been “successful” on their own terms. I don’t believe one can ever know this, since a composer has a priori knowledge of her work precisely because she composed it and can only conjecture about what a listener might bring to it. However, as a listener to other composers’ works—at concerts and especially in master classes and compositional tutorials—I am often called upon to immedi-ately voice my reactions to a piece I have never heard before. And in the roles of writer and teacher about other composers’ music, I may try to provide ways to help the listener get from the “facts” of the score to an appreciation of the work. On one occasion, I retraced the path I myself took from listening to a work for the first time to getting to that work’s underlying structure. (I am referring to my essay in part 1, “Not Only Rows in Richard Swift’s Roses Only .”) I wrote that text 
not only to express my appreciation of the relation between the compositional surface and the way that I heard it, but also to show that one can piece together the structure from the particulars and how that process deepens and changes what one hears in the work. I will attempt something like this in this paper, but with the disclaimer that I am only imagining what I might hear if I didn’t know this piece.

Compositional Units
From my comments on these passages, it can be inferred that I use the measure to delineate local aspects of structure. In point of fact, for more than twenty years, I have notated my music so that the measure indicates to the player and listener the most foreground compositional unit  of a piece. Each compositional 
unit is a self-contained whole, but is also part of the flow. The pacing of these units by using different tempi and time signatures is an important part of my compositional practice. In my concerted music, the conductor’s gestures there-fore indicate to the players and audience much more than where the beat is and how it is to be divided, as they do in older music. In almost all of my pieces, the unit is also divided into layers or registers that remain invariant throughout a section or even throughout an entire piece. In Each Time , the entire range of 
the piano is divided into seven nonoverlapping, adjacent registers, as shown in figure 5.2. The registers are numbered from 1 to 7, from top to bottom. The 
outer and middle registers employ 12 pitches, while registers 2, 3, 5, and 6 have

Having discussed unit macrorhythm, we turn to the microrhythm within a 
compositional unit. Some serial composers have used duration or time-point series or arrays to determine the local rhythm, that is, the durations between adjacent time-points of notes or other musical events. For a number of reasons, I have resisted this approach in favor of looser, more malleable techniques. In Each Time , the internal rhythms are subject to what I call rhythmic rates . In the 
piece I specified four rhythmic rates: progression by 3s (triplets, sextuplets, 
etc.), 4s (eighth-, sixteenth-, thirty-second-notes, etc.), 5s (quintuplets, etc.) and 
7s (septuplets, etc.) Each unit is assigned one to three of these rates out of which 
the rhythmic durations and sequences are constructed. In figures 5.1a and 5.1b,

One of the ways units can resemble each other is by the distribution of their 
pitch-classes, that is, by the number of pitch-classes in each of the seven regis-ters. There are two ways we compare the distributions of units. The first involves a simple mathematical concept: the composition  of a number. Given a number 
n, we can express n as sum of other numbers, paying attention to the order in 
which they are given. If we set n to 12, then the following number sequences 
are distinct “compositions of 12”: 66, 152121 , 543, 453, 211125 , 57, etc. These 
examples confirm that it is the order of the numbers that sum to 12 that dis-
tinguishes one composition from another. We can use compositions of 12 to 
describe the distributions of pitch-classes in a unit. We simply write down the number of pitch-classes in each register (from 1 to 7). In figures 5.5a and 5.5b, 
four units are given. The unit in figure 5.5a has 1 pitch-class in register 1, 0
