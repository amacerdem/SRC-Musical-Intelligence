Abstract
Revealed  more than two millennia  ago by Pythagoras,  consonance  and dissonance  (C/D)  are foundational  concepts  in music  
theory,  perception,  and aesthetics.  The search  for the biological,  acoustical,  and cultural  factors  that affect C/D perception  has 
resulted  in descriptive  accounts  inspired  by arithmetic,  musicological,  psychoacoustical  or neurobiological  frameworks  without  
reaching  a consensus.  Here, we review  the key historical  sources  and modern  multidisciplinary  ﬁndings  on C/D and integrate  
them into three main hypotheses:  the vocal similarity  hypothesis  (VSH),  the psychocultural  hypothesis  (PH), and the sensorimotor  
hypothesis  (SH). By illustrating  the hypotheses-related  ﬁndings,  we highlight  their major  conceptual,  methodological,  and termi-
nological  shortcomings.  Trying  to provide  a unitary  framework  for C/D understanding,  we put together  multidisciplinary  research  
on human  and animal  vocalizations,  which  converges  to suggest  that auditory  roughness  is associated  with distress/danger  and, 
therefore,  elicits  defensive  behavioral  reactions  and neural  responses  that indicate  aversion.  We therefore  stress the primacy  of 
vocality  and roughness  as key factors  in the explanation  of C/D phenomenon,  and we explore  the (neuro)biological  underpinnings  
of the attraction-aversion  mechanisms  that are triggered  by C/D stimuli.  Based  on the reviewed  evidence,  while the aversive  nature  
of dissonance  appears  as solidly  rooted  in the multidisciplinary  ﬁndings,  the attractive  nature  of consonance  remains  a somewhat  
speculative  claim that needs further  investigation.  Finally,  we outline  future  directions  for empirical  research  in C/D, especially  
regarding  cross-modal  and cross-cultural  approaches.

1. Introduction
The laws governing  why the creative  combination  of sounds  that we call music  moves  us and is able to induce  pow-
erful feelings  in listeners  have been investigated  since the beginning  of human  civilization.  One of these fundamental  
laws is consonance  (C), and its opposite  is dissonance  (D). C/D have been often deﬁned  in terms  of the different  
effects  generated  on the listener  by two tones  played  either  simultaneously  or one after the other,  with consonances  
typically  being  perceived  as smooth,  harmonious,  and positively  valenced  and dissonances  typically  being  perceived  
as rough,  unstable,  and negatively  valenced  [92,135,139,168,191]. However,  the extent  to which  such deﬁnition,  and 
especially  the dual link between  pleasantness  and consonance,  on the one side, and dissonance  and unpleasantness,  
on the other,  can be considered  universal  has been questioned  by recent  ﬁndings  (e.g., [124]).
The combination  of C/D is one of the building  blocks  of Western  music  composition,  contributing  both to the har-
monic  (i.e., simultaneous)  and melodic  (i.e., successive)  combination  of tones  [173]. However,  cross-cultural  studies  
have shown  that most traditions  in the world  make  speciﬁc  use of many  of the same intervals  (e.g., [23,36,120,198]) 
and that the intervals  that are most frequently  used across  cultures  correspond  to those  considered  more consonant  
[26,38,205]. Remarkably,  archaeological  evidence  of bone ﬂutes  that are capable  of sounding  consonances  suggests  
that those  sounds  were used much  earlier  than Pythagoras  (∼8000 BP from Zhang  et al. [258]; ∼35000  BP from 
Conard  et al. [51]; though  see Diedrich  [63]). These  ﬁndings  led researchers  to claim  that if, as suggested  by Carterette  
and Kendall  [41], the cultural  elaboration  of music  relies  on a few allegedly  universal  traits,  one of these traits might  
be the preference  for consonance  ([77], see also [68]).
The scientiﬁc  investigation  of C/D started  in the nineteenth  century,  particularly  with the works  of the German  
scientists  Hermann  von Helmholtz  [95] and Carl Stumpf  [219,220], who fostered  the rise of psychoacoustics  and of 
the empirical  investigation  of music  perception.  More  recently,  the seminal  work of Ernst  Terhardt  [228]p a v e d  the way 
for the modern  understanding  of C/D. In particular,  Terhardt  crucially  distinguished  between  two main components  
of the C/D phenomenon,  namely,  sensory  consonance  and harmony,  thus isolating  the music-related  component  from 
nonmusic-speciﬁc  aspects  of C/D that can also pertain  to environmental  or natural  stimuli.  Importantly,  Terhardt  
started  to conceive  C/D perception  as resulting  from distinct  processing  levels,  i.e., a lower  one mainly  responsible  for 
sensory  consonance  and a higher,  cognitive,  and culturally  affected  processing  that is related  to harmony  perception.
In recent  years,  interest  in C/D perception  has grown  exponentially,  making  it one of the most investigated  and 
debated  issues  in both specialized  and general,  often inﬂuential,  scientiﬁc  journals  including  Nature or PNAS (e.g., 
[26,28,39,66,82,83,92,130,167,176,181,224,248]). Findings  have shed light on the role of both low-level  sensory  
processes  and cultural  exposure  in C/D perception.  A growing  body of converging  evidence  suggests  that C/D dis-
crimination  has a biological  basis (e.g., [54,56,170,234,243]). However,  inconsistent  behavioral  ﬁndings  on infants  
(e.g., [174,200]), as well as controversial  cross-cultural  evidence  on adults  (e.g., [77,144]), have prevented  the estab-
lishment  of a consensus  view (e.g., [26,28,29,82]) and have raised  issues  about  the role of biological  constraints  in 
shaping  the aesthetic  perception  of C/D.
Thus,  C/D emerges  as an intensely  debated,  complex  and multifaceted  phenomenon  resulting  from a puzzling  com-
bination  of intertwined  elements,  such as arithmetic  ratios,  biological  constraints,  and cultural  systems.  To assess  the 
role of these factors  in C/D perception,  in this paper,  we aim to critically  review  the various  descriptive  accounts  of 
C/D perception—i.e.,  arithmetic,  psychoacoustic,  neurobiological,  and cultural  —that  have been put forward  through-
out the bimillennial  history  of the problem.  Then,  we systematically  integrate  the diverse  explanations  into the major  
existing  hypotheses  about  C/D origin,  and we highlight  the key role of vocality,  roughness,  and attraction-aversion  
mechanisms  in the explanation  of C/D phenomenon.

2. The arithmetical  origins  of consonance:  scientiﬁc  discovery  or metaphysical  speculation?
The early theorization  of C/D in music  dates back to the 6thcentury  B.C.E.,  when  Pythagoras  proved  that plucking  
two strings  with a length  ratio that can be expressed  by small  integers  results  in a harmonious  sound.  Pythagoras  
recognized  that the ﬁrst four integers  (i.e., the tetraktys ) can account  for perfect  consonances—i.e.,  octave  (2:1),  ﬁfth 
(3:2),  and fourth  (4:3)—while  more complex  ratios,  i.e., 5:4, 6:5, 7:6, give rise to increasingly  unpleasant  auditory  
sensations.  For Pythagoreans,  such empirical  evidence  proved  a metaphysical  assumption,  i.e., that numbers  are the 
principles  of reality  and that abstract  numerical  relationships  shape  human  perceptions  of the world  [188].1
The possibility  of grounding  music  theory  in numbers  has long fascinated  theorists  and composers  throughout  the 
centuries,  and several  Pythagorean  approaches  were later proposed  in Western  culture.  During  the Renaissance,  for 
example,  Zarlino  [254] expanded  the tetraktys , including  the numbers  5 and 6, thus admitting  the major  third (5:4),  
minor  third (6:5) and major  sixth (5:3) among  consonances.  The magic  of numbers  also inspired  ﬁgures  that strongly  
contributed  to the rise of modern  science,  such as Kepler  [111], who considered  debita  proportio and consonant  
ratios  as the basis of both cosmos  and music,  and Euler,  who formulated  a rigorous  algorithm  to derive  the degree  of 
consonance,  i.e., gradus  suavitatis , of a given  interval  ([69]; see also [171]).
The Pythagorean  foundation  of music  has also inspired  more general  aesthetic  theorizations  based  on simplicity,  
harmony,  and proportions  ([226]). One of the most famous  and literal  interpretations  of Pythagorean  principles  was 
by the great architect  and theorist  Leon Battista  Alberti  (1404–1472).  In his treatise  De Re Aediﬁcatoria , Alberti  
acknowledged  the role of Pythagoras  in shaping  his architectural  view,  explicitly  declaring  that architecture  should  
get its inspiration  from numbers  and proportions  that are best represented  in music:  «I am every  day more and more 
convinced  of the truth of Pythagoras’s  saying,  that nature  is sure to act consistently,  and with a constant  analogy  in all 
her operations:  From  whence  I conclude  that the same numbers,  by means  of which  the agreement  of sounds  affects  
our ears with delight,  are the very same which  please  our eyes and mind.  We shall therefore  borrow  all our rules for 
the ﬁnishing  our proportions,  from the musicians,  who are the greatest  masters  of this sort of numbers,  and from those  
particular  things  wherein  nature  shows  herself  most excellent  and compleat2» ([1, pp. 196-197]).
In parallel  to the enthusiastic  readings  of Pythagorean  numerology,  a long history  of objections  started  with the 
Greek  philosopher  Aristoxenus  of Tarentum  (375-322  B.C.E.).  In contrast  with his “predecessors”,  i.e., the Pythagore-
ans, Aristoxenus  banned  the arithmetical  foundation  of music  theory:  “For some  of these [i.e., theorists]  introduced  
extraneous  reasoning,  and rejecting  the senses  as inaccurate  fabricated  rational  principles,  asserting  that height  and 
depth  of pitch consist  in certain  numerical  ratios  and relative  rates of vibration  – a theory  utterly  extraneous  to the sub-
ject and quite at variance  with the phenomena”  ([7, pp. 188-189]).  In contrast,  Aristoxenus  regarded  sense  perception  
as “one of the primary  truths  of harmonic  science”  ([7, p. 198]).  Accordingly,  in his music  treatise,  Armonica (1902),  
neither  a numerical  proportion  nor a reference  to the length  of vibrating  strings  is used in the explanation  of scales  or 
intervals.
In the 18th century,  harsh  criticism  was raised  against  Jean-Philippe  Rameau’s  Traité  de l’harmonie  réduite  à 
ses principes  naturels (1722),  in which  the author  used Pythagorean  principles  to demonstrate  that the key element  of 
Western  harmony,  i.e., the major  triad, is generated  “naturally”  from the integer  ratio and from the laws of the physical  
resonance  of strings.  To explain  musical  intervals,  Rameau  suggested  considering  seven  strings  tuned  in unison  and 
dividing  the nstring  into nparts,  thus obtaining  seven  tones.  For example,  tuning  the ﬁrst string  at C3, the others  will

is related  to synchronous  vibrations,  claiming  that any further  reference  to the “natural  foundation”  of harmony  is 
illegitimate  and that the only possible  way to ground  music  relies  on subjective  judgment,  culture,  and individual  
experience.
The Illuminists’  arguments  resonate  with modern  critics  of Pythagorean  theory.  In a seminal  article,  the American  
composer  and musicologist  Norman  Cazden  [45] argued  that Pythagorean  foundations  of music  are highly  speculative  
and unsupported.  He refused  any attempts  to ground  the distinction  between  consonance  and dissonance  on the sim-
plicity  of relationships,  the prevalence  of harmonics  or the absence  of beats,  concluding  that C/D are cultural  criteria  
([45, p. 8]). «In a philosophical  sense»,  Cazden  wrote,  «it is hard to decide  what is meant  by “simplicity”  in this case. 
Abstractly  speaking,  the ratio π:1 or √2:1 is quite as simple  and elemental  as, for example,  the ratio 2:1. Speculations  
of this nature  have led to a kind of number  magic,  the mystery  of the “harmony  of the spheres”»  ([45, p. 3]). Focusing  
on the difference  between  just intonation  and equal  temperament,  Cazden  also noted  that perceiving  consonances  in 
equal  temperament  contradicts  the arithmetic  theory  of consonance.  In fact, in the equal  temperament,  only unison  
and the octave  are the true Pythagorean  intervals,  being  expressed,  respectively,  by 1:1 and 2:1 ratios,  while  all the 
remaining  intervals  are adjusted,  i.e., stretched  or narrowed.  For example,  a perfect  ﬁfth is not obtained  by multiply-
ing the root frequency  for 1,5, but for 1,498.  Despite  being  represented  by such a complex  ratio, the adjusted  ﬁfth is 
still perceived  as consonant.  Thus,  Cazden  concluded  that C/D are functions  of Western  musical  language  rather  than 
properties  of sounds  (see, also, [46,47]).
In a more recent  and much  more empirically  informed  article,  provokingly  subtitled  Bye bye Pythagoras! , Parncutt  
and Hair [166]t r y to demonstrate  that the Pythagorean  approach  to music  theory  is inconsistent  with the modern  
evidence-based  understanding  of musical  structure,  perception  and cognition.  They hold that musical  intervals  and 
scales,  although  they depend  in part on acoustic  factors,  are primarily  psychocultural  entities,  not merely  mathematical  
or physical.  Parncutt  and Hair solidly  ground  their claims  on a number  of relevant  facts:  i) as observed  by Cazden,  
most tuning  systems  use “adjusted”  (either  compressed  or larger),  not Pythagorean,  interval  sizes;  ii) the simplest  and 
most fundamental  interval  ratio, namely,  the octave  (2:1),  is almost  never  played  as such, but rather  is slightly  wider  
than 2:1 (e.g., [38,222]); iii) slightly  “untuned”  octaves  are reported  to be preferred  by listeners  [64,246]; and iv) just 
intonation  would  inevitably  lead to internal  inconsistency,  meaning  that a sixth scale degree  that is 5:3 above  the ﬁrst 
degree  cannot  be at the same time a perfect  ﬁfth (3:2) apart from the second  (9:8),  and this is among  the reasons  why 
Pythagorean  tuning  needs  to be adjusted  (see [40], for a review  on scales  and tuning  systems).  Notably,  regarding  the 
factors  that might  have contributed  to shaping  intonation  in real music  contexts,  Parncutt  and Hair refuse  to assign  
any primary  role to the biology  or mathematics  of sound  perception,  suggesting  that any neural  synchronies,  phase  
locking,  and resultant  ratios  do not necessarily  correlate  with conscious  experience.

Further  insights  into the Pythagorean  foundation  of consonance  derive  from neurophysiological  ﬁndings  showing  
that neural  processing  of equal  temperament  intervals  is affected  by the degree  of consonance,  even with consonances  
having  extremely  complex  ratios  in equal  temperament.  Measuring  neurophysiological  activity  in the primary  auditory  
cortex  of epileptic  patients  undergoing  surgery,  Fishman  and colleagues  [72] found  that neural  responses  to most 
dissonant  chords  (minor  second,  major  second,  and major  seventh)  display  prominent  oscillations  phase-locked  to 
the predicted  difference  frequencies.  In contrast,  responses  evoked  by perfect  consonances  (octave  and perfect  ﬁfth) 
are characterized  by an absence  of rapid oscillations  and by comparatively  ﬂat, low amplitude  spectra.  Importantly,  
patterns  of oscillatory  activity  evoked  by equal  temperament  intervals  were similar  to those  evoked  by Pythagorean  
intervals.
To summarize,  the Pythagorean  foundation  of C/D has inaugurated  a bimillennial  debate  on music  theory  and 
aesthetics,  engaging  music  theorists,  philosophers,  and scientists.  Such an approach  dominated  C/D’s  theorizations  
for centuries,  strongly  fascinating  those  scholars  who were attracted  by the idea that the essential  laws of the universe  
are simple  and, therefore,  beautiful,  and elegant.  However,  the evidence  gathered  thus far and reviewed  in this section  
clearly  demonstrates  that the principle  of mathematical  simplicity  of consonance  ratios  cannot  fully account  for the 
perception  of C/D. In the following  sections,  we will continue  with reviewing  other explanatory  factors  that have 
emerged  from the psychoacoustic  (§3) and neurobiological  (§4) approaches  to C/D.
3. The psychoacoustics  of C/D
3.1. Helmholtz’s  approach:  roughness  and beating
One of the earliest  systematic  approaches  to C/D was presented  by the eminent  psychophysicist  Hermann  von 
Helmholtz  (1821–1894).  In his groundbreaking  treatise  On the sensations  of tone (1875/1954),  Helmholtz  attempted  
“to connect  the boundaries  of two sciences,  which,  although  drawn  toward  each other by many  natural  afﬁnities,  
have hitherto  remained  practically  distinct  – [...] the boundaries  of physical  and physiological  acoustics on the one 
side, and of musical  science  and esthetics on the other”  (Helmholtz,  1854,  p. 1, italics  in the text). By doing  so, 
he also attempted  to connect  the mathematical  explanation  with the physiological  explanation,  providing  the ﬁrst 
psychoacoustic  explanation  of C/D.
Helmholtz  based  his explanation  on two related  key notions,  i.e., “roughness”  (or “beating”)  and “critical  band-
width”.  Roughness  is the sensation  characterizing  complex  tones  that contain  inharmonicities  such that their adjacent  
partials  interfere  ([165,261]; see also [61], for a recent  review).  In psychoacoustic  terms,  roughness  has been deﬁned  
as the auditory  effect  elicited  by “beating  at frequencies  in the range  20–300  Hz” ([165, p. 178];  15-300  Hz according  
to Zwicker  & Fastl [261, p. 257]),  reaching  a maximum  at a frequency  of approximately  70 Hz ([165, p. 58], [261, p. 
257]).5For Helmholtz,  the absence  of roughness  characterizes  consonance  that, as such, is considered  an “exceptional  
case”  ([95, p. 194]).  Roughness  is, in turn, related  to beating,  the sensation  that is evoked  when  harmonics  of two 
tones  sounding  simultaneously  are not sufﬁciently  spaced  far apart and therefore  mutually  interfere,  giving  rise to a 
modiﬁed  waveform  with a rhythmic  oscillation  in the vibration  pattern  or amplitude.  For example,  an interval  of an 
octave  (2:1) has all the harmonics  that coincide,  thus resulting  in the absence  of beating.  In contrast,  an octave  slightly  
mistuned  by an εfactor  (f2=2f1+ε) will generate  beating  at the frequency  of ε[175].
Helmholtz  was the ﬁrst to explain  the link between  beating,  roughness,  and C/D in physiological  terms:  “The 
nerves  of hearing  feel these rapid beats as rough and unpleasant,  because  every  intermittent  excitement  of any nervous  
apparatus  affects  us more powerfully  than one that lasts unaltered.  With this, there is possibly  a psychological  cause.  
The individual  pulses  of tone in a dissonant  combination  certainly  give us the same impression  of separate  pulses  as 
slow beats,  although  we are unable  to recognize  them separately  and count  them;  hence,  they form a tangled mass 
of tone, which  cannot  be analyzed  into its constituents.  The cause  of the unpleasantness  of dissonance  we attribute  
to this roughness and entanglement . The meaning  of this distinction  may thus be brieﬂy  stated:  Consonance  is a 
continuous,  dissonance  an intermittent  sensation  of tone. [...] Consonance  is the blending  of a higher  with a lower  
tone. Dissonance  is incapacity  to mix, when  two tones  cannot  blend,  but appear  rough  to the ear” ([95, p. 226], italics  
in the text).

For a given  frequency,  the smallest  band of frequencies  that evoke  beating  is called  the “critical  bandwidth”,  
which  Helmholtz  claimed  was rooted  in the biological  properties  of the inner ear, i.e., in the mechanics  of the basilar  
membrane.  According  to Helmholtz,  “When  a simple  tone is presented  to the ear, those  Corti’s  arches  which  are nearly  
or exactly  in unison  with it will be strongly  excited,  and the rest only slightly  or not at all. Hence,  every  simple  tone 
of determinate  pitch will be felt only by certain  nerve  ﬁbers,  and simple  tones  of different  pitch will excite  different  
ﬁbers.  When  a compound  musical  tone or chord  is presented  to the ear, all those  elastic  bodies  will be excited,  which  
have a proper  pitch corresponding  to the various  individual  simple  tones  contained  in the whole  mass of tones,  and 
hence  by properly  directing  attention,  all the individual  sensations  of the individual  simple  tones  can be perceived.  The 
chord  must be resolved  into its individual  compound  tones,  and the compound  tone into its individual  harmonic  partial  
tones”  ([95, pp. 147-148]).  According  to this now commonly  accepted  notion,  also known  as ‘tonotopic  theory’,  each 
site along  the basilar  membrane  is characterized  by a resonance  frequency  based  on the morphological  characteristics  
of the organic  tissue,  such as thickness,  elasticity,  and rigidity  (cf. [95, pp. 138-141]).6Different  frequencies  trigger  
different  modes  of membrane  vibration,  i.e., with different  points  of maximum  oscillation.  When  two sounds  within  
critical  bandwidth  hit the ear, portions  of the membrane  that overlap  each other start vibrating,  possibly  giving  rise to 
the unpleasant  sensation  associated  with the beats.
Helmholtz’s  psychoacoustic  explanation  of C/D, thus, holds  that pitches  of consonant  dyads  have fewer  partials  
that pass through  the same critical  bands,  while  the pitches  of dissonant  intervals  have partials  that compete  within  
individual  channels.  For Helmholtz,  therefore,  “the enigma  which,  about  2500 years  ago, Pythagoras  proposed  to 
science,  which  investigates  the reason  of things,  ‘Why  is consonance  determined  by the ratio of small  whole  numbers?’  
has been solved  by the discovery  that the ear resolves  all complex  sounds  into pendular  oscillations,  according  to the 
laws of sympathetic  vibration,  and that it regards  as harmonious  only such excitements  of the nerves  as continue  
without  disturbance”  ([95, p. 229]).
Although  the role of beating  and its possible  resolution  by critical  bands  for C/D perception  has been empirically  
supported  by later ﬁndings  (e.g., [175]), additional  evidence  showed  that beating  only plays  a secondary  role in C/D 
perception.  In particular,  studies  on dichotic  listening,  i.e., where  the two tones  forming  the interval  are separately  fed 
to two ears, thus eliminating  the interactions  that are responsible  for roughness  and beating,  suggest  that C/D percep-
tion is not altered  [19,99,143]. Additionally,  when  intervals  are created  with synthesized  tones  from which  interacting  
partials  have been artiﬁcially  removed,  this does not consistently  affect  their perceived  consonance  [157]. However,  
additional  studies  provided  at least partially  inconsistent  ﬁndings,  thus leaving  the issue open for further  investigation.  
For example,  evidence  showed  that dichotic  presentation  increases  the consonance  of small  pitch intervals,  particu-
larly major  and minor  seconds  ([87,92]), while  Bones  et al. [25], showed  that diotically  presented  consonances  are 
perceived  as more pleasant  than dichotically  presented  ones. Finally,  ﬁndings  by McDermott  and colleagues  [144]
have revealed  cultural  differences  in dissonance  perception  when  the stimuli  were presented  diotically  as opposed  to 
dichotically.
3.2. Fusion
Helmholtz’s  approach  has strongly  contributed  to the birth of tone psychology,  conceived  of as the experimental  
study  of sound  perception.  In the attempt  to explain  C/D, Carl Stumpf,  one of the key ﬁgures  of this novel  approach,  
presented  the notion  of “perceptual  fusion”  [219,220], conceived  of as the sensory  correlate  of the merging  of multiple  
harmonic  complex  tones  into a unique  percept.  For Stumpf,  fusion  results  from low-level  processes  of sensory  percep-
tion, which  triggers  a sense  of unity in the listener.  As a matter  of fact, unity can also be deﬁned  as a lack of distinction  
among  elements,  and therefore  “fusion”  has been occasionally  operationalized  as “perceptual  indiscriminability”,  that 
is, the inability  to identify  the constituent  tones  of a sonority  [60,146]. However,  Stumpf  apparently  conceived  fusion  
as an active  perceptual  process  that leads to the formation of a coherent whole  [121,202].
Stumpf  carried  out experiments  in which  he investigated  how nonmusicians  perceived  tone pairs as single  tones  
depending  on the intervals  [121] and  found  that judgments  of fusion  correlated  with the degree  of consonance  of 
intervals.  Speciﬁcally,  the most fused  intervals  were the unison,  the octave,  the perfect  ﬁfth and perfect  fourth,  cor-
responding  to the order  with respect  to the perceived  degree  of consonance  (cf. [121, p. 141]).  Therefore,  Stumpf

3.3. Tension
Another  psychoacoustic  construct  addressed  early in C/D studies  was ‘tension’  (e.g., [238]; see also [9]),7which  
expresses  the degree  of instability  that characterizes  an auditory  percept.  Therefore,  in music,  the construct  of tension  
is connected  to expectancy  and relaxation,  with tension  increasing  the expectation  for resolution,  which  is perceived  
in turn as relaxation  (e.g., [22,149]).
Several  psychoacoustic  factors  have been recognized  as affecting  tension,  such as roughness,  sensory  consonance,  
and timbre  [179]. With respect  to other psychoacoustic  descriptors  of consonance  (e.g., harmoniousness  and purity),  
tension  seems  to be correlated  with consonance  independent  of musical  expertise  within  the Western  culture  [124]. 
Arthurs  and colleagues  [9] presented  chords  played  either  by piano  or by organ  and asked  participants  to rate them 
for consonance,  pleasantness,  stability,  and relaxation  (deﬁned  as the opposite  of tension).  The ﬁndings  showed  that 
consonance  ratings  strongly  correlated  with ratings  for the other three dimensions,  such that more consonant  chords  
were also typically  judged  to be more pleasant,  stable,  and relaxed.  However,  Lahdelma  and Eerola  [123] found  
that, among  Western  listeners,  mildly  dissonant  (and hence  also tense)  yet culturally  familiar  chords  are generally  
slightly  more preferred  than maximally  consonant  and relaxed  ones. Authors  interpreted  such ﬁndings  according  to 
the inverted-U  theory  (e.g., [15]), which  postulates  that moderate  complexity  yields  preference.
3.4. Sharpness
Recent  research  on C/D perception  has drawn  attention  on another  acoustic  factor,  namely  acoustic  sharpness  
[11,12,140,261]. Sharpness  describes  the proportion  of energy  at high frequencies  and, according  to Zwicker  and 
Fastl ([261], p. 239-242)  is essentially  inﬂuenced  by the spectral  content  and bandwidth.  Together  with other audi-
tory sensations,  such as roughness  and loudness,  sharpness  affects  sensory  pleasantness,  with sharper  sounds  (those  
with more high-frequency  energy)  generally  found  to be less pleasant.  Sharpness  tends  to increase  with frequency,  
with frequencies  in the range  of 2–4 kHz  contributing  the most to annoyingness  [140]. The recent  study  by Eerola  
and Lahdelma  [67] provided  illuminating  ﬁndings  about  how sharpness  (and roughness)  affect(s)  the perception  of 
consonance,  demonstrating  that sharpness  impacts  on perceived  C/D in the highest  register,  while  roughness  is mainly  
responsible  for the decrease  in consonance  at the lowest  register.

classical  music  to classify  the most frequent  trichords.  They found  that certain  combinations  of sound,  e.g., a minor  
or major  triad, were more frequently  and consistently  used throughout  centuries.  Then,  they explored  C/D concepts  
by comparing  the prevalence  of chord  types  in different  periods  with predictions  based  on quantitative  models.  Each 
model  was based  on a different  explanatory  factor  of C/D, such as, diatonicity,  roughness,  familiarity,  unevenness,  
and harmonicity.  They showed  that both psychoacoustic  and cultural  factors—namely,  roughness,  harmonicity,  and 
familiarity—have  most contributed  to determining  the use of C/D throughout  Western  music  history.9

perception,  McDermott  and colleagues  [144] exposed  listeners  naïve  to Western  music  (the Tsimané,  an indigenous

4. The neurobiological  basis  of C/D perception
Different  experimental  methods  have been used to investigate  the neurobiological  processes  underlying  C/D per-
ception  in humans.  Studies  adopting  brain research  methodologies,  such as functional  magnetic  resonance  imaging  
(fMRI),  electroencephalography  (EEG)  or magnetoencephalography  (MEG),  have shown  that consonant  and disso-
nant auditory  stimuli  related  by simple  and complex  frequency  ratios  generate  distinct  reactions  in the auditory  cortex  
of the adult human  brain ([32,33,74,75,151,170,187]). Additionally,  it has been observed  that C/D stimuli  elicit dif-
ferent  responses  at low levels  of the auditory  nervous  system,  for example,  in the auditory  nerve  (e.g., [18,234]) and 
subcortical  brainstem  activity  (e.g., [19]). Such results  led researchers  to claim  that listeners  (at least Western  listen-
ers, who are typically  involved  as participants  in the majority  of the reviewed  studies)  have processing  advantages  
for consonance  [25,224]. Hereinafter,  we will delve  into this claim,  ﬁrst by reviewing  evidence  on neurobiological  
C/D discrimination  in the brainstem  and auditory  cortex  and then by illustrating  ﬁndings  on the ease of processing  of 
consonant  stimuli.
4.1. Neurobiological  discrimination:  subcortical  and cortical  processing
Neurobiological  evidence  has shown  that C/D stimuli  are differently  processed  at both subcortical  and cortical  
levels  of the auditory  system.  Studies  on brainstem  evoked  potentials  showed  that consonant  intervals  produced  more 
robust  neural  responses  than dissonant  intervals  ([19]). Further  evidence  demonstrates  that the more dissonant  the 
interval  is, the less periodic  the response  ([127]s e e also [25,54]) and that the magnitude  of phase-locked  activity  
in the auditory  nerve  and inferior  colliculus  correlate  with the perceived  C/D of musical  intervals  [145,234]. Tramo  
and colleagues  [234] conducted  a systematic  study  of the neural  correlates  of consonance  in the auditory  nerve  of 
anesthetized  cats. Using  both pure and complex  tones,  they found  strong  neural  correlates  both for roughness  and 
for pitch fusion/stability  that replicated  the rank ordering  of perceptual  consonance  judgments  for both pure and 
complex  dyads.  These  ﬁndings  suggest  that information  relevant  to musical  C/D is encoded  in earlier  components  of 
the auditory  system.  In particular,  in the auditory  nerve,  spikes  are correlated  with the stimulus  waveform,  such that 
the periodicities  in the waveform  are impressed  on the temporal  patterns  of spikes.  Because  of this correlation,  also 
known  as “phase  locking,”  patterns  of time durations  between  spikes—interspike  intervals—reﬂect  periodicities  in the 
stimulus  [40].
In the case of cortical  processing,  studies  have shown  that C/D stimuli  are differently  processed  in the primary  
auditory  cortex  independent  of listeners’  musical  expertise  and tuning  system.  Examining  responses  of nonmusicians  
to two-note  intervals  varying  in sensory  consonance  in equal-tempered  tuning,  Itoh, Suwazono,  and Nakada  [104]
found  that the most dissonant  interval  (the minor  second)  produced  the most negative  response,  while  the consonant  
interval  (the perfect  ﬁfth) produced  the least negative  (most  positive)  response  for both P2 and N2 event-related  poten-
tial (ERP)  components.  Similar  results  were obtained  in a later study  comparing  musicians  and nonmusicians  ([105]; 
see also [72,196]). In an MEG  protocol,  Pallesen  and colleagues  [161]s h o w e d  that musical  expertise  is reﬂected  in 
generally  enhanced  phase  locking  of ongoing  oscillations  to the onset  of musical  chords.  In particular,  musicians  and 
nonmusicians  had strengthened  phase  locking  to nonprototypical  dissonant  chords  and to minor  chords,  respectively.
C/D discrimination  has also been investigated  using  mismatch  negativity  (MMN)  response.  The MMN  response  
is a component  of event-related  potential,  which  is typically  evoked  by auditory-cortex  neurons  when  an infrequent  
sound  interrupts  a sequence  of regular  ones, indexing  automatic  predictive  and discriminatory  processes  in the central  
auditory  system  (for reviews,  [155,156]). Brattico  and colleagues  [34]u s e d  an MMN  paradigm  to test the role of the 
auditory  cortex  in C/D discrimination  of a subject  with bilateral  lesions  in the auditory  cortex.  Brain  responses  did 
not differentiate  musical  intervals  in terms  of their C/D, thus conﬁrming  the idea that this phenomenon  requires  the 
integrity  of cortical  functions,  in addition  to hearing.  In a critical  study  by Virtala  and colleagues  [243], the MMN  
response  was used to investigate  C/D discrimination  in newborn  infants.  While  root form major  chords  served  as

ﬁrst two notes,  followed  by a tritone,  both considered  highly  dissonant  intervals.  Findings  showed  that newborn  infants  
discriminate  between  consonance  and high levels  of dissonance  in chords  at the level of the preattentive  processing

or inhibition  when  the tones  are harmonically  related.  Bendor  and Wang  [13] further  showed  that in regions  bordering  
the primary  auditory  cortex,  neurons  respond  to both isolated  fundamental  frequencies  and their associated  harmonic  
series,  even when  the latter is presented  without  the fundamental.
Taken  together,  the ﬁndings  reviewed  in this section  suggest  that C/D discrimination  might  be rooted  in preattentive  
early stages  of auditory  processing,  both at the level of the brainstem  and the primary  auditory  cortex.  Moreover,  per-
ceptually  salient  features  of the auditory  stimuli,  i.e., harmonicity,  have been shown  to correlate  with harmonic  neural  
responses,  showing  resonance  between  physical  and neural  oscillations.  The results  obtained  from nonmusicians  and 
nonhuman  primates  [13,108]a l s o  suggest  that the indexed  mechanisms  underlying  C/D perception  might  be universal.  
Thus,  nonlinear  models  to describe  phase-locked  neural  populations  as the basis for musical  consonance  have been 
proposed,  leading  to the development  of a dynamical  system  model  based  on stylized  neural  oscillators  producing  
both synchronization  and mode  locking  ([131]; see, also, [162], and [237], for neural  networks  and computational  
approaches  to C/D, respectively).  However,  the extent  to which  such processing  mechanisms  determine  or shape  aes-
thetic  preferences  for C/D demands  further  investigation.  Hereinafter,  we review  evidence  indicating  that consonant  
stimuli  are processed  more efﬁciently  by the central  auditory  system  than dissonant  stimuli.
4.2. Processing  advantages:  perceptual  coherence  and processing  ﬂuency
Several  neurophysiological  studies  have shown  that consonance  processing  starts  early in the human  auditory  cortex  
and that additional  neural  resources  are needed  to encode  and discriminate  dissonant  chords  compared  with consonant  
chords  [33,56,229,243]. In an MEG  study,  Tabas  and colleagues  [224] measured  the neuromagnetic  activity  evoked  
by dyads  with varying  degrees  of consonance  or dissonance,  ﬁnding  that dissonant  dyads  evoke  a pitch onset  response  
(POR)  with a latency  up to 36 mslonger  than consonant  dyads.  Anderman  and colleagues  [3] conﬁrmed  that POR is 
strongly  modulated  by the degree  of consonance  and that latencies  of the transient  wave peaks  in response  to consonant  
dyads  are shorter  than those  elicited  by dissonant  stimuli.  Such ﬁndings  conﬁrm  that the auditory  cortex  requires  more 
time to accomplish  the early processing  of dissonant  vs. consonant  dyads.  At this point,  one might  ask why consonant  
stimuli  are processed  faster  than dissonant  sounds.  We propose  two possible  reasons,  namely,  perceptual  coherence  
and processing  ﬂuency.
Perceptual  coherence  is a property  that can be attributed  to stimuli  that form a coherent  whole  and, therefore,  are 
more likely  perceived  as unitary  [89]. This concept  was popularly  debated  in the Gestalt Psychologie [114], which  
applied  it mainly  to visual  objects  (e.g., Kanizsa  ﬁgures).  Neurophysiological  studies  have shown  that higher  gamma  
band responses  are typically  associated  with more coherent  percepts  in vision  (see, e.g., [57,154]). For example,  
an MEG  study  showed  enhancement  of induced  gamma  band activity  to the perception  of Kanizsa  triangles  [109]. 
Similar  ﬁndings  were also obtained  in protocols  in which  perceptual  coherence  emerges  as a result  of dynamic  motion.  
For instance,  Muller  and colleagues  [154] recorded  brain activities  from participants  watching  on a screen  a single  
bar (coherent  motion)  or two identical  bars moving  in opposite  directions  (incoherent  motion).  The ﬁndings  revealed  
enhanced  gamma-band  activity  elicited  by coherent  vs. incoherent  motion  stimuli.
Moving  back to audition,  one might  hypothesize  that the perception  of intervals  with pitches  that merge  coherently  
(i.e., consonances)  elicits  higher  gamma  band activity  than combined  tones  that are not perceived  as coherent  and 
fuse (e.g., dissonances).  Empirical  ﬁndings  seem to support  this hypothesis,  revealing  that consonant  sounds  elicit

higher  gamma  band activity  than dissonant  sounds  [164,168] and  conﬁrming  that dissonant  sounds  might  produce  
a breakdown  of the percept  of a unitary  sound  (see, e.g., [57,154]).12This hypothesis  receives  further  support  by a

are more fuse, unitary,  and coherent  percepts  compared  with dissonant  perceptions.  At this point,  one might  expect  
that more coherent  percepts  are also more ﬂuently  processed  by the sensory  system.  Therefore,  we examine  the 
psychological  construct  of “ﬂuency”  as the second  factor  that might  inﬂuence  the processing  of C/D stimuli.
The ‘processing  ﬂuency’  theory  suggests  that the more ﬂuently  the perceiver  can process  an object,  the more 
positive  his or her aesthetic  response  is [186]. An early formulation  of such a principle  might  be traced  back to 
Helmholtz,  who wrote:  “The more easily  we perceived  the order  which  characterizes  the objects  contemplated,  the 
more simple  and the more perfect  will they appear,  and the more joyfully  shall we acknowledge  them”  ([95, p. 230]).  
Due to the abundance  of ﬁndings  suggesting  that Western  listeners  have a preference  for listening  to consonant,  as 
opposed  to dissonant,  stimuli,  the pleasantness  of consonances  might  well represent  a correlate  of processing  ﬂuency.  
Neurobiological  evidence  seemingly  supports  this claim.  For instance,  frequency-following  response  studies  indicate  
that the perception  of consonance  vs. dissonance  might  correspond  to the ﬂuency  of neural  processing  for pitch at the 
level of the brainstem  (e.g., [19]) and of the auditory  cortex  (e.g., [33,72]). Incidentally,  the ease of processing  might  
also impact  cognitive  tasks,  such as facilitating  the detection  of abstract  patterns  ([55]).
Interestingly,  while  coherence  arises  “instantaneously”  from the combination  of diverse  perceptual  elements,  ﬂu-
ency can be conceived  of as a property  emerging  from the processing  of auditory  signals  over time. Therefore,  the ease 
of processing  might  be rooted  in the dynamical  properties  of the auditory  system.  Pioneering  studies  by Cartwright  
and colleagues  (e.g., [42,43]) showed  that the perception  of the pitch of complex  sounds  can be described  by means  
of the dynamical  properties  of our auditory  system.  In particular,  nonlinear  approaches  to auditory  perception  indicate  
a link between  consonance  and the dynamic  features  of the signal,  suggesting  that dissonance  is more demanding  for 
the sensory  system  due to the lack of periodicity  at various  levels  of the auditory  system  that is associated  with sensory  
dissonance  (e.g., [19,127,234]). These  models  conﬁrm  that information  related  to consonant  stimuli  is more ﬂuently  
analyzed  and more rapidly  resolved  by the auditory  system.
Taken  together,  the reviewed  evidence  indicates  that C/D stimuli  are processed  differently  due to properties  of both 
the auditory  signal,  e.g., periodicity  and harmonicity,  and of the auditory  system,  i.e., neural  resonance  and processing  
ﬂuency  (see Table 2). Such physical  and biological  biases  might  have paved  the way for the general  prevalence  of 
consonant  pitch relationships  in (at least Western)  compositional  practice  and, to some  extent,  for (at least Western)  
listeners’  more positive  aesthetic  evaluation  of consonance  than dissonance.13
5. Discussion
In what follows,  we examine  a number  of conceptual,  methodological,  and terminological  issues  that arise from 
the reviewed  literature.  In Section 5.1, we take a position  in the debate  over the Pythagorean  origins  of C/D, trying  
to assess  whether  and how integer  ratios  might  be considered  foundational  in C/D theorizations.  In Section 5.2, we 
discuss  inconsistent  ﬁndings  in the neurophysiological  literature.  In Section 5.3, we reﬂect  on the nature  vs. culture  
dichotomy  and on its role in inspiring  much  of the empirical  research  in C/D perception.  Finally,  in Section 5.4, we 
critically  examine  two variables/factors  that have been widely  investigated  in recent  behavioural  literature,  namely  
preference  and familiarity.

Notwithstanding  the apparent  falsiﬁcation  of the Pythagorean  hypothesis,  we suggest  that those  ﬁndings  do not 
necessarily  lead to rejecting,  and therefore  abandoning,  the core tenets  of Pythagorean  theory,  namely,  that numbers  
play a role in C/D explanation.  To support  this claim,  we discuss  Pythagorean  theory  in light of two well-known,  and 
to some  extent  opposed,  philosophical  accounts  of scientiﬁc  theories,  namely,  Popper’s  falsiﬁcationism  and Lakatos’  
idea of research  program.
According  to Popper  [177], for a theory  to be scientiﬁc  it must be able to be proven  false.  The ultimate  goal of 
researchers  is the falsiﬁcation  of a theory,  and any refuting  evidence,  when  encountered,  must lead to the rejection  of 
the theory  itself [177]. Applying  such a view to Pythagorean  theory,  two consequences  would  follow:  i) the theory  is 
scientiﬁc  (it is falsiﬁable);  and ii) it should  be rejected  (it has been falsiﬁed).  However,  as evidenced  also by many  
commentators  (e.g., [71,118]), Popper’s  theorization  is seemingly  too simplistic  to account  for the way scientiﬁc

theories  have evolved  throughout  the history  of science.  An alternative,  and likely  much  more realistic,  approach  was 
proposed  by Lakatos  [126], who believed  that Popper  was wrong  to think theories  must be rejected  when  they fail 
tests. In contrast,  for Lakatos,  scientiﬁc  theories  are research  programs  composed  of a hard core and a protective  belt 
surrounding  the core. When  refuting  evidence  is encountered,  according  to Lakatos,  scientists  do not simply  “refute”  
the core tenets;  rather,  they will begin  to alter the assumptions  of the “protective  belt” in ways suggested  by additional  
empirical  evidence.
Based  on Lakatos’  account,  we suggest  viewing  Pythagorean  theory  as a research  program,  with the simplicity  of 
integers  ratio as scientiﬁc  core and the following  facts as auxiliary  evidence  that can defend  the core.
i) In most adopted  tuning  systems,  adjusted  consonant  ratios  are kept very close to their Pythagorean  counterparts,  
while  dissonant  ones are altered  more to save others  (for example,  in an equal  temperament,  an octave  is expressed  
in the ratio of 2:1, and ﬁfths and fourths  are very close to 2:3 (1,5) and 3:4 (1,333),  i.e., 1,498  and 1,335,  respec-
tively,  while  a minor  second  is altered  slightly  more,  e.g., 1.059  vs. 1,063).  Similar  adjustments  might  reﬂect  
the Pythagorean  idea that simpler  (or better,  very close to simpler)  ratios  constitute  the key pillars  of the tuning  
system,  thus suggesting  that Pythagorean  criteria  were used for the creation  of the non-Pythagorean  systems  that 
are commonly  adopted  in Western  musical  practices.  This would  also be supported  by evidence  suggesting  that 
the auditory  system  tolerates  small  deviations  from perfect  integer  relationships  ([234, p. 97]), in line with the 
modern  conception  of intervals  as (to a certain  extent)  ﬂexible  frequency  ranges  rather  than sharp  frequency  ratios  
[198].
ii) Recent  approaches  to the functioning  of the auditory  system  from the perspective  of nonlinear  dynamics  indirectly  
support  Pythagorean  ideas [44]. Modelling  the perception  of harmonic  intervals  as a dynamical  system  forced  by 
two independent  frequencies  (the tones  that form the interval)  and the third triggered  frequency  (the response),  
the frequencies  of harmonic  intervals  that resonate  in the brain appear  to be organized  in a Pythagorean  fashion  
by means  of hierarchical  rules dictated  by number  theory  (e.g., the Farey  tree) ([42]; see also [237], for a different  
approach,  and [75], for neurophysiological  ﬁndings).
To summarize,  Pythagorean  theory  has been considered  for centuries  as a theoretically  powerful,  simple,  and elegant  
way to account  for the C/D phenomenon.  However,  as often happens  when  simple  and abstract  principles  are applied  
to concrete  practices,  several  factors—e.g.,  polyphonic  composition  and singing,  construction  and tuning  of musical  
instruments,  creation  of an easy-to-learn  and −read music  notation—must  have forced  theorists,  composers,  and 
musicians  to slightly  adapt  Pythagorean  ratios  to concrete  practices.  However,  the point we want to stress  here is that 
such a process  of adaptation  might  have been possible  thanks  to, and not despite,  the existence  of Pythagorean-like  
principles  that likely  served  as key criteria  guiding  the introduction  of adjustments  into the tuning  system.  Thus,  it 
might  be worth  returning  to Helmholtz  on this point:  “If the boundary  between  consonance  and dissonance  has really  
changed  with a change  of tonal system,  it is manifest  that the reason  for assigning  this boundary  does not depend  on 
the intervals  and their individual  musical  effect,  but on the whole  construction  of the tonal system”  ([95, p. 229]).
5.2. Inconsistent  neurophysiological  ﬁndings
Most neurophysiological  studies  have provided  evidence  of different  processing  of consonant  vs. dissonant  stimuli  
in the human  brain,  both at the subcortical  and cortical  levels  (see Section 4). However,  inconsistent  ﬁndings  have also 
been reported  regarding  several  speciﬁc  neurophysiological  responses  to C/D. In what follows,  we focus  especially  
on the N100  (or N1) transient  wave response  and on hemispheric  specialization.
5.2.1.  N100
N1 or N100  is a negative  evoked  potential  (or event-related  potential,  ERP)  measured  with EEG and with a mag-
netic counterpart  measured  with MEG,  peaking  negatively  over the vertex  of the scalp (when  measured  with EEG)  
at approximately  80–120  ms after the onset  of a stimulus.  N1 is elicited  by the onset  or offset  of any sound  and is 
typically  followed  by the P2- or  P200-evoked  potential.  While  most research  on N1 focuses  on auditory  stimuli,  such 
a response  also occurs  for other kinds  of stimuli,  such as visual  and olfactory  stimuli.
Studies  investigating  N1 responses  to C/D have reported  inconsistent  ﬁndings.  Using  consonant  and dissonant  
chords  played  with a piano  timbre,  Minati  and colleagues  [151] found  no effects  of chord  type and participant  group

5.2.2.  Hemispheric  specialization

consonant  stimuli  are typically  perceived  as positively  valenced  [92,139,168].

5.3. Nature  vs. culture
Much  of the research  effort  in the ﬁeld of C/D studies  has attempted  to determine  whether  the origins  of consonance  
are biological  or cultural.  To this aim, developmental,  cross-cultural,  and cross-species  studies  have been developed  to 
unravel  universal  aspects  of C/D perception.  For example,  researchers  have investigated  young  listeners  at early stages  
of development,  i.e., infants  and toddlers,  as they are less culturally  inﬂuenced  than older listeners.  More  recently,  ex-
perimenters  involved  participants  from different  cultures  naïve  to the Western  musical  system  (e.g., [10,122,144,147]) 
or, conversely,  exposed  Western  listeners  to unconventional  tuning  systems  [76]. Additionally,  experimental  protocols  
on nonhuman  animals  have been carried  out with the idea that any discrimination  abilities  found  in animals  would  
shed light on the biological  origin  of C/D. Hereinafter,  we provide  a brief overview  of some  of these ﬁndings  with the 
aim of understanding  whether  the nature  vs. culture  dichotomy  is a fruitful  conceptual  approach  for investigating  C/D 
perception.
In the developmental  literature,  several  studies  have successfully  proven  that infants  and children  are able to dis-
criminate  between  consonant  and dissonant  stimuli  [62,133,138,200,201,232,233]. In these studies,  researchers  have 
adopted  a wide variety  of stimuli,  such as, isolated  pure sound  intervals  or complex  sound  intervals  [232,233], se-
quences  of chords  [257], a short unaccompanied  melody  [133], and melody  and chords  together  [138,232]. Given  the 
rationale  of testing  subjects  prior to any signiﬁcant  impact  of culture,  ﬁndings  on younger  listeners  are assumed  to 
provide  much  stronger  results  than those  gathered  from older and culturally  biased  listeners.  To date, evidence  has 
demonstrated  that infants’  ability  to distinguish  between  C/D appears  as early as 2 days after birth [138]. Neurophys-
iological  evidence  further  conﬁrmed  that 1- to 4-day-old  infants’  brains  are sensitive  to consonance  and dissonance  
([170,243]). The literature  thus seems  to suggest  that culture  plays  little or no role in consonance  discrimination  
among  humans  (though  see [174], on 6-month-old  infants).
In parallel  with research  on infants,  an increasing  number  of behavioral  studies  has also investigated  C/D perception  
in nonhuman  animals.  In a pioneering  study,  Fannin  and Braud  [70] exposed  albino  rats for 20 mins per day for 10 
days to two levers,  one producing  consonant  sounds  and the other producing  dissonant  sounds  (arguably,  melodic  
intervals).  The mean  number  of daily presses  on the consonant  and dissonant  levers  increased  with exposure  to the 
tones,  with animals  increasingly  preferring  to act on the lever producing  the consonant  tones.  Using  a similar  protocol,  
Borchgrevink  [30] obtained  the same results,  thus suggesting  that albino  rats are able to distinguish  between  C/D 
and develop  a preference  for consonance  (though  see [178], on laboratory  mice).  Using  a discrimination  protocol,  
Izumi  [106] trained  three Japanese  monkeys  to distinguish  simultaneous  two-tone  complexes  presenting  different  
degrees  of C/D. In the experiments,  the performances  with detecting  changes  from consonant  to dissonant  chords  
(perfect  ﬁfth to major  seventh;  perfect  fourth  to major  seventh)  were better  than those  with detecting  reverse  changes,  
thus suggesting  that the consonance  of chords  affected  the performances  of monkeys.  Additionally,  Sugimoto  and 
colleagues  [221] reported  that a ﬁve-month-old  human-raised  chimpanzee  (Pan Troglodytes ) consistently  preferred  to 
produce  consonant  versions  of music  for a longer  duration  than dissonant  versions,  and Chiandetti  and Vallortigara  
[50] found  that newly  hatched  domestic  chicks  show  a spontaneous  preference  for a visual  imprinting  object  associated  
with consonant  intervals  over an identical  object  associated  with dissonant  intervals.  Together  with other ﬁndings  (e.g., 
[65,101,247]), these results  suggest  that the preference  for consonance  might  be related  to certain  acoustic  features  of 
biological  sounds  in natural  environments  and not to human  culture  (though  see [141], on tamarin  monkeys  and [113]
on Campbell’s  monkeys;  [35,245]).
The effect  of culture  on C/D theorization  and perception  has also been investigated  in several  cross-cultural  music  
studies.  In general,  those  studies  showed  that the main musical  traditions  in the world  make  speciﬁc  use of many  
of the same intervals  (e.g., [23,36,120]) and that the most frequently  used intervals  correspond  to those  considered

To recapitulate,  the neurophysiological  evidence  on humans  and nonhuman  primates  (see Section 4) seem to

why such pitch combinations  have been favored  by composers  and listeners  for centuries  [38]. However,  two relevant  
issues  remain  open,  one regarding  the inﬂuence  of culture  and musical  expertise  on C/D perception  and the other,  
more general,  concerning  the real beneﬁt  of approaching  C/D perception  from a dichotomic,  i.e., nature  vs. culture,  
perspective.
Regarding  the impact  of musical  expertise  on C/D processing,  studies  have shown  that perceived  dissonance  is 
reduced  by chord  familiarity  and greater  music  training  [127] and that consonant  and dissonant  stimuli  (major  sixth 
vs. minor  seventh)  evoke  different  responses  in the primary  auditory  cortex  in musicians  vs. nonmusicians  ([146]; 
see also [160]). Together  with other evidence  (e.g., [16,119,161,174,248]), these ﬁndings  suggest  that enculturation,  
individual  experience,  and music  training  effectively  impact  listeners’  perceptions  of C/D. In particular,  as suggested  
by Bidelman  [17], late, endogenous  auditory  processing  seems  particularly  sensitive  to the inﬂuence  of familiarity,  as 
also indicated  by EEG and functional  neuroimaging  studies  ([151]). Remarkably,  beyond  C/D (e.g., [124]), familiarity  
is also shown  to affect  the perceived  pleasantness  of different  auditory  stimuli,  such as music  excerpts  (e.g., [117]) 
and human  voice  (e.g., [240]). This may be due to the mere exposure  effect  [253]o r , more speciﬁcally,  to an increase  
in processing  ﬂuency  [186] that is shown  to be linked  to an increase  in preference  toward  the stimulus  [250].
Rather  than rejecting  the universal  nature  of consonance,  the evidence  on the inﬂuence  of culture  and musical  
expertise  on C/D perception  might  suggest  that we reinterpret  the ﬁndings  on the biological  origins  of C/D and, 
more generally,  consider  the biology  vs. culture  dichotomy  more carefully  with respect  to the origin  of C/D. Rather  
than an alternative  between  opposites,  the distinction  between  nature  and culture  should  be better  conceived  in terms  
of continuity.  In this respect,  the ﬁndings  of the recent  study  by Linnavalli  and colleagues  [130] proved  illuminat-
ing. Combining  both behavioral  (i.e., discrimination  task) and neurophysiological  measures  (i.e., MMN),  the authors  
found  that musicians  and nonmusicians  discriminated  dissonant  from consonant  chords,  both neurally  and behav-
iorally.  However,  musical  expertise  affected  only performances  in the behavioral  task, with musicians  being  more 
accurate  than nonmusicians.  In contrast,  MMN  seems  to conﬁrm  that sensory  discrimination  between  consonance  
vs. dissonance  is universal  and is based  on physiological  properties  of the auditory  system.  The study  indicates  that 
music  expertise  facilitates  only behavioral  performance,  which  spans  a much  longer  temporal  frame  than MMN  re-
sponses  and involves  higher-order  cognitive  abilities,  while  fundamental,  low-level  automatic  sensory  reactions  are 
not differentiated  based  on musical  expertise.
In summary,  empirical  evidence  seems  to suggest  that enculturation  has to be conceived  as part of a biologically  
constrained  process  and, at the same time, that (human)  biology  is not carved  in stone,  but rather  results  from a 
constant  and dynamic  interaction  with a changing  environment.  Therefore,  any further  attempt  to demonstrate  that 
consonance  is a cultural  creation  should  not necessarily  lead to the conclusion  that it is conventional  or arbitrary  
(as claimed  by Cazden  [45]). Rather,  as suggested  by Linnavalli  and colleagues  [130]), similar  ﬁndings  would  only 
indicate  that the processing  of the auditory  system  does not rigidly  determine  higher  attentional,  cognitive,  or aesthetic  
abilities  (e.g., preference).  As pointed  out by Brattico  [31], properties  of auditory  stimuli  (such  as sensory  consonance  
or dissonance)  trigger  early and largely  automatic  low sensorimotor  responses,  while  aesthetic  preferences  rely on 
higher  cognitive  processes,  in which  social,  individual,  and cultural  variables  come  into play. Therefore,  sensory  (or 
perceptual)  discrimination  and aesthetic  judgment  (or appreciation)  apparently  identify  two different,  although  related,

5.4. Preference  and familiarity
Going  deeper  into the dichotomy  between  nature  and culture,  several  concerns  emerge  with respect  to speciﬁc  
variables,  namely  ‘preference’  and ‘familiarity’,  that are being  increasingly  studied  in recent  experimental  protocols  
(e.g., [66,76,122,124,144,167,248]).
Preference  is typically  measured  asking  participants  to evaluate  the attractiveness  of perceived  stimuli  through  
Likert-scales.  However,  such methodology  has limited  sensitivity,  reliability,  and validity  (see e.g., [180]), and is 
demonstrably  affected  by cultural  differences  in interpretation  and use [49]. In fact, literature  showed  that respondents  
styles  may vary with culture,  with participants  more prone  to use extreme  values  of the scale while  others  that tend to 
choose  average  values  (e.g., [100]). These  differences  might  reﬂect  differences  in the anthropological,  ethnic,  and reli-
gious  identity,  which  are extremely  difﬁcult  to grasp  empirically  and are rarely  taken  into account  in the experimental  
protocols  (e.g., the inﬂuence  of Confucianism  in China  vs capitalism/individualism  in north  America).  Moreover,  
preference  has been regarded  as a problematic  concept  to be empirically  investigated  in the aesthetic  domain,  likely  
resulting  from a complex  combination  of several  universal  and individual  factors,  such as aesthetic  pleasure,  aesthetic  
interest,  ease of processing,  affective  mediation,  and familiarity  with the stimuli  (e.g., [85,218]; see also the recent  
paper  by Chen  et al. [48], on “taste  typicality”).
Familiarity  has been measured  in different  ways,  ranging  from the assessment  of participants  musical  expertise  
and cultural  background  through  questionnaires  (e.g., Gold-MSI,  see [153]) to corpus-based  approaches.  The latter 
methods  are based  on the assumption  that listeners  become  familiar  with chords  in proportion  to chords’  frequency  
of occurrence  in the listener’s  musical  culture  (e.g., [91,92]). Therefore,  while  in questionnaires  participants  provide  
information  about  their own musical  expertise  and culture,  the corpus-based  attempts  aim at deriving  the same infor-
mation  via a more objective  and empirically  solid methodology.  However,  both the two methodologies  seemingly  have 
similar  limitations.  The self-report  questionnaires  provide  quite detailed  information  about  the experimental  sample,  
but such subjectively-provided  information  might  be hard to generalize  at the population  level;  by contrast,  the cor-
pus based  measures  provide  reliable  and objective  information  about  the musical  production  speciﬁc  to that cultural  
context,  but can only presume  the information  about  familiarity  of the listeners  enrolled  in the study.  In this respect,  
much  more methodologically  straightforward  would  seem to train participants  to perform  a familiarization  task with 
the stimuli  and then ask participants  to compare  stimuli  from the training  set with novel  stimuli  (e.g., with respect  to 
their consonance  or pleasantness)  (e.g., [146]). Results  obtained  in this way could  then be interpreted  as evidence  that, 
at least, the studied  variables  (e.g., consonance  or pleasantness)  is positively  inﬂuenced  by (mere)  exposure.
Despite  the above  highlighted  methodological  concerns,  preference  and familiarity  represent  key factors  of aes-
thetic  liking,  and thus need to be regarded  as crucial  in the future  investigation  on C/D perception.  However,  much  
effort  is seemingly  needed  to design  novel  protocols  that can measure  those  variables  in a more objective  way, so to 
obtain  clear indications  about  their effective  role in C/D perception.
6. Three  hypotheses  on the origins  of C/D
Over the last decade,  researchers  have tried to interpret  the great diversity  of empirical  ﬁndings,  formulating  alter-
native  theories  that rely on different  approaches  to C/D based  on either  property  of the auditory  system  and acoustic  
signals  or cultural  exposure  and individual  factors.  Additionally,  some  comprehensive  explanations  have been sug-
gested  that are based  on the combination  of bioacoustic  and cultural  factors.  Hereinafter,  we will distinguish  the 
various  accounts  that emerge  from the literature  into three hypotheses,  namely,  vocal  similarity  (6.1), psychocultural  
(6.2), and sensorimotor  hypotheses  (6.3).
6.1. The vocal  similarity  hypothesis
Formulated  by Bowling  and Purves  [26], the vocal  similarity  hypothesis  (VSH)  considers  the attraction  to the har-
monic  stimuli  as fundamental  for the biological  foundation  for consonance.  This hypothesis  is grounded  in critical  
evidence  on audio-vocal  communication  and on the prominent  role of harmonicity  in vocal  communication.  Crucial

for VSH is the fact that most frequent  intervals  in the spectra  of speech  parallel  consonance  ranking,  showing  that 
the intervals  we perceive  as consonant  are speciﬁcally  emphasized  in vocal  spectra  [203]. Together  with other evi-
dence  (e.g., [80]), these ﬁndings  have suggested  the idea that the most plausible  explanation  for consonance  might  
be an evolved  attraction  to the harmonic  series  that characterizes  conspeciﬁc  vocalizations  based  on the biological  
importance  of social  sound  signals  (see also [28]).
The VSH aligns  with most of the reviewed  literature  on psychoacoustic  factors  affecting  C/D (see Section 3) that 
regards  harmonicity  as the most fundamental  factor  at the origin  of consonance.  Harmonicity  is conceived  as inti-
mately  related  to social  communication  due to the natural  link between  harmonic  sounds  and sound  signals  produced  
by animals,  especially  by conspeciﬁcs,  for social  communication  (e.g., by contrast  with environmental  sounds  that 
lack harmonicity).  This perspective,  therefore,  holds  that the auditory  system  evolved  to easily  and quickly  detect  
harmonic  sounds,  as they might  convey  relevant  social  and biological  information  (e.g., sex, age, emotional  state)  that 
attracts  attention  and is therefore  encoded  by neural  circuitry  with special  efﬁciency.  Hence,  VSH is solidly  rooted  
in evolutionary  biology,  evidencing  the role that (psycho)acoustic  features  of C/D (i.e., harmonicity)  have played  
throughout  the evolution  of human  verbal  communication.
Grounding  a musical  phenomenon  such as C/D on nonmusical  factors,  however,  might  raise issues  regarding  the 
explanatory  ability  of VSH.  First, with respect  to the timeframes  that are considered  for evolutionary  changes  to occur,  
the entire  history  of Western  music  is too short and, thus, necessarily  disregarded  in the VSH.  At the same time, VSH 
seems  to perfectly  explain  intonation  only, which  is allegedly  a recent  factor  in the history  of human  vocalization.  
Second,  and relatedly,  focusing  mainly  on the use of voice  for phonetic/linguistic  communication,  VSH neglects  other 
forms  of vocalizations  (e.g., singing,  distress  signals  or cries)  that are crucial  for survival.  Third,  the VSH does not 
make  any empirical  prediction  that can be tested  among  humans.  However,  based  on VSH,  it can be predicted  that 
animal  species  that are capable  of generating  harmonics  in vocal  communication  do develop  some  form of attraction  
to consonants  compared  with dissonant  tones,  although  ﬁndings  from similar  cross-species  studies  would  seemingly  
support  the generality  of VSH rather  than shedding  light on C/D perception  in humans.
6.2. The psychocultural  hypothesis
Given  the limitation  to just intonation  tuning  system,  the VSH has not been considered  a universal  hypothesis  
for C/D perception  (see, e.g., [82]). Alternative  accounts  were then put forward  to widen  the number  of factors  that 
are assumed  to better  explain  and understand  C/D perception  in the Western  tuning  system,  which  is based  on equal  
temperament  (e.g., [92,166]). We refer to the ‘psychocultural  hypothesis’  (PH) as those  accounts  that give importance  
to musical/cultural  factors  (e.g., tuning  systems  and compositional  practices)  beyond  biological  and perceptual  ones 
and, thus, accounting  for the historical  evolution  of musical  language  and practices.
In contrast  to VSH,  PH relies  on a wider  set of explanatory  factors  that are assumed  to have equally  determined  
consonance  perception.  For example,  the difference  between  theoretical  intervals  and how they are typically  played  
in performances;  the further  distinction  between  intonation  in solo vs. accompanied  performances  (either  sung or 
instrumental);  psychological  factors  of interval  size perception  and production;  and the historical  evolution  of musical  
instruments  and practices.  In such a view,  being  “cultural”  means  that intervals  are subject  to learning  and exposure  
effects  and that such effects  are robust  and consistent.  This was clearly  demonstrated  by Loosen  [132], who showed  
that violinists  preferred  something  approaching  Pythagorean  tuning,  pianists  preferred  the 12-equal  division  of the 
octave,  and nonmusicians  had no signiﬁcant  preference.  Similar  ﬁndings  suggest  that tuning  preferences  are primarily  
learned,  and not primarily  determined  by the acoustics  of the sounds  themselves  or by the physiology  of hearing.  
However,  such a hypothesis  risks rigidly  conceiving  the biology  vs. culture  dichotomy  in terms  of innate  vs. acquired  
abilities,  implying  that rules of any musical  traditions  are, at least to some  extent,  arbitrary  (e.g., [45,47]). However,  
as noted  above  (Section 5.3), given  that enculturation  is a biologically  constrained  process  and that auditory  biology  
is shaped  by cultural  experience,  it seems  misleading  to conceive  of the inﬂuence  of culture  as ‘arbitrary’,  as well as 
the discrimination  between  C/D as ‘innate’.
6.3. The sensorimotor  hypothesis
A third hypothesis  stresses  the role of sensorimotor  properties  of vocal  production,  and in particular  singing,  as 
constraining  the allegedly  universal  features  of musical  scales  [198]. Stressing  the role of motor  actions  for tuning

sung intervals,  SH holds  that the origins  of tonality  must be rooted  in the tuning  of melodic  intervals  when  sung 
rather  than in any theorizations  or compositional  practices.  Based  on the evidence  showing  that intervals’  size of sung 
intervals  is larger  than predicted  on the basis of Pythagorean  music  theory  ([172]), SH conceives  intervals  as imprecise  
“islands”  of close frequencies  often overlapping  one another  rather  than as sharp  integer  ratios  based  on abstract  rules.
As PH, SH seems  to recall  Aristoxenus’  anti-intellectualist  emphasis  on singing  and musical  practices  as the only 
solid ground  for any explanations  of consonance.  At the same time, with VSH,  SH draws  attention  to biological  con-
straints.  However,  it also claims  that the mere attraction  toward  harmonicity  is not enough  to explain  why concrete  
practices  of music  have included  more harmonic  intervals.  In fact, this ﬁrstly  requires  humans  to be capable  of pro-
ducing  such intervals.  However,  SH risks overestimating  the impact  of music  practice  on music  theory.  In fact, if, 
on the one hand,  it is likely  that the production  of music  affected  music  theory  and conceptualization  throughout  the 
centuries,15it is also unlikely  that issues  arising  from musical  practice  altered  music  theory;  rather,  they only im-
pacted  musical  practices  themselves.  As an example,  one might  consider  how twentieth-century  music  avant-gardes,  
and much  of contemporary  experimental  music,  dramatically  impacted  music  production,  albeit  having  limited,  or no 
impact  at all, on music  theory.16
7. The vocal  origins  of C/D, auditory  roughness,  and aversion
As Section 6has hopefully  made  clear,  the reviewed  hypotheses  largely  converge  with the identiﬁcation  of vocal-
ization  as a crucial  factor  in the explanation  of consonance.  Both VSH and SH explicitly  refer to vocal  communication  
and vocal  tuning,  respectively,  as determinants  for the evolution  of consonance  perception.  Vo i c e  also plays  a role in 
the PH (see, e.g., [167], analysis  of a database  of vocal  polyphony).  Additionally,  and of note, the importance  of 
vocality  in music  perception  has been recognized  beyond  the melodic/harmonic  domain,  for instance,  in research  
on fundamental  rhythmic  abilities.  In fact, vocality  is key in one of the most accredited  hypotheses  on the origin  of 
auditory-motor  synchronization,  namely,  the vocal  learning  hypothesis  (VLH,  see [169]).
Taken  together,  therefore,  the hypotheses  about  consonance  origins  (i.e., VSH,  SH and PH) and the VLH suggest  
that the perception  of both the fundamental  pillars  of musicality—rhythm  and melody/harmony  perception,—might  
stem from a common  origin,  that is, vocality.  In such a view,  vocality  is primarily  conceived  of as a means  for 
producing  phonetic  vocalizations  (i.e., singing,  see PH and SH), and producing  speech/language  (VSH).  However,  
from a broader  biological  perspective,  nonphonetic  vocalizations  are also crucial  in human  communication,  in which,  
for example,  screaming  and cries are used to warn/attract  conspeciﬁcs  attention.  In what follows,  we therefore  review  
the relevant  literature  showing  that rough  vocalizations  trigger  aversive  reactions  in listeners.
In the repertoire  of human  nonphonetic  vocalization,  screaming  is a primary  way to increase  signal  intensity  to 
amplify  sensory  salience  and guarantee  efﬁcient  reactions  by bystanders  that often lack other situational  information  
about  the caller.  Screams  might  have evolved  initially  for defensive  purposes,  for instance,  to signal  potential  at-
tacks by predators  and, at the same time, to discourage  predators,  soliciting  aid from a conspeciﬁc  ([190]). Analyzing  
the spectro-temporal  proﬁle  of human  screams,  Arnal  and colleagues  [6] found  that screamed  vocalizations  contain  
stronger  temporal  modulations  in the range  of 30–150 Hz.  Interestingly,  such a range  of temporal  modulations,  gen-
erally  considered  irrelevant  for human  communication,  corresponds  to the perceptual  attribute  of roughness,  typically  
characterized  by a coarse,  grating,  or harsh  subjective  experience  ([227]; see [61], for a multisensory  review  on rough-
ness).  The fact that dissonant  sounds  elicit temporal  modulations  in the spectro-temporal  regime  that are also exploited  
to communicate  danger  suggests  that aversion  to dissonance  might  have a biological  origin.  Interestingly,  investigating

Roughness  clearly  plays  a prominent  role in determining  the perceived  quality  of auditory  stimuli  compared  with 
other acoustic  features,  such as loudness  [4,260]. Biological  evidence  supports  this claim,  indicating  a relationship  
between  the physical  structures  of animal  sounds  and the motivation  underlying  their use. As suggested  by Morton  
[152], birds and mammals  use harsh  and rough  sounds  when  hostile  and in “face-to-face”  encounters  with other 
animals.  In contrast,  they tend to produce  pure tone-like  sounds  when  frightened  or being  approached  in a friendly  
manner.  In general,  Morton  observed  that, independent  of the frequency  range,  the greater  the sound’s  harshness,  the 
greater  the aggressive  motivation  and, vice versa,  the more pure-tone  like, the more fearful  or friendly.
Such considerations  might  have evolutionary  roots,  as harsh  and rough  sounds  are associated  with the size of the 
animal  producing  it. In fact, body mass correlates  with vibrating  membranes,  and a more massive  vibrating  membrane  
can more likely  produce  secondary  vibrations  and rough  sounds.17Hence,  roughness  biologically  conveys  information  
about  the size of the signaler,  thus warning  about  the potential  danger  and triggering  defensive  behaviors  (though  see 
[81]). Recent  studies  conﬁrmed  that aggressive  nonverbal  vocalizations  (roars)  are cues for physical  strength  both in 
Western  and non-Western  cultures  (e.g., [112,184]), suggesting  that speciﬁc  nonlinear  phenomena,  namely,  roughness,  
are among  those  that might  predict  strength,  a physical  trait that is associated  with potential  danger  for the listener  in 
the case of aggression.
Studies  on humans  and animals  provide  additional  evidence  on the association  between  roughness  and danger/dis-
tress situations.  For example,  Koutseff  and colleagues  [116]h a v e  shown  that the acoustic  structure  of baby cries 
signiﬁcantly  varies  between  nonpainful  and painful  conditions.  In particular,  they showed  that pain-associated  cries 
contain  higher  levels  of roughness  than nonpainful  cries,  suggesting  that roughness  is a biological  cue to communi-
cate distress  levels.  Relatedly,  an MEG  study  by Young  and colleagues  [251] demonstrated  that the different  acoustic  
proﬁles  of adult and infant  cries induce  different  neural  responses  that allow  the listener  to rapidly  identify  infant  
cries and, promoting  prompt  adaptive  behaviors.  In a large online  study,  Anikin  et al. [5] investigated  the effects  on 
nearly  700 listeners’  perceptions  of three psychoacoustic  (pitch,  timbre,  roughness)  and three ecological  (body  size, 
formidability,  aggression)  characteristics.  The results  conﬁrmed  that nonlinear  vocal  phenomena  lowered  perceived  
voice  pitch,  increased  voice  darkness  and roughness,  and caused  vocalizers  to sound  larger,  more formidable,  and 
more aggressive.  Moreover,  in the absence  of emotional  or social  contextual  information,  auditory  roughness  per se
has been shown  to trigger  defensive  reactions  in humans  with respect  to nonrough  stimuli  (harmonic  tone) [225]. For 
these reasons,  infant-directed  vocalization  across  cultures  tends  to be less rough  than adult-directed  vocalization  [98].
Interestingly,  the association  between  roughness  and danger  is also demonstrated  in ﬁctional  contexts,  such as 
cinema.  Analyzing  horror  movie  soundtracks,  Trevor  and colleagues  [236] found  that their acoustic  proﬁle  exhibits  
similarity  with screams  (i.e., a high level of roughness)  and that they are typically  perceived  as having  a negative  
valence.  Roughness  also appears  to be an aversive  feature  of sounds  in animals  (e.g., in seals [84]). Rumbles  produced  
by African  elephants  in distressful  contexts  exhibited  increased  roughness  with respect  to neutral  and positive  contexts  
[211], and roughness-like  structures  are also present  in vocalizations  emitted  by bats in distressful  contexts  ([93]). 
Many  rodents,  including  shrews,  marmots,  meerkats,  and prairie  dogs,  emit rough  warning  calls when  danger  is 
imminent  (see also [137], for results  on nonlinearities  as stress  indicators  in dog whines).  These  timbrally  “nonlinear”  
calls are capable  of conveying  information  about  both the kind of predator  and its behavior  and information  of obvious  
adaptive  advantage  to the group.18
The reviewed  evidence  allows  us to establish  a solid link between  dissonance  and aversion.  Such a link is mediated  
by roughness,  as rough  auditory  stimuli  signal  potential  danger  or harm,  which  are likely  to be perceived  as unpleasant.

To summarize,  existing  explanations  of C/D perception  converge  on the claim  that phonetic  and non-phonetic  
forms  of vocalizations  play a crucial  role in shaping  C/D perception  (see Fig.1). Auditory  roughness,  that characterize  
dissonant  sounds,  is universally  associated  with distress/danger  and, therefore,  elicits  defensive  behavioral  reactions  
and neural  responses  that indicate  aversion.  On the contrary,  cross-cultural  behavioural  studies  failed  to demonstrate  
the universal  attractive  nature  of consonance,  which  therefore  remains  a somewhat  speculative  and unsupported  claim.
Based  on the reviewed  evidence,  we might,  therefore,  expect  dissonant  sounds  to synchronize  salience-related  net-
works  throughout  superior  temporal  regions,  subcortical  and cortical  limbic  areas,  and the frontal  cortex  (see [6]). 
Moreover,  we might  expect  dissonant  sounds  to evoke  activity  in regions  associated  to processing  of aversive  stim-
uli, such as cortical  regions,  including  the auditory  cortex,  the insula,  and the anterior  cingulate  cortex  (ACC)  and 
subcortical  ones such as the amygdala  (see [90]). In contrast,  we might  only speculate  that consonant  sounds  elicit 
activation  in circuitries  typically  involved  in positive  rewards  (e.g., mesolimbic  striatal  areas,  such as the nucleus  
accumbens  (NAcc),  see [256]f o r a review;  see also Zatorre  et al. 2019).  Similar  neurophysiological  ﬁndings  would  
also provide  additional  data to progress  on the investigation  of the biological  link between  Pythagorean  simple  ratios  
and aesthetic  attraction,  thus relating  to the recently  addressed  question  on the existence  and nature  of allegedly  uni-
versal  crossmodal  or amodal  features  (e.g., symmetry,  harmony,  balance)  that might  trigger  aesthetic  appreciation  in 
humans  [206–208]. Finally,  behavioral  tests on perceived  pleasantness/consonance/harmonicity  or unpleasantness/dis-
sonance/roughness  of stimuli  might  help disentangle  aesthetic  and acoustic  features,  thus avoiding  misinterpreting  
neural  ﬁndings.
8. Future  directions
We conclude  our review  outlining  three potentially  fruitful  lines of inquiry  for future  research  on C/D perception.  
First, we suggest  that novel  insights  into C/D perception  might  result  from experimental  protocols  that actively  involve  
participants,  for instance,  coupling  C/D perception  to simple  nonmusical  motor  and nonmotor  tasks,  thus closing  the 
perception-action  loop that has been recognized  as allegedly  essential  to any form of perception,  including  music  [110,
230]. Second,  exploiting  recent  theories  on multimodal  perception  of music  and on sensory  integration  ([193,216]), 
we suggest  that the investigation  of C/D perception  might  be extended  to sensory  modalities  other than audition,  such 
as, vision  and touch,  to gather  data on the multimodal  and crossmodal  perception  of C/D. Finally,  we indicate  a couple  
of potential  studies  for cross-cultural  research  on C/D.
8.1. Beyond  passive  listening:  closing  the perception-action  loop
To date, in most of the empirical  protocols  designed  to test C/D perception,  adult human  participants  were asked  
to passively  listen  to a wide range  of different  auditory  stimuli  (e.g., harmonic  intervals,  melodies,  short excerpts).  
In some  behavioral  protocols,  participants  were also asked  to evaluate  the stimuli  for certain  aesthetic  features,  such 
as harmoniousness  and pleasantness.  However,  based  on embodied  approaches  to music  perception  and cognition

Toro [55] pioneered  the exploration  of the effect  of C/D on humans  and rats in the extraction  and generalization  of 
abstract  rules.  The results  showed  that, for humans,  the learning  of rules is facilitated  when  they are conveyed  through  
consonant  rather  than dissonant  intervals.  In contrast,  no effects  of C/D were found  on rat learning.  The ﬁndings  
suggest  not only that consonance  is processed  in an easier  and faster  way by the auditory  system  in humans  (see 
Section 4.2) but also that consonance  positively  impacts  higher-level  cognitive  abilities.  An avenue  of research  could  
stem from this study,  with the aim of exploring  the impact  of C/D priming  on different  kinds  of abilities,  such as 
learning,  memorizing,  and reasoning  (see, in this direction,  [8,52,102,125,197,210,217]).

8.3. Cross-cultural  research
Hereinafter,  we ﬁnally  outline  two possible  ways to gather  critical  evidence  on the cross-cultural  perception  of C/D. 
First, it would  certainly  be interesting  to determine  more about  whether  neural  differences  between  the processing  of 
consonant  and dissonant  auditory  stimuli  that have been demonstrated  in Western  listeners  are also observed  in those  
groups  of individuals  where  a preference  for consonance  has not been observed  ([144,209]). Based  on the evidence  
reviewed  here (e.g., [130]), we expect  that similarities  will be shown  (at least)  with regard  to the early processing  
of auditory  stimuli.  Subtler  distinctions  might  be revealed  in those  responses  that were demonstrated  to be related  to 
familiarity  with the stimuli  (e.g., [146]). Naïve  listeners  might  also be tested  with behavioral  protocols  implying  active  
participation,  from sensory  discrimination  to cognitive  tasks.  Second,  longitudinal  protocols  might  be carried  out with 
naïve  listeners  to assess  the effect  of regular  exposure  to Western  music  on participants’  neurophysiological  and 
behavioral  responses,  thus providing  unique  results  on the meaning  of innateness  vs. acquiredness  in C/D perception  
and on related  brain plasticity.
9. Conclusion
C/D are among  the most intriguing  and fascinating  concepts  in music  theory  and perception.  After  their early 
theorization  by Pythagoras,  C/D have been long debated  throughout  the millennia  in Western  culture  by scientists,  
composers,  and music  theorists.  In recent  decades,  the topic has been thoroughly  investigated,  attracting  the attention  
of scholars  from several  disciplines,  including  neurophysiology,  evolutionary  biology,  and systematic  musicology.  
However,  such a huge research  effort  failed  to result  in a coherent  explanatory  framework  for C/D, suggesting  that C/D 
is a multifactorial  concept  that emerges  from the complex  dynamics  among  several  intertwined  factors,  in particular,  
(psycho)acoustics,  biology,  and culture.  Whereas  biological  constraints  and acoustic  features  of sounds  determine  the 
way C/D are discriminated  and processed  by the sensory  system,  culture  provides  the formalization  of C/D into music  
theory  and harmony  and ultimately  inﬂuences  the aesthetic  judgment  and/or  subjective  preference  for C/D. In such a 
process,  musical  practices,  traditions,  and systems  signiﬁcantly  contribute  to shaping  musical  expertise  and familiarity  
(see Table 4).
As our review  attempted  to illustrate,  vocality,  harmonicity,  and roughness  play a fundamental  role in the deﬁnition  
and perception  of C/D. Phonetic  and non-phonetic  vocalizations  emerged  as a key factor  in the explanation  of C/D, 
determining  the universal  aversion  for dissonance  through  roughness  (of both phonetic  and nonphonetic  utterances,  
i.e., screams  and cries)  and allegedly  contributing  to the attraction  for consonances  through  harmonicity  among  the 
Western  listeners.  Additionally,  numbers  have strongly  inﬂuenced  the conceptualization  of C/D since its discovery.
