Introduction
Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a heterogeneous con­
dition in terms of both the phenotypic characteristics and
the severity of behavioral symptoms (Jeste & Geschwind,
2014). Cooccurring conditions are highly common in chil­
dren with ASD, with approximately 60–70% presenting at
least one comorbidity (Hirota & King, 2023) and 48% expe­
riencing two or more co-occurring conditions (Simonoff et
al., 2008). These factors add complexity to the diagnos­
tic process and can pose additional challenges for correct
diagnosis. Currently, the diagnostic process for ASD includes a neu­
rodevelopmental history, clinical evaluations of language,
functionality, intellectual abilities and assessments of educa­
tional or vocational skills. Medical evaluations and the use
of standardized instruments, such as tests and genetic exam­
inations, are also conducted to exclude other neurological

Abstract
Purpose  The goal of this study was to evaluate eye-tracking screening for ASD among 585 typically developing toddlers 7
to 48 months of age in vulnerable districts of São Paulo. Methods  Eye-tracking assessment was done with children in the participating community nurseries on Joint Attention,
composed of the Initiation Joint Attention (IJA) and Responding to Joint Attention (RJA). All parents responded to the
questionnaire on the educational level and socioeconomic family status (SES). Children received ratings on the Childhood
Autism Rating Scale (CARS) by trained psychologists and those above 25 points underwent consultations with a pediatric
neurologist to establish a clinical diagnosis according to DSM-5 criteria. Children were assigned to three groups: TD (typical
development), ASD (autism spectrum) and nTD (impaired development without ASD). The groups were compared regard­
ing the mean gaze time and proportion of transition betweene Areas of Interest (AOIs) on face to target and face to distractor. Results  ASD group spend less time looking to the Face and Target AOIs than other groups (F [3.73, 765.98] = 2.49, p =.04,
η2G = 0.01) and made less transitions (F [2, 411] = 4.33, p <.01, η2G = 0.01). The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC)
Curve of the overall mean gaze was 0.65. Conclusion  This study could identify neurodevelopmental alterations of ASD in a large sample of typically developing chil­
dren. Considering the screening and diagnosis in ASD children before the age of 3 years old, eye tracking offers an important
add-on alternative for early identification. Keywords  Autism spectrum disorder · Eye tracking · Joint attention · Children

high financial costs and the need for specialized training and
expertise of the assessors (Klin, 2015). Furthermore, owing
to the intricate and multifaceted nature of the disorder and
the complexity of the diagnostic process, which requires a
comprehensive, systematic approach based on the work of
a multidisciplinary team, achieving an early diagnosis of
ASD on a broader scale remains a challenge. In this context, a growing number of studies have
adopted an objective eye-tracking method to quantify visual
attention in search of potential biomarkers to aid in the
early detection of ASD (Johnson & Myers, 2007; Magán-
Maganto et al., 2017; Pierce et al., 2016). Eye-tracking tech­
nology captures children’s gaze through a video camera of
an eye-tracking device, either in a naturalistic setup or on
a computer screen, and can be applied from a very young
age. Eye-tracking studies have shed light on the specific
gaze behaviors and fixation patterns exhibited by toddlers
with ASD, primarily during social attention tasks, which
have yielded promising results (Mundy et al., 2018). Among
various tasks, joint attention has been studied specifically in
relation to ASD. Joint attention (JA) is a crucial social cognitive skill that
begins to emerge as early as 5 months of age and continues
to develop. This ability involves sharing attention and focus­
ing on objects and events with others, it also encompasses
the capacity to understand other people’s intentions (Toma­
sello et al., 2005; Mundy et al., 2018). JA plays a significant
role in early communication, language development, and
social interaction. Importantly, its early signs of develop­
ment appear even before the onset of language (Bates et al.,
1975). Individuals with ASD often experience challenges
in their joint attention abilities. Eye-tracking research has
reported results on two aspects of JA in ASD: responding to
joint attention (RJA) and initiating joint attention (IJA). RJA
involves the ability to shift one’s visual attention following
another person’s gaze, whereas IJA refers to the capacity
to capture another person’s attention and direct it toward a
shared experience, such as a moving toy (Mundy & Gomes,
1998). The development of RJA and IJA has been found
to be closely related to social and language abilities, and
children who receive a diagnosis and intervention before the
age of 3 years often show advancements in symbolic play
and communication (Gulsrud et al., 2014). Numerous studies have utilized eye-tracking technology
to examine IJA and RJA, aiming to differentiate between
ASD and typical development. For gaze following in the
RJA condition, between-group differences have been
observed, although these differences appear to depend on
factors such as the participant’s age, the type of stimuli
used, eye-tracking designs, and calculated metrics (see
Anthony et al., 2023). With respect to IJA gaze initiation,
children with ASD tend to decrease their gaze toward the
target object while increasing the cumulative fixation dura­
tion for the nontarget object (Billeci et al., 2017). In toddlers with an elevated likelihood of having ASD,
aged 7 months, gaze following does not differ significantly
from that of typically developing children. However, at the
13-month assessment, children with communication prob­
lems, regardless of their group, may display reduced total
gaze time toward a moving toy. This suggests that reduced
interest in an unexpected object’s behavior and its social
communication relevance may reflect difficulties in under­
standing the communicative relevance of staring (Bedford
et al., 2012). Longitudinal studies support this perspective,
showing that atypicality found in IJA tends to persist and
remain stable throughout development compared with the
differences in RJA (Nyström et al., 2019). The IJA affects
the number of gaze transitions from the actor’s face to the
moving target. While eye tracker measurements in younger
children may not consistently yield statistically significant
between-group results, other forms of IJA, such as point­
ing, may still show impairments in individuals with ASD. Additionally, the number of gaze transitions correlates with
the severity of social symptoms, with a decrease in pointing
and showing behavior at 18 months (Maestro et al., 2001; Muratori et al., 2019; Nyström et al., 2019). Eye tracking has proven to be a valuable tool for assess­
ing cognition in prelinguistic children. An analysis of visual
search and visual attention to naturalistic scenes has dif­
ferentiated children into various age groups. Specifically, it
has been found that 18-months-olds tend to prefer gazing at
faces, whereas 30-month-olds direct their gaze more toward
scenes where something is being observed or touched by
others. This indicates a developing interest in understanding
people’s intentions (Dalrymple et al., 2019). For very young typically developing children, ranging
from 4 to 11 months old, the predominant gaze pattern was
also centered on the eyes, and a positive preference was
observed for looking at humans over geometric figures. Additionally, joint attention skills significantly improve in
infants approximately 10  months old (Fukushima et al.,
2021). In the context of ASD, a promising advancement has
been the validation of the GeoPref Test, a social preference
task that assesses the looking pattern to dynamic social and
geometric stimuli. This test serves as a potential biomarker
for children aged 12–48 months with indications for ASD
diagnosis (Wen et al., 2022). However, despite these promising findings, the incorpo­
ration of eye tracking into routine screening for toddlers is

not yet common practice because of the lack of standard­
ized protocols and diagnostic criteria for the early detection
of children with ASD. While eye tracking is increasingly
employed in research medical facilities for children show­
ing indications of developmental delay, particularly among
those whose parents seek medical assistance, it remains
largely inaccessible to populations without access to pri­
mary health care. One study conducted eye tracking
research at Child Care Centers and showed comparative
results with laboratory data regarding the quality of the eye
tracking data; additionally, it was able to assess a cultur­
ally and economically diverse population, which is usually
not available for Baby laboratory-based studies (Haiko Bal­
lieux et al., 2016). Therefore, well-structured eye tracking
protocols executed at loci could be particularly valuable in
countries where access to medical attention is precarious
and could yield more insightful data on the effects of low
socioeconomic status (SES). This prospective diagnostic study was conducted to assess
eye tracking measures of joint attention (JA), including ini­
tiating joint attention (IJA) and responding to joint attention
(RJA). The aim of this study was to evaluate a large cohort
of young children attending nurseries in low SES districts
and identify early signs of ASD via eye tracking measures. Following the confirmation of the diagnosis through stan­
dard ASD diagnosis procedures, the properties of JA within
the whole sample were compared for the specific ASD gaze
traits that would validate the use of eye tracking as a screen­
ing tool for early predictors of ASD. This study was a part
of a larger project focusing on early intervention in ASD
patients funded by the Ministry of Health (PRONAS/PCD, Brazil) and whose data are being published elsewhere. Materials and Methods
Participants
A total of 784 children between 7 to 48  months of age
were enrolled in this study. The participants were recruited
through the Early Childhood Education Centers of the
Rainha da Paz Association, which operates in nine different
daycare centers and nurseries located in the district Jardim
São Luiz, situated in the southern outskirts of São Paulo, Brazil. This region is characterized by a low Socioeconomic
Status (SES) and densely populated informal settlements
with a population density of 10,845 Hab/km2, in contrast to
the high SES district of Morumbi with 4119 Hab/km2 (Pre­
feitura de São Paulo, n.d.). However, in recent years, sev­
eral SES metrics have improved. One notable improvement
is the reduction in the demand for public and/or affiliated
private daycare and nursery placements, with a reported
attendance rate of 91% (source: ​h​t​t​p​s​:​/​/​w​w​w​.​o​b​s​e​r​v​a​p​r​i​m​e​i​
r​a​i​n​f​a​n​c​i​a​.​o​r​g​.​b​r​/, data from 2018). The Early Childhood ​E​d​
u​c​a​t​i​o​n Centers of Rainha da Paz Association belong among
those organizations that focus on providing daycare services
for infants. In the initial phase of the project, a brief interview was
conducted with parents and/or teachers to assess the exclu­
sion criteria, which included the following: (a) any previ­
ously diagnosed neurological syndromes or neurological
signs; (b) head injury or epilepsy; and (c) vision, motor or
hearing impairments that were uncorrected and/or incom­
patible with eye tracking. The parents and/or legal guard­
ians of children who met the inclusion criteria were invited
to participate in the study and were provided with the
informed consent form, which was previously approved
by the research Ethics Committee of the José Luiz Egydio
Setúbal Foundation (CAAE: 06684819.1.0000.5567) in
accordance with the guidelines and regulations outlined in
the Declaration of Helsinki. Owing to the absence of signa­
tures together with children who were transferred or with­
drawn from the school during the project, the remaining
sample consisted of 676 children (see Flowchart, Fig. 1, for
further details). In the second phase of the study, parents and/or legal
guardians completed questionnaires related to their family’s
SES status. Trained psychologists assessed each child using
the Childhood Autism Rating Scale (CARS). For children
with high scores on this assessment, psychologists collected
additional information on the child’s behavior from parents
and school caretakers. They also observed the children to
assess their behavior in the daily routine of the education
center, including activities in the classroom, during meal­
times and during playtime in the park. During this phase of the project, all children underwent
eye-tracking assessments conducted by independent asses­
sors who were unaware of the CARS results. Therefore,
both CARS and eye-tracking assessments were performed
in a blinded manner on children initially considered typi­
cally developing. In the third phase of the project, children who scored
above 25 points on the CARS scale underwent consultations
with a pediatric neurologist to establish a clinical diagnosis
of ASD in accordance with the DSM-5 criteria (American
Psychiatric Association, 2013). The CARS scale’s total
score ranges from 15 to 60 points, and while the typical
CARS cutoff is set at 30 points, some studies suggest that
a score between 25 and 30 may indicate an increased risk
of autism, particularly in milder cases (Mayes et al., 2012; Chlebowski et al., 2010).

Each stimulus category was presented with 6 trials for
the RJA, 6 for the IJA, and 3 for the filler. Prior to the onset
of each trial, a visual and sound stimulus was displayed in
the center of the screen until the child’s gaze was focused on
it for at least 100 ms. This was done to ensure that the start­
ing position of the eyes was standardized before the stimu­
lus onset. Additionally, a red cross was superimposed on a
black background in the forehead region of the actresses
before the start of the experimental phase. The stimuli are
depicted in Fig. 2. Areas of interest (AOIs) were defined on the actress’
face, the target toys, the distractor toy and the background. Fixations occurring outside these predefined areas were
excluded from analysis. To evaluate participants’ engage­
ment with each AOI, the following variables were recorded:
the mean gaze duration on AOIs and the number of gaze
transitions between different AOIs. Importantly, only the
experimental phases of the IJA and RJA were subjected to
analysis. Socioeconomic Status (SES)
In the second phase of the study, parents and/or legal guard­
ians received questionnaires with inquiries about their age,
educational level and socioeconomic family status (SES). SES was evaluated via the ABEP scale, a standard tool for
assessment in Brazil, developed and periodically validated
by the Brazilian Association of Research in Commerce
(Associação Brasileira de Empresas de Pesquisa—ABEP,
2022). The scale provides a multidimensional assess­
ment, considering estimated income, access to goods and
services, and family educational status. For reference, the
mean monthly family income for ABEP SES classification,
which is based on the currency rate as of September 2023,
was as follows: (1) Low SES (E and D classes): $183.2; (2)
Middle SES (C1/2 and B1/2 classes): $340.0 to $2108.1 and
(3) High SES (A class): $4440.7 (ABEP; www.abep.org). Additional information about the mother’s age and educa­
tion level was also collected. Procedure
Children’s eye movements were recorded by a Tobii Pro
Fusion (Tobii AB, Danderyd, Sweden) with a sample rate
frequency of 120 Hz. The eye tracker was positioned below
a 22-inch flat screen monitor, where stimuli were presented
using Tobii Pro Lab (Tobii AB, Danderyd, Sweden). The
both eye infrared light off the cornea and the pupil of each
subject were recorded. Each child was positioned approxi­
mately 50 cm in front of the computer screen while sitting
on a teacher’s lap or in a chair. The distance from the screen
and the angle of inclination of the system were adjusted for
each child to obtain the best tracking of their eyes. Before
the procedures were started, a random five-point calibration
sequence was performed, with the use of a sound for each
calibration point to increase the children’s attention to the
screen. The calibration was repeated in case of target devia­
tion. The data were extracted for offline analysis with the
default setting of I-VT fixation. The data acquisition was performed in a dedicated room
in each of the participating nurseries following a similar
setup regarding the room luminance, doors shut for noise
reduction, position of the child, position and role of each
researcher and the before acquisition familiarization time
for each child. Joint Attention and Sociodemographic Assessment
Joint attention was assessed via blocks of three types: Ini­

one of the toys positioned on both sides of the actor. In the

Approximately 16% of the sample had missing demographic
data. This was primarily due to incomplete or incorrect reg­
istration of one or more items of the questionnaire. These
cases were not included in the analyses because partial data
did not permit an accurate estimation of SES. The means
for the demographic variables are depicted in Table 2. There
were no significant differences among the groups for any
of the variables, except for the CARS score, which was as
expected since the score was used to assign the children to
the TD group. In order to assess the potential correlation between the
CARS score and demographic variables, the Spearman test
was used. Low correlations were found between CARS

(rho = 0.08), and between CARS and maternal education

of maternal education to overall family SES as measured

showed a specific pattern of eye movement in the joint

reduced attention to the actor’s face and diminished transi­
tions from face to target, which differentiated children with
ASD from typically developing children and children with
other developmental impairments. The feasibility of using eye tracking for early assess­
