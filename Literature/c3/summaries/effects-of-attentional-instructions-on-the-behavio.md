Abstract
The present study investigated how instructions for paying attention to auditory feedback may 
affect speech error detection and sensorimotor control. Electroencephalography (EEG) and speech 
signals were recorded from 21 neurologically intact adult subjects while they produced the speech 
vowel sound /a/ and received randomized ±100 cents pitch-shift alterations in their real-time 
auditory feedback. Subjects were instructed to pay attention to their auditory feedback and press 
a button to indicate whether they detected a pitch-shift stimulus during trials. Data for this group 
was compared with 22 matched subjects who completed the same speech task under altered 
auditory feedback condition without attentional instructions. Results revealed a significantly 
smaller magnitude of speech compensations in the attentional-instruction vs. no-instruction group 
and a positive linear association between the magnitude of compensations and P2 event-related 
potential (ERP) amplitudes. In addition, we found that the amplitude of P2 ERP component 
was significantly larger in the attentional-instruction vs. no-instruction group. Source localization 
analysis showed that this effect was accounted for by significantly stronger neural activities in 
the right hemisphere insula, precentral gyrus, postcentral gyrus, transverse temporal gyrus, and 
superior temporal gyrus in the attentional-instruction group. These findings suggest that attentional 
instructions may enhance speech auditory feedback error detection, and subsequently improve

1. Introduction
1.1. The role of auditory feedback in speech control
The unique ability to produce speech is supported by sensorimotor mechanisms that enable 
humans to monitor, detect, and correct for their production errors for effective verbal 
communication (Hickok et al., 2011). Auditory feedback provides an important source of 
sensory input that is used to guide fluent speech (Guenther, 2016). The critical involvement 
of the auditory system in speech error detection and correction is supported by evidence 
from previous studies showing that the vocal motor system produces a compensatory 
response that changes speech fundamental frequency fo in the opposite direction of 
perceived pitch-shift alterations in the online auditory feedback (Burnett et al., 1998; JL, 
1981; Larson, 1998). Speech compensation responses are also produced by changing the 
position of articulatory muscles to oppose online alterations in the formant frequencies of 
auditory feedback (Cai et al., 2011). Under delayed auditory feedback (DAF) conditions, 
speech production is disrupted by the reduced rate, increased intensity, increased fo, and 
exaggerated (e.g., stutter-like) repetitions that occur to overcome the effect of feedback 
delays (Fairbanks, 1954; Yates, 1963). In addition, according to the Lombard effect, speech 
loudness increases in the presence of background noise (Lane and Tranel, 1971), and the 
amplification of auditory feedback leads to compensatory increases in speech loudness 
outputs (Chang-Yit et al., 1975).
According to the contemporary models of speech, the aforementioned behavioral effects 
in response to altered auditory feedback (AAF) are indicative of reciprocal interactions 
between audio-vocal brain networks implicated in the integration of sensory and motor 
processes during speech (Guenther, 2016; Hickok, 2012; Hickok et al., 2011, 2023; Hickok 
& Poeppel, 2000, 2007; Houde and Chang, 2015; Houde & Nagarajan, 2011). In this 
context, the activation of motor commands and their efference copy representations during 
speaking results in the generation of sensory predictions that modulate feedback processing 
in the auditory system via an internal forward model. The presence of a sensory prediction 
mechanism enables selective suppression of neural responses of the auditory cortex to 
normal speech feedback in comparison to listening to the playback of self-produced speech 
sounds (Behroozmand and Larson, 2011; Flinker et al., 2010; Heinks-Maldonado et al., 
2005). This effect, known as speaking-induced suppression (SIS), results from the central 
cancellation of neural representations associated with predicted auditory feedback derived 
from the efference copy of intended speech motor commands compared with the actual 
sensory consequences of produced speech. By selectively attenuating the neural response

to self-generated feedback, the brain can distinguish it from external auditory stimuli, 
facilitating accurate perception and processing of own speech. Thus, sensory predictions 
play a crucial role in differentiating self-generated speech from external sounds, ensuring 
efficient and accurate speech production and perception. In addition, evidence demonstrating 
neural enhancement of responses to auditory feedback alterations (i.e., errors) during 
speaking vs. listening tasks suggests that a predictive mechanism is crucial for error 
detection and subsequent corrective motor reactions (Behroozmand et al., 2009; Chang 
et al., 2013; Greenlee et al., 2013). Speaking-induced enhancement of neural responses 
to altered feedback signifies increased sensitivity to deviations between predicted and 
actual sensory representations. This heightened neural responsiveness enables the brain to 
efficiently detect and recognize errors in speech production (Behroozmand et al., 2009; 
Behroozmand and Larson, 2011; Chang et al., 2013; Flinker et al., 2010; Greenlee et al., 
2013; Heinks-Maldonado et al., 2005). Accordingly, the discrepancy between predicted and 
actual feedback plays a crucial role in driving compensatory behavior for speech motor 
control. When the brain detects a mismatch between the expected and actual feedback, 
it triggers corrective (i.e., compensatory) responses to reduce the disparity and improve 
speech accuracy. This entails real-time adjustments in motor commands that drive vocal 
or articulatory movements during speech production. The interplay between feedback error 
detection and the subsequent compensatory behavior lies in the pivotal role of comparing 
the internally predicted sensory representations with actual feedback for monitoring and 
adjustment processes that drive corrective actions to maintain the desired speech output.
1.2. Attentional modulation of speech sensorimotor processing
Despite significant advancements in our understanding of theoretical and neuro-
computational models of speech, the effects of higher-level cognitive processes (e.g., 
attention) on these mechanisms remains insufficiently investigated. Recent studies have 
revealed the instructional effects of divided attention on sensorimotor mechanisms of speech 
auditory feedback control during the processing of irrelevant visual stimuli (Liu et al., 
2015, 2018; Scheerer et al., 2016; Tumber et al., 2014). In two studies (Scheerer et al., 
2016; Tumber et al., 2014), subjects performed a rapid serial visual processing (RSVP) 
task while receiving pitch-shift alterations in their online auditory feedback during speech 
vowel vocalizations, while a different study (Liu et al., 2015) utilized a number of flash 
lights as the attention-dividing visual stimuli during the same speech AAF tasks. Despite 
existing differences in the implementation of divided-attention experimental paradigms (e.g., 
types and ranging complexity of visual stimuli), results of these studies have consistently 
reported a reduced magnitude of compensatory responses to pitch-shifted auditory feedback 
alterations when subjects were instructed to pay attention to competing visual cues (e.g., 
letters or flashing lights) during steady speech vowel production (Liu et al., 2015; Scheerer 
et al., 2016; Tumber et al., 2014).
In addition, Liu et al. (2018) conducted a follow-up study in which the load of attention-
dividing stimuli was systematically manipulated via changing the presentation rate of 
irrelevant flash light visual cues during the same speech AAF tasks. Results of this 
study showed that subjects produced larger speech compensatory responses to pitch-shifted 
auditory feedback during the high vs. low attentional-load condition. This latter finding

was rather paradoxical in that one would anticipate smaller speech compensations in the 
high-load condition where there is increased demand on attentional resources for processing 
the irrelevant visual cue stimuli. As discussed by Liu and colleagues (Liu et al., 2018), one 
possible account of the observed effect is the potential interaction between working memory, 
divided attention, and sensorimotor mechanisms of speech auditory feedback control. In 
this context, it has been proposed that the increased demand for the allocation of resources 
to working memory for storing sensory stimuli during a more complex divided-attention 
task may increase the overall neural capacity for the sensorimotor mechanisms implicated 
in speech error detection and motor control (Liu et al., 2018). While our understanding 
about the nature of interplay between such mechanisms remain mostly speculative, findings 
of these previous studies corroborate the notion that attentional instructions influence the 
underlying sensorimotor mechanisms involved in speech auditory feedback control.
The contribution to the understanding of the potential influence of higher-level cognitive 
mechanisms (e.g., attention) on speech function has important implications for developing 
new theoretical perspectives and advancing clinical approaches for targeted diagnosis and 
treatment of communication disabilities in individuals with neurological disorders. In the 
present study, we aimed to address this need by designing an experimental paradigm to 
probe the effects of attentional instructions on the behavioral and neural mechanisms of 
speech auditory feedback control. This paradigm differed from the ones used in previous 
studies in that it involved instructing subjects to pay attention to relevant pitch-shift 
alterations in their real-time auditory feedback within the single-modality auditory system 
instead of dividing attention between relevant and irrelevant stimuli in the auditory and 
visual modalities, respectively. Therefore, our paradigm provided a unified framework for 
examining the effects of attentional instructions on speech auditory feedback control while 
ruling out the inherent variability associated with the presentation of irrelevant visual cues 
during speech production tasks. This approach was motivated by evidence from previous 
studies showing that selective attention improves speech perception under normal and 
adverse listening conditions via enhancing the ability to track the fine acoustic structures 
of presented auditory stimuli (Lambez et al., 2020; Lesenfants and Francart, 2020; Rimmele 
et al., 2015; Saltzman et al., 2021; Tóth et al., 2020). In addition, it has been demonstrated 
that such improvements in speech perception abilities are also present in clinical populations 
such as those with autism spectrum and developmental language disorders (Emmons et al., 
2022; Shafer et al., 2007; Soskey et al., 2017). Moreover, other studies have shown that the 
external focus of attention on acoustic goals can facilitate efficient and automatic control of 
speech movements (Lisman and Sadagopan, 2013), and active attention to auditory stimuli 
elicits stronger neural activities as indexed by the increased amplitude of event-related 
potentials (ERPs) (Hink and Hillyard, 1976; Stevens et al., 2006), as well as increased 
hemodynamic responses in cortical auditory networks (Ahveninen et al., 2006; Johnson and 
Zatorre, 2006; Sabri et al., 2008).
1.3. Study goals and hypotheses
In the present study, we used an experimental paradigm in which subjects in one group 
were explicitly instructed to pay attention to and detect pitch-shift alterations in their 
online speech auditory feedback while no such instructions were provided to the other

(i.e., control) group. This experimental design allowed us to study the effects of attentional 
instructions on the behavioral and neural correlates of speech error correction and motor 
control in response to pitch-shifted auditory feedback alterations. As evidenced by previous 
studies, we hypothesized that cortical neural responses to AAF, as indexed by ERPs, would 
exhibit stronger modulations in the group that received attentional instructions compared 
with the no-instruction group (Getzmann et al., 2016; Hink and Hillyard, 1976; Stevens et 
al., 2006). In addition, we hypothesized that there would be a direct relationship between 
the ERP and behavioral compensation responses to AAF with stronger associations for 
the attentional-instruction group. This hypothesis was motivated by the evidence that ERP 
components reflect how strongly the sensorimotor system responds to auditory feedback 
errors during speech production (Houde and Chang, 2015). Moreover, as indicated by 
previous data showing that the ability to correct for speech errors is diminished during 
divided attention (Liu et al., 2015, 2018; Scheerer et al., 2016; Tumber et al., 2014), 
we hypothesized that attentional instructions would result in modulation of speech AAF 
compensation behavior. Finally, we hypothesized to observe a stronger modulation of 
neural activities in sensorimotor brain networks implicated in speech auditory control in 
the attentional-instruction compared with no-instruction group. This latter hypothesis was 
supported by previous findings showing that attended stimuli elicit stronger brain activity in 
cortical auditory regions compared with non-attended tasks (Ahveninen et al., 2006; Johnson 
and Zatorre, 2006; Sabri et al., 2008).
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Subjects
Two independent samples of 21 (14 females; age range: 52–74 yrs; mean age: 62 yrs) and 23 
subjects (17 females; age range: 55–87 yrs; mean age: 64 yrs) were recruited and randomly 
assigned to the attentional-instruction and no-instruction groups, respectively. Subjects in 
both groups had no history of speech, language, hearing, or neurological disorders and were 
recruited from the greater Columbia, SC area through flyers, social media, and word-of-
mouth. All subjects passed a binaural hearing screening at 40 dB threshold for frequencies at 
250, 500, 1000, and 2000 Hz. The choice of study sample size was motivated by previously 
published data in a similar group indicating adequate statistical power at 1-β > 0.80 while 
controlling for α < 0.05 for examining the speech compensation magnitude (Cohen’s d = 
1.82, tcrit(20) = 2.086, p < 0.05, 1-β = 1.0) as well as neurophysiological ERP responses 
to auditory feedback pitch-shift stimuli (Cohen’s d = 0.89, t crit(20) = 2.086, p < 0.05, 1-β 
= 0.97) (Behroozmand et al., 2022). Informed consent was obtained from all subjects, and 
they were compensated for their participation time. Research protocol was approved by the 
University of South Carolina Institutional Review Board.
2.2. Experimental paradigm
The experiments were conducted in a sound-attenuated booth in which subjects’ 
electroencephalography (EEG) and speech signals were recorded while performing speech 
production tasks under the AAF paradigm. For the AAF paradigm, subjects were instructed 
to follow the picture of a human face visual cue on the computer screen and produce 
steady vocalizations of the speech vowel sound /a/ for approximately 2–3 seconds at

their conversational pitch and loudness levels (Fig. 1). Following each vocalization trial, 
a listening trial was implemented and cued by presenting the picture of a human ear 
on the screen while subjects silently listened to the playback of their own vocalization 
recorded in the previous trial. During both vocalization and listening trials, a brief (200 
ms) pitch-shift stimulus at ±100 cents (100 cents = 1 semitone) magnitude was delivered 
to randomly alter the auditory feedback in upward or downward directions at randomized 
times between 750 and 1250 ms after the onset of vocalizations. In music theory, a semitone 
represents the smallest perceptible difference in pitch between two adjacent notes in the 
Western musical scale. In addition, control trials with no pitch-shift stimuli were randomly 
interleaved between AAF trials during both vocalization and listening tasks. This approach 
was implemented to prevent subjects from anticipating altered and unaltered trials during the 
experiment. Subjects in the attentional-instruction group were explicitly instructed to focus 
their attention on their auditory feedback and press a green button (Yes) if they detected 
an upward or downward pitch shift and press a red button (No) to indicate the absence of 
pitch-shift stimuli (i.e., control). Subjects performed the button press task approximately 
4–5 seconds after the completion of each vocalization or listening trial, separately. For 
the no-instruction group, subjects were only asked to follow the visual cues to perform 
speech vowel vocalization and listening without any additional attentional instructions for 
pitch-shift detection and button press tasks. Training was given to both groups prior to the 
start of data collection to ensure that subjects understood the tasks and were able to perform 
them correctly. A total of 210 vocalization and 210 listening trials were collected for each 
subject, with each task comprising randomized presentations of upward and downward 
pitch-shift stimuli, as well as no-pitch-shift (control) trials. This resulted in 70 trials per 
stimulus type for both vocalization and listening tasks, separately. As motivated by our 
specific hypotheses, in the present study, we only analyzed the data for vocalization trials to 
investigate the effect of attentional instructions on the neural and behavioral compensatory 
responses to auditory feedback alterations during speech vowel sound production.
2.3. Speech data acquisition and analysis
The subjects’ speech signals were recorded by a head-mount AKG condenser microphone 
(model C520), amplified by a Motu Ultralite-MK3, and recorded on a laboratory computer 
at 44.1 kHz sampling rate. A custom-design program in Max/Msp (Cycling 74, v.5.0) was 
used to control an Eventide Eclipse Harmonizer for delivering randomized upward (+100 
cents) and downward (−100 cents) pitch-shift stimuli to speech vowel vocalizations. The 
online auditory feedback signal was delivered to subjects’ ears via Etymotic insert earphones 
(models ER1–14 A). The Max/Msp program controlled various aspects of visual cues 
and pitch-shifting stimuli (e.g., duration, direction, amplitude, and timing), and generated 
transistor-transistor-logic (TTL) pulses to mark the onset of each event during speaking 
and button press tasks for synchronization with concurrently recorded EEG signals. The 
behavioral measures of subjects’ speech compensatory response to upward and downward 
pitch-shift stimuli were calculated using the following procedures: First, the Praat software 
(Boersma and Weenink, 2009) was used to extract the pitch frequency contours of the 
recorded speech signals, and data were then exported to MATLAB to segment the extracted 
contours into epochs from −100 ms before to 500 ms after the onset of AAF pitch-shift 
stimuli. For each trial, the magnitude of speech compensation responses was calculated

2.4. EEG data acquisition and analysis
EEG signals were concurrently recorded during the speech AAF tasks in both groups 
from 64 scalp electrodes following a standard 10–10 montage using the BrainVision active 
electrode system (Brain Products GmbH, Germany). The PyCorder software integrated with 
the BrainVision actiCHamp amplifier was used to record EEG signals at 1 kHz sampling 
rate after applying a low-pass anti-aliasing filter with 200 Hz cut-off frequency. Electrode 
impedances were kept below 5 kΩ for all channels. The EEGLAB toolbox (Delorme and 
Makeig, 2004) was used to interpolate artifactual data from noisy channels affected by 
high impedance or defected electrodes, and to filter the EEG signals at frequency ranges

between 1 and 30 Hz (−24 dB/oct). The Independent Component Analysis (ICA) was 
applied to remove eye movement, blinks, muscle, and line noise artifacts. Following these 
pre-processing steps, the EEG signals were segmented into epochs ranging from −200 
ms before to 500 ms after the onset of the pitch-shift stimuli, and baseline correction 
was performed by removing the mean amplitude of the pre-stimulus neural activities in 
a time window from −200 to 0 ms on a trial-by-trial basis for each channel. The choice 
of the baseline was based on visual inspection to identify the pre-stimulus time window 
within which the evoked neural activities were relatively stable. Individual event-related 
potentials (ERPs) were calculated by averaging the pre-processed EEG data for each subject 
across at least 100 trials combined across upward and downward pitch shifts (minimum 
50 trials for each stimulus direction). In our analysis, we primarily focused on examining 
the effect of attentional instructions on the underlying mechanisms of speech feedback 
control irrespective of directional effects associated with pitch-shift stimuli. Therefore, for 
both the magnitude of speech compensation and amplitude of ERPs, responses to upward 
and downward pitch-shift directions were combined for statistical analysis. This allowed 
us to maintain statistical power by minimizing the number of factors and focusing on 
those with higher priorities relevant to our study questions and hypotheses. In addition, 
this approach was critical to provide a holistic account of the observed effects and allowed 
us to consistently (and concurrently) examine the neural correlates of behavioral effects 
using ERP by averaging data over a larger number of trials (i.e., minimum 100 trials per 
condition for each group, important for enhancing signal-to-noise ratio for ERP analysis). 
The grand-average measures of neural activity in response to pitch-shift stimuli were 
calculated by averaging individual ERP responses for subjects in the attentional-instruction 
vs. no-instruction group, separately. However, since ERPs are only elicited in response to a 
stimulus, we did not measure neural responses to no-alteration trials where there were no 
pitch shifts in the auditory feedback during speech vowel vocalizations.
The amplitude of the N1 and P2 components was measured at 130–170 ms and 230–350 
ms in response to pitch-shift AAF stimuli, respectively. The choice of the ERP components 
was motivated by evidence supporting the notion that the N1 and P2 responses reflect 
how strongly the speech system responds to auditory prediction errors during the AAF 
experimental paradigm (Houde and Chang, 2015). The amplitude of N1 and P2 ERP 
responses were calculated by averaging neural activities across 22 fronto-central electrodes 
in each subject (FP1, FP2, AF7, AF3, AFz, AF4, AF8, F3, F1, Fz, F2, F4, FC3, FC1, FCz, 
FC2, FC4, C3, C1, Cz, C2, and C4). The choice of electrodes was based on visual inspection 
of topographical maps to identify areas showing the most prominent distribution of neural 
activities for the two ERP components.
2.5. Statistical analysis of speech compensation and ERP responses
We used an analysis of covariance (ANCOV A) model in SPSS (v.28) to examine the 
effect of attentional instructions on speech compensation magnitude, and to determine the 
association between speech compensation magnitude and neural activity as indexed by the 
ERP components. In this ANCOV A model, we examined the effect of group (attentional-
instruction vs. no-instruction) as the independent variable on the magnitude of vocal 
compensation as the dependent variable while controlling for the variability accounted for

respectively. In addition, εi represents the residuals between the predicted value by the model

coefficient of determination (R-squared) was utilized as a measure to assess the strength 
of relationship between speech compensation magnitude and ERP neural activity responses 
in the attentional-instruction and no-instruction groups. Normality and homogeneity of 
variance of residuals assumptions were examined using the Shapiro-Wilk and Breusch-
Pagan tests, respectively. Violation of the homogeneity assumption was detected for speech 
compensation responses, and therefore, a square-root transformation was applied to the 
dependent variable. The square-root transformation was a special case of the Box-Cox 
transformation with λ= 0.5 (Box and Cox, 1964), which did not change the monotonicity 
of data. Then the Shapiro-Wilk and Breusch-Pagan tests were performed again to confirm 
that the fits did not deviate from model assumptions. These procedures were conducted 
to minimize the potential impact of outliers and to ensure the overall robustness of the 
statistical model and the validity of outcome results. In addition, a two-sample independent 
t-test analysis was conducted to examine the effect of attentional instructions on neural 
activities as reflected by the amplitude of N1 and P2 ERP components. Effect size was 
estimated using Cohen’s d measure while controlling for Type-I error at α < 0.05.
2.6. ERP source localization
Modulation of cortical neural activity sources for altered auditory feedback in different 
groups (attentional-instruction vs. no-instruction) was determined based on cortical 
distribution differences in ERP current source densities extracted using the sLORETA 
algorithm. sLORETA solves the inverse problem of EEG source localization by generating 
a reference-free and polarity-independent single linear solution based on the linearly 
weighted sum of all scalp electrode potentials in the montage (Pascual-Marqui, 2002). For 
consistency, we extracted the profiles of the most salient neural activities averaged within 
the same time window at 130–170 ms and 230–350 ms to estimate the cortical sources of 
N1 and P2 ERP components for subjects in the attentional-instruction and no-instruction 
groups, respectively. Previous studies have validated the accuracy of the sLORETA source 
localization algorithm with the fMRI (Mulert et al., 2004), PET (Pizzagalli et al., 2004) 
and intracranial ECoG recordings (Zumsteg et al., 2006). For each subject, voxel-based 
sLORETA images were generated using realistic normalized head phantoms and Montreal 
Neurological Institute’s standardized brain templates (MNI152 templates), and the 3D

4. Discussion
In this study, we used an AAF experimental paradigm to examine the effects of attentional 
instructions on the underlying behavioral and neural mechanisms of speech auditory 
feedback control in response to pitch-shift stimuli. Our data revealed stronger P2 ERP 
neural responses to speech auditory feedback alterations in the attentional-instruction as 
compared with no-instruction group. This finding is in support of our hypothesis and aligns 
with previous reports on the modulatory effects of attentional instructions, where attended 
stimuli were shown to elicit stronger auditory neural activities during speech production 
and comprehension tasks (Getzmann et al., 2016; Hink and Hillyard, 1976; Rimmele et al., 
2011; Stevens et al., 2006). Moreover, previous evidence has indicated that the amplitude of 
P2 ERPs is systematically decreased under conditions in which attentional instructions are 
implemented to increasingly divided neural resources towards concurrent and yet irrelevant 
AAF stimuli during speech production (Liu et al., 2018).

For tasks involving speech feedback control, the P2 ERP component has been proposed to 
represent higher-level sensorimotor neural processes implicated in auditory error detection 
and correction (Houde and Chang, 2015). Consistent with findings from a previous study 
(Scheerer et al., 2013), our data support this proposal by showing a significant positive 
relationship between the amplitude of P2 ERP neural activities and the magnitude of 
speech compensation in response to pitch-shifted auditory feedback in both the attentional-
instruction and no-instruction groups. In this context, the P2 component has been suggested 
to receive contributions from higher-level cortical auditory-motor networks within the 
posterior peri-Sylvian regions (Crowley and Colrain, 2004; Hari et al., 1990), including 
the posterior superior temporal sulcus (pSTS) and area Spt at the temporo-parietal boundary, 
which serve as a potential neural interface for speech sensorimotor integration (Hickok et 
al., 2009). Converging evidence from several studies has supported this view by showing 
that the top-down effects associated with speaking-induced modulation of neural activities in 
response to AAF stimuli are predominantly observed in components that emerge at longer 
latencies overlapping with P2 ERPs (Behroozmand et al., 2009; Chang et al., 2013; Kort 
et al., 2014). Accordingly, such integrative processes are hypothesized to play a key role in 
comparing the efference-based sensory representations of the intended speech output with 
their actual auditory feedback for sensory prediction error processing (Houde and Chang, 
2015). In addition, our data suggests that P2 responses may also represent the neural state of 
the internal model that translates auditory prediction errors into corrective motor commands 
for speech compensation. However, while this is a plausible interpretation, the relative 
contribution of the underlying sensory error detection and motor correction mechanisms 
to P2 ERPs are not clearly understood. This is partly due to the fact that prediction 
errors driving speech compensations could not be directly quantified in the present study. 
Therefore, the above arguments should be considered with caution while interpreting the 
observed effects in the context of our hypotheses.
Based on the observed between-group effects, we suggest that our data provide preliminary 
evidence that attentional instructions can improve the mechanisms of speech error detection 
and sensorimotor control. This notion is supported by findings of the present study 
showing that the fronto-central P2 ERPs in response to AAF stimuli were elicited with 
significantly stronger amplitudes in the group that received corresponding attentional 
instructions compared with their no-instruction counterparts. This effect reflects a complex 
interplay between neural processes implicated in the allocation of attentional resources and 
those involved in speech auditory feedback control. Although a mechanistic account of 
cross-modal interactions between these systems remains elusive, we argue that attentional 
instructions may consequently induce top-down modulatory effects that selectively enhance 
auditory neural sensitivity for detecting mismatches (i.e., errors) between the predicted and 
actual sensory representations of intended speech. Supporting evidence for this notion is 
provided by recent studies in humans showing that top-down activation of frontal attentional 
networks can selectively modulate the tuning properties of neurons in the auditory cortex 
(Chou and Sen, 2021; Lesenfants and Francart, 2020; Mesgarani and Chang, 2012; Wikman 
et al., 2021). This effect may consequently result in the refinement of sensorimotor 
transformations, thereby aiding the optimization of corrective motor behavior in response 
to speech auditory feedback error signals.

Our findings revealed that while the P2 component of neural responses to AAF was 
enhanced in the attentional-instruction group, the magnitude of speech compensation was 
significantly reduced in these subjects compared with the no-instruction group. This rather 
counter-intuitive finding provides new insights into the potential effects of attentional 
instructions on speech production and motor control. In the following sections, we 
provide several interpretations that may potentially account for the observed effects in 
our study. Firstly, we propose a model that incorporates attentional instructions as a gain-
modulating factor in speech sensorimotor integration mechanisms (Fig. 5). According to 
this model, the observed increases in P2 neural activities and localization of corresponding 
sources to the right auditory (i.e., STG and TTS) and somatosensory (i.e., post-central 
gyrus) cortical networks provides indication for enhanced sensory processing of speech 
feedback errors in the attentional-instruction compared with no-instruction group. This 
effect can in part be accounted for by a greater involvement of higher-level cognitive 
mechanisms (e.g., attention) in the former group, leading to enhanced neural processing 
of auditory and somatosensory feedback changes arising from pitch shifts and subsequent 
compensatory movement of laryngeal muscles for speech motor control, respectively (Chou 
and Sen, 2021; Lesenfants and Francart, 2020; Mesgarani and Chang, 2012; Wikman et al., 
2021). Consequently, increased neural activities within the right post-central gyrus in the 
attentional-instruction group may account for a larger error signal represented in the motor 
system. This effect may in turn result in an adaptive modulation of compensation gain for 
generating corrective motor commands in response to feedback errors. As supported by our 
data, we suggest that the compensation gain follows a Gaussian distribution function (see 
yellow curve in Fig. 5), which assigns smaller gains to larger deviations (i.e. errors) from 
the intended speech output, as determined by the efference copies of motor commands. 
Converging evidence from previous studies support this notion by showing that speakers 
generate larger compensatory responses for smaller changes in their auditory feedback 
(Behroozmand et al., 2009; Liu and Larson, 2008). For example, Behroozmand et al.’s 
study (Behroozmand et al., 2009) reported an increase in auditory responsiveness to the 100 
cents compared to 500 cents pitch-shift stimuli. In addition, speaking-induced modulation 
of neural activities are shown to be largest when feedback deviations are small and 
closely match the intended output (Behroozmand et al., 2009). In this context, the adaptive 
modulation of compensation gain has important functional implications for allowing 
speakers to optimally correct for self-generated feedback errors while avoiding destabilizing 
behavior by suppressing corrective motor responses to larger deviations induced by external 
sources (e.g., environmental sounds and those produced by other speakers). In this context, 
we suggest that our data corroborate the notion that attentional instructions may lead to 
increased sensitivity to feedback changes, resulting in neural interpretations of a larger error 
signal in response to the same level of pitch-shift alterations during speech production. 
This effect would then result in assigning a lower compensation gain, which would lead to 
smaller corrective speech motor response to AAF stimuli.
Although our proposed model provides a possible explanation of the observed behavioral 
and neural consequences of attentional instructions, it is noteworthy that its principles 
remain mostly speculative and will require further validation using evidence from future 
studies. This is mainly because of several limitations outlined in the following section.

Firstly, while attentional instructions led to increased neural responses to AAF stimuli, 
our data did not provide direct evidence for an increase in the size of perceived feedback 
errors. This is partly because although P2 ERPs were positively correlated with speech 
compensation magnitudes, the underlying error signals driving such behaviors are not 
directly observable. In addition, the relation between feedback errors and compensation 
magnitudes is greatly affected by the properties (e.g., slope) of the gain function. For 
example, with a steep-enough slope, compensatory responses to a large feedback error can 
be potentially smaller than those in response to a smaller error. This implies that there can 
be different combinations of error signals and gain functions leading to a similar speech 
compensatory response. Moreover, the complex interplay between attentional instructions 
and higher-level cognitive and sensorimotor mechanisms could not be examined in the 
present study. This is in part due to an inherent limitation in our experimental design, 
which did not provide a direct measure to determine the extent of attentional modulations 
between subjects in the two groups. Therefore, future studies are warranted to probe the 
influence of instructions on higher-level attentional and sensorimotor mechanisms during 
speech auditory feedback control. Some studies have also suggested that performing an 
active motor behavior may affect the neural state of brain networks involved in sensory 
processing (Reznik and Mukamel, 2019). While it is reasonable to assume that this 
may affect the neural processing of pitch shifts in the attentional-instruction group who 
performed an additional button press task, the temporal separation between the vocalization 
and listening tasks vs. the button press responses in these subjects would minimize potential 
confounds associated with motor-related cross-modal influences. Specifically, subjects in the 
attentional-instruction group registered their responses via a button press that was performed 
a few (i.e., 4–5) seconds after the vocalization and listening tasks were completed, which 
minimized the temporal overlap between motor planning and execution of upper limb (i.e., 
hand) movements vs. pitch-shift responses to speech vowel productions. In addition, the 
recruitment of predominantly older adult subjects in this study raises the question whether 
including a younger group would have yielded different results due to potentially normative 
attentional abilities in younger individuals (Kok, 2000; Madden, 2007; Sekuler et al., 2008). 
Also, it should be noted that three out of the twenty-one subjects had low accuracy rates in 
the button press task, possibly attributed to fatigue among the aging subjects. Furthermore, 
the present study utilized a between-group design by comparing data between two groups of 
subjects who performed the tasks under attentional-instruction and no-instruction conditions. 
Although, as compared to a within-group design, the choice of this experimental approach 
raises potential concerns regarding the inherent individual variability for the behavioral 
and neural responses to AAF stimuli, we aimed to mitigate such confounding effects by 
comparing data between two randomly assigned age- and gender-matched groups in our 
study. Further, we conducted assumption checks and applied appropriate transformations 
to ensure that the residuals were normally distributed and had equal variances during 
statistical analysis. Moreover, we performed supplementary analyses to account for between-
subject variations by examining the linear association between the behavioral and neural 
responses while splitting the data into upper and lower halves based on the magnitude of 
speech compensatory responses. These additional analyses further validated our findings by 
showing a consistent pattern of significant positive linear association between the amplitude 
of P2 ERP components and the magnitude of speech compensations in the regression

models. In addition, consistent with earlier results, we found that compensation magnitudes 
were significantly diminished in the attentional-instruction compared to no-instruction 
group. These additional analyses further validated our earlier findings and corroborated 
our interpretation of the role of attentional instructions in modulating the underlying 
mechanisms of speech auditory feedback control. Our data provide new insights into 
the potential implications of utilizing attentional instructions as a method for enhancing 
speech feedback error compensation mechanisms. However, despite such efforts, it remains 
a possibility that unaccounted individual variability may, at least partially, confound the 
results. Therefore, because of the lack of determinacies and potential limitations, caution 
should be considered while interpreting the observed effect in the context of the proposed 
model.
The dissociation between the neural and behavioral results in our study can also be discussed 
in the context of the dual-stream model of speech (Hickok & Poeppel, 2004, 2007). In this 
model, the dorsal stream provides an integrative sensorimotor interface to compare predicted 
and actual sensory representations for speech control while the ventral stream is primarily 
implicated in comprehension of incoming sensory information. Research on the dual-stream 
model of visual awareness has shown a dissociation between the ventral and dorsal streams 
where the ventral pathways are activated during conscious perception of sensory stimuli, 
while the dorsal pathways can process visually guided actions without conscious perception 
(Fang and He, 2005; Goodale and Westwood, 2004; Zhan et al., 2018). In addition, it 
has been shown that conscious awareness and increased confidence in speech feedback 
outcomes can lead to reduced reliance on dorsal streams for the integration of external 
auditory feedback (Subramaniam, 2021), thereby influencing (i.e., reducing) the extent to 
which motor compensation is generated in response to AAF stimuli. Thus, it is reasonable 
to assume that conscious processing of speech feedback errors is mediated by dissociated 
mechanisms in ventral and dorsal streams, leading to different behavioral consequences in 
the attentional-instruction vs. no-instruction group. In our study, we explicitly instructed 
one group to be attentive and report external pitch changes in their real-time speech 
auditory feedback, making them to be cognizant of and consciously perceive and detect 
AAF stimuli, while such instructions were not given to the other group. Therefore, subjects 
in the attentional-instruction group may have compromised sensorimotor processing of 
feedback error signals in the dorsal stream for conscious processing of those changes 
in the ventral stream networks. Such switching processes may limit neural processing 
capacity in dorsal pathways for error correction, providing an alternative explanation for 
the diminished magnitude of speech compensatory responses in subjects in the attentional-
instruction group. Consequently, we posit that this may account for the observed reduction 
in compensatory response when subjects are instructed to pay attention to auditory feedback. 
Our data provide preliminary evidence for a potential link between attentional instructions, 
feedback error processing, and speech compensation; however, further research is warranted 
to fully understand the complex nature of relationship between these behavioral and neural 
mechanisms.

previous studies has indicated the more predominant role of the right-hemispheric cortical 
auditory networks in pitch processing during speech production and singing (Riecker et 
al., 2000; Van Lancker and Sidtis, 1992; Zatorre et al., 1992). In addition, other studies 
have reported bilateral activation of cortical motor and insular networks during tasks 
involving vocal pitch modulation (Dichter et al., 2018; Perry et al., 1999). In this context, 
our findings regarding increased neural activity in right-hemispheric sensorimotor cortical 
regions (e.g., STG, TTG, PrCG, and PoCG) in the attentional-instruction group further 
highlight the significant role of these brain networks in vocal pitch regulation during speech 
production. In addition, the insular cortex has been proposed to be involved in ventral and 
dorsal stream processing of speech while receiving contribution from higher-level cognitive 
mechanisms during conscious processing of relevant sensorimotor stimuli (Fridriksson et 
al., 2016). Therefore, considering findings in our study, a plausible proposition is that 
heightened neural activity in the right insular cortex in the attentional-instruction group 
may reflect conscious perception of speech errors in the ventral stream networks. This 
notion is supported by the dual-stream model of speech, suggesting that while dorsal stream 
processing is predominantly mediated by left-lateralized brain networks, the ventral stream 
processing of speech is bilaterally represented in the human brain (Hickok et al., 2011; 
Hickok and Poeppel, 2004). In addition, increased neural activity in areas within the right 
STG and TTG in the attentional-instruction group further corroborates the notion of elevated 
conscious processing of speech auditory feedback errors in the ventral stream (Fridriksson 
et al., 2016; Hickok and Poeppel, 2015). Interestingly, we also found a concurrent pattern of 
neural activity increase in areas within the right PrCG, suggesting potential modulation of 
speech compensatory gain in cortical motor areas in response to auditory feedback errors in 
the attentional-instruction group. This effect was accompanied by elevated neural activities 
in somatosensory cortical areas within the right PoCG, potentially resulting from changes in 
proprioceptive feedback input arising from laryngeal muscles for vocal pitch control during 
speech vowel production. Together, these results highlight the effect of top-down attentional-
instruction mechanisms in modulating neural activities in distributed right-hemispheric brain 
networks implicated in speech feedback error detection and correction.
There are several practical and clinical implications of our study findings that can inform 
future research and interventions for individuals with neurological conditions leading to 
speech disorders. Along this line, our study addresses a critical gap in previous literature 
by elucidating the effects of attentional instructions on the underlying behavioral and neural 
mechanisms of speech production and auditory feedback control. These findings suggest that 
instructions for maintaining attention to auditory feedback may be a useful tool for clinicians 
working with individuals with speech disorders, such as apraxia of speech or neurological 
disorders with language impairments, such as Parkinson’s disease and stroke ( Behroozmand 
and Larson, 2011; Brendel et al., 2004; Maas et al., 2008; Patel et al., 2023). Clinical 
interventions involving attentional instructions may improve behavioral outcome measures 
while facilitating neural activities in key brain regions including frontal, temporal, and 
parietal cortices implicated in speech production and sensorimotor control. In this context,

Fig. 5. 
A) The attentional-instruction model of speech sensorimotor integration. During speech 
production, a motor plan is generated in the pre-motor cortex and transmitted to cortical 
motor areas. An efference copy of the motor plan is translated into a forward prediction code 
in the auditory cortex via the auditory-motor interface. The auditory cortex then compares 
the forward prediction with the speech output, generating an error signal in case of any 
mismatches. The auditory-motor interface translates the error signal from sensory to motor 
domain while assigning an adaptive compensation gain to correct for feedback changes. The 
compensation gain follows a Gaussian distribution function, which assigns larger gains to 
correct for smaller errors in the auditory feedback, and vice versa. Attentional instructions 
can increase neural sensitivity to feedback errors, which consequently leads to smaller 
speech compensations. B) The Gaussian function for adaptive speech compensation gain in 
response to auditory feedback errors. The x-axis represents the magnitude of error signals, 
and the y-axis shows the gain for speech motor compensation. For a given error signal, 
the compensation gain is determined by mapping the x-axis value onto the Gaussian curve 
on the y-axis (upward vertical arrows and dotted lines). Attentional instructions increase 
in the magnitude of error signals, leading to a reduction in speech compensation gain 
(downward vertical arrow). The Gaussian function demonstrates symmetrical properties, 
which similarly maps positive (i.e., predicted feedback > actual feedback) and negative 
(i.e., predicted feedback < actual feedback) auditory error signals onto corrective motor 
commands for speech compensation. AAF: altered auditory feedback; IPL: inferior parietal 
lobe; HG: Heschl’s gyrus; STG: superior temporal lobe; STS: superior temporal sulcus.
