Active avoidance of potential
threat is reflected in oscillatory
brain activity
Arash Mirifar1, Yannik Stegmann2, Hannah Engle1, Janna Teigeler2, Matthias Gamer2 &
Andreas Keil1
Detecting and responding to real and potential threats is crucial for self-preservation. While most
studies have focused on passive threat perception, the neural mechanisms underlying active defensive
behaviors like avoidance remain poorly understood. A secondary analysis was conducted on previously
published EEG data from 95 participants who performed a task with three cue conditions. Each
trial featured a colored cue (red, yellow, or green) followed by a naturalistic image: red signaled
an unavoidable electric shock, yellow an avoidable shock requiring a rapid response, and green no
shock. Time-frequency analyses revealed differences in oscillatory activity within the alpha and mu
rhythms across conditions. Parietal alpha activity following cue and image onset, and left central mu
activity, preceding the offset of the naturalistic image in the avoidance condition, differed significantly
compared to other conditions. Notably, reduced mu power in the avoidance condition prior to image
offset linearly predicted faster avoidance responses, while posterior alpha power correlated negatively
with self-reported anxiety. These findings support the idea that oscillatory activity in visual and motor
systems differentially drives avoidance responses to threat. This study advances understanding of the
interplay between visual and motor cortices from threat detection to avoidance behavior. Keywords  Potential threat, Active avoidance, Alpha, Mu, Visual and motor cortex
Human defense relies on the interaction between psychological, physiological, and cognitive processes that enable
individuals to rapidly assess their environment, perceive threats, and engage in adaptive responses. When threat
becomes imminent and the opportunity to escape or defend exists, behavioral dispositions must shift to fight or
flight, requiring parasympathetic withdrawal and sympathetic dominance1,2. The aim of the present study was
to shed light on the neurophysiological mechanisms underpinning defensive action dispositions and, ultimately,
defensive behavior. The temporal dynamic from threat perception to defensive action is often referred to as the
defense cascade, and has been well defined in the rodent model3. In humans, however, it is currently unclear
how the perceptual and attentional processing of threats interface with neurophysiological signals of motor
preparation and execution of defensive action, such as avoidance and escape. Clarifying this temporal dynamic
from threat perception to defensive action is especially important in the context of anxiety spectrum disorders,
where heightened sensitivity to threat-related cues may reflect altered dynamics of attentional selection and
distractor suppression during early processing stages. This heightened sensitivity may also influence later phases
of motor preparation and execution. It has been argued that anxiety can affect both perception and performance
across multiple levels of operational control (e.g., from attentional and interpretational processes to motor
preparation and execution), with downstream consequences for higher-order functions, such as situational
awareness and decision making4. A deeper understanding of these processes can ultimately shed light on how
defensive actions are shaped and on the ways in which they may be disrupted or optimized. Here we use a large
data set from Stegmann et al5. to examine the overarching hypothesis that active avoidance, compared to other
scenarios, facilitates the electrocortical signatures of action preparation and execution. A long-held view on motor preparation is that visuomotor tasks prompt inhibitory interactions between the
visual and the motor systems, as has been demonstrated when either system is engaged transiently (e.g6–8,.). In
human electrophysiology, this interaction is thought to involve facilitation of one system (motor or visual) and
its corresponding oscillatory activity, accompanied by downregulation of the other system9–11. Extending this
view, Sabate et al12. demonstrated that visual cues signaling forthcoming movement elicit a brief phase-locked
mu increase within 60–80 ms, followed by a sustained non–phase-locked mu suppression. These two phases
may reflect complementary mechanisms of visuomotor integration: the early increase promotes local neuronal

observed in motor systems at rest, possess several different characteristics than parieto-occipital alpha waves, the
amplitude of which is negatively related to attentive visual processing: (1) the mu rhythm primarily occurs in the
sensorimotor cortex; (2) it desynchronizes during movement processing over the contralateral hemisphere and
does not respond to eye opening; and (3) the mu rhythm exhibits an arc-shaped waveform, which distinguishes
it from sinusoidal alpha waves16,17. The present study intended to determine the extent to which the power
of mu rhythm activity varies with the ability to avoid an imminent threat. To examine the specificity of the
hypothesized sensitivity of mu rhythm power to active threat avoidance, we also examined the extent to which
avoidance behavior prompts changes in time-varying alpha-band power. It has long been established that alpha power is sharply reduced immediately following the presentation of
a salient visual cue, over posterior areas of the human cerebral cortex. By contrast, reduction in mu rhythm
amplitude is reliably observed over contralateral sensors as participants imagine, prepare for, or execute a
lateralized motor response18. In the paradigm employed here, participants were presented with visual cues that
signaled either certain threat (unavoidable shock), potential threat (avoidable shock through a speeded motor
response), or safety (no shock). We, therefore, hypothesized that upon viewing visual cues that indicate an
avoidable threat, participants will exhibit a greater transient reduction in alpha power in the parietal and visual
cortex compared to the safe and certain threat conditions. Importantly, as participants prepare for avoidant motor
action, we expected that the power of the mu rhythm will selectively decrease, compared to safety and certain
threat. In addition to testing these main hypotheses, we also conducted manipulation checks and exploratory
analyses: First, we established, using independent component analysis (ICA) that the power of the mu rhythm
in the motor cortex is independent of the posterior alpha rhythm, and that pre-response mu power is related to
response times, within subjects. Finally, we examined the correlation between participants’ self-reported anxiety
and the power of alpha and mu rhythms. Materials and methods
To ascertain the robustness of the observed effects, data were collected from two laboratories located in
Gainesville/Florida, USA and Würzburg, Germany. These datasets were previously used in Stegmann et al.5,,
who examined heart rate, gaze behavior, pupil dynamics, and alpha-band changes activity in a-priori defined
time windows and frequency bands. In the present analysis, however, we focus on the investigation of neural
responses across the whole scalp, with particular emphasis on motor-related activity when facing and actively
avoiding threats. Moreover, this study investigates relationships not previously addressed, such as correlations
between alpha/mu activity and trait anxiety, as well as between mu power and response times. Finally, in the
current analysis we applied different statistical approaches tailored to these questions. Together, these variations
in focus, methodology, and analytic strategy make the present study independent from the earlier work and
ensure that no duplication occurs. We employed a dual-stage approach to analyze our data. Initially, we conducted analyses on the combined data
from both laboratories, which was followed by separate analyses for each laboratory to assess the generalizability
of our findings. Through this approach we were able to examine the robustness of the findings across samples
differing in demographic, cultural, and socio-economic variables. Participants
The sample size for the first project (earlier publication of this dataset) was determined based on a priori power
analysis using GPower19 (see5 for details). In total, 102 nonclinical participants completed the study across the
two laboratories; only one participant was excluded — due to EEG artifacts. In the present study, which focused
more specifically on motor preparation and execution, we employed a different preprocessing pipeline and
extended the time window to cover more activity prior to movement onset. Seven participants were excluded
due to muscle artifacts in their data, which led to a final sample of 95 participants. Given the final sample (N =
95) and α = 0.05, a sensitivity analysis in G*Power for a repeated-measures ANOVA (3 measurements, assumed
within-subject correlation = 0.50, ε = 1) indicates ≥ 95% power to detect effects of f ≥ 0.17. Thus, the study is well
powered for small-to-medium effects. Study design and experimental paradigm
In this study, we used a within-subjects design, exactly replicating the paradigm from Rösler and Gamer20. The
experiment consisted of three conditions: A) a certain threat condition, B) a potential threat (active avoidance)
condition, and C) a safe condition. Consequently, participants either received an annoying electric shock (i.e.,
threat condition), or avoided the shock if they hit the space key fast enough with their right index finger (i.e.,
potential threat/avoidable condition), or did not respond and received no shock at all (i.e., safe condition). These
three conditions were indicated by three distinct visual cues and presented in a pseudo-random order, ensuring
that no more than three consecutive trials of the same type occurred. In three distinct blocks, there were a
total of 180 trials, 60 trials for each condition. Each trial comprised a white fixation cross (4.5 s duration at the
Florida lab and between 4 and 6 s at the Würzburg lab), a subsequent colored fixation cross (2 s duration; red

Electric shock adjustment
An annoying electrical pulse was administered using a Digitimer DS7A constant current stimulator (Hertfordshire, United Kingdom) and a surface electrode placed on the top of the non-dominant wrist, approximately two
centimeters below the wrist, on the outer arm. The pulse duration switch was set to 2000 µs, the voltage
amplitude control of output pulses was set to 400 V, and the three-position polarity control toggle switch was
in the center for ‘Alternating.’ The electrical pulse consisted of a sequence of three 2-millisecond square-wave
pulses, separated by 50 milliseconds, with alternating polarity. The intensity of the electrical pulse was adjusted
for each participant. They were instructed to inform the experimenter about the level of pain experienced upon
receiving the electrical shock. Each electrical pulse was verbally rated using a Visual Analog Scale (VAS), ranging
from 0 to 10, where 0 indicated “not painful at all” and 10 indicated “unbearably painful”. At the University of
Florida, the experimenter delivered electrical pulses repeatedly, starting from 0 mA, and increased the intensity
in 0.1 mA increments. This process continued until the participants perceived the electrical pulse as having an
intensity of 4 on the VAS scale. At that point, the amperage was reduced to the minimum, and the incremental
stimulation process was repeated. This procedure was repeated three times. After the third iteration, the mean
amperage of the stimulations represented the VAS level 4. To determine the final stimulation for use throughout
the experiment, an additional 50% of the level 4 amperage was added. However, at the University of Würzburg,
the experimenter delivered electrical pulses repeatedly starting from 0 mA and then increased the intensity to
the level rated as 6 in steps of 0.25 mA. From there, they decreased the intensity until a pain rating of 5 was
reached. Then, they increased intensity again, until a pain rating of more than 6 (= 7) was reached for the first
time. After calibration, the mean shock intensities were 1.13 ± 0.54 mA (M ± SD) at the University of Florida and
1.90 ± 1.06 mA (M ± SD) at the University of Würzburg. Questionnaires
In the present study, we employed several standardized computer based psychometric questionnaires to assess
symptoms of emotional psychopathology, including the Beck Depression Inventory (BDI; consisting of 21
multiple-choice questions), the State-Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI) in its trait version (STAI-T; assessing
trait anxiety), and the Anxiety Sensitivity Index (ASI; measuring the participants’ fear of potentially negative
consequences of anxiety-related symptoms and sensations). The STAI was used in planned correlational analyses
reported below. The BDI and ASI were administered as part of a larger project and as control measures to be used
if unexpected variance occurred. Such analyses were, however, not necessary upon examining the data. EEG recording and preprocessing/analysis
EEG data at both sites were continuously recorded through an Electrical Geodesics (EGI) high-impedance
system with a 128-channel (Ag-AgCl electrodes) HydroCel net and an EGI Net Amps 300 amplifier at a sampling
rate of 500 Hz with the vertex electrode (Cz), as recording reference. Electrode impedances were kept below 60
kΩ, as recommended for EGI high-impedance amplifiers22. At the University of Würzburg, the data were online
bandpass filtered between 0.1 and 100 Hz with an additional 50 Hz notch filter. At the University of Florida, only
a Nyquist online filter (elliptical, 200 Hz lowpass) was applied. EEG data from both sites were processed offline
using EMEGS 2.8 (Electromagnetic Encephalography software)23in MATLAB 2019a (MathWorks, Natick, MA, USA). All channels underwent preprocessing by means of a 3 Hz high-pass filter (defined as the 18-dB point, Butterworth, 4th order filter) and a 34 Hz low-pass filter (defined as the 45-dB point, Butterworth, 10th order
filter), and were re-referenced to the average reference. Subsequently, EEG data were segmented into epochs of
600 ms before and 9800 ms after the cue onset and automatically corrected for eye artifacts using the method
proposed by Schlögl et al24.. The epoched trials underwent artifact rejection using the Statistical Correction of
Artifacts in Dense Array Studies (SCADS) procedure25. This involved calculating data quality indices for each
channel and trial, including absolute value, standard deviation, and maximum differences across time points. Eye movements were corrected using regression-based EOG24,26 with HEOG and VEOG sensors (consisting
of EGI sensors 127, 128, 25, 126, 125, 8). Initially, sensors in Cz-referenced data were classified as bad if, for a
minimum of one parameter, the sensor’s median was above 5 SD of the grand median of all sensors. Bad sensors
were interpolated using only weighted spherical splines27. Thereafter, data were referenced against the average. For each trial, depending on the number and spatial distribution of the remaining bad sensors, the sensors were
interpolated, or the trial was rejected entirely23,28 for details. We excluded the following percentages of trials from
Gainesville/Florida and Würzburg data, respectively: 44.52% ± 16.97% and 40.20% ± 15.01% from the certain
threatening condition; 43.36% ± 13.91% and 41.70% ± 14.72% from the potentially threatening (avoidable)
condition; and 45.14% ± 14.79% and 41.49% ± 13.28 from the safe condition. The percentage of excluded trials
is higher than in other EEG/ERP experiments, likely due to the unique demands of this paradigm, including
its extended trial duration, motor preparation and responses in avoidable trials, as well as anticipatory muscle
tension in unavoidable trials followed by electric shocks, all of which increase the potential for artifacts. Despite
the higher number of excluded trials, the large sample size and sufficient number of retained trials per condition
(M = 69.16 for certain threat; M = 68.95 for potential threat/avoidable; M = 68.01 for safe) ensure that the dataset
remains robust for statistical analysis. Despite the higher number of excluded trials, the large sample size and the
sufficient number of retained trials per condition (M = 69.16 for certain threat; M = 68.95 for potential threat/

rhythm (8–14 Hz) at visual and motor cortex, respectively. To this end, artifact-free EEG epochs were convolved
with a family of complex Morlet wavelets that had a wavelet coefficient F/sigmaF = 7, where F is a given frequency

To accurately differentiate between alpha and mu rhythms in the motor cortex, we employed Independent
Component Analysis (ICA) as an additional analytical technique, deviating from the original study by Stegmann
et al5.. ICA was used to isolate the mu rhythm, minimizing overlapping spectral properties with the alpha
rhythm29. This step was crucial for the present analysis because the alpha and mu rhythms co-occur in similar
frequency bands, even though they are associated with different cognitive and motor functions. Additionally,
since the amplitude of mu oscillations is generally substantially smaller than that of the more dominant posterior
alpha oscillations16,29, sensorimotor mu activity estimated at the sensor level is probably contaminated by visual
alpha activity as a result of volume conduction. To address this, artifact-corrected EEG data was decomposed
into independent components (ICs) using the Second Order Blind Identification (SOBI) method30,31employed in
EEGLAB. An ICA component was selected for further examination if (1) the time series had a power spectrum
with a well-defined peak within the 8–14 Hz range and (2) the spatial topographic map suggested a centro-
lateral, sensorimotor, origin. By separating these components, we were able to more precisely investigate the
specific contributions of the mu rhythm to motor readiness in our experimental conditions, examining aspects
of the data that were not considered in the original study by Stegmann et al5.. Statistical analysis
To capitalize on the advantages of a multisite study, we conducted our data analysis in two phases. Initially, we
conducted all statistical analyses on the combined sample from both laboratories. In the subsequent stage, we
replicated each analysis individually for both laboratories, as needed, to confirm the consistency and robustness
of our findings, thereby minimizing the risk of laboratory-specific biases influencing the results. To specifically test the hypotheses of differences of alpha and mu rhythm activity in a potentially threatening
(avoidable) condition versus a certain threatening condition and a safe condition, as indicators of neural
mechanisms underlying an active defensive behavior, we fitted signed F-contrast weights representing the
mean differences expected under this hypothesis: The time-varying spectral power obtained from the wavelet
transform was modelled as a quadratic trend (weights: 1, −2, 1) for the certain threat, avoidable threat, and safe
condition, respectively, applied to each frequency, time point, and sensor. Multiple comparisons were addressed
by calculating permutation distributions for surrogate data in which conditions were shuffled among participants,
with the maximum of each of 1000 shuffled F-tests entering the permutation distribution32. The 0.3 quantile
of these distributions served as the threshold for statistical significance, resulting in an overall F-criterion of
16.16 (and individual thresholds of 13.65 and 15.86 for Gainesville/Florida and Würzburg, respectively). For
robustness, we only report time frames where more than three sensors exceeded the critical threshold at any
point in time within the time frames. In addition to these planned comparison analyses, we conducted two mass univariate comparisons between
the three conditions (threat vs. avoidance and safe vs. avoidance), using paired t-tests, to visualize the nature of
any quadratic effects detected by the above contrast analysis and examine their frequency specificity to the bands
of interest. Several studies have highlighted that the power of alpha and mu activity is significantly and positively
correlated with response time latency (see e.g34–36.,). To test this hypothesis in the present paradigm, we conducted
a Spearman’s correlation analysis within each participant, between alpha/mu rhythm power and response times
(RT) in the avoidable condition. For this analysis, we examined both 10 Hz and 12 Hz because these frequencies
are within the alpha (8–12 Hz) and mu (8–14 Hz) bands37,38,. We then quantified the relation between RT and
spectral power at each frequency across the whole topography. To rule out artifactually high and low RTs, we
retained only RTs that were greater than 100 ms and less than 1000 ms. We then paired these RTs with the trial-
wise spectral power in the alpha and mu bands during the last second prior to the response time, from the same
trial. The individual correlations contributed by each participant were then tested against zero using a non-
parametric sign test, testing the hypothesis that mu rhythm power and RT were positively correlated, whereas
posterior alpha power was not. Paralleling the rationale presented above, we expected correlations between RT
and mu rhythm changes at left-central sensors. Next, we conducted exploratory analyses to examine whether a correlation exists between participants’ self-
reported trait anxiety levels and the power of the alpha and/or mu rhythms. To investigate this relation, we
computed Spearman’s correlation coefficients between participants’ STAI-T scores and EEG power in the alpha/
mu bands across all sensors and time points. A cluster-based permutation test was used to control for multiple
comparisons. Results

(sign test, p = 0.002). However, as expected, this correlation was not statistically significant at central sensors
of the ipsilateral (right) hemisphere (i.e., sensor 87, relative to the responding hand, sign test, p = 0.606). This
pattern was consistent beyond the sensors of interest (see Fig. 6A). Note that there were also differences in the
direction of correlation among participants. For example, approximately two-thirds of participants showed a
positive correlation between 12 Hz power and the response times at sensor 37, while the other third showed
a negative correlation (see Fig. 6B; and Fig. 6C for the overall distribution of individual correlations between
the mu rhythm (12 Hz) at sensor 37 and participants’ response times). This ratio was almost the same for the
neighboring frequencies and adjacent sensors, as well as sensors in the parieto-occipital region. Correlation between Alpha/Mu rhythms and STAI-T

In the present re-analysis, we sought to determine the extent to which the power of oscillatory activity during
natural image viewing is influenced by action preparation during threat avoidance, when a motor response
is required, and how this relates to the corresponding power of mu rhythms in the motor cortex. We then
investigated the correlation between the power of alpha and mu rhythms and response times in the avoidable
threat condition, followed by an analysis on correlation between participants’ trait anxiety levels and the power
of the posterior alpha and/or mu rhythms. As part of motor preparation, we found that mu rhythm power in the contralateral motor cortex –
corresponding to the hand used to respond to the cue – decreased more significantly in the avoidable condition,
where a motor response was required, than in the certain threat and safe conditions. Notably, this reduction
occurred in the time window immediately preceding the avoidance cue, whereas the posterior alpha rhythm
showed no power change during this period. A decrease in the power of the mu rhythm was also observed
earlier in the trial, in response to the onset of cues and natural images, and these changes were similar across
left-central and parieto-occipital sites, where typical alpha power reduction was observed in response to changes
in the visual environment. The correlation between the left-central mu rhythm and RTs supports the notion that mu activity is associated
with motor preparation and lower mu rhythm power is associated with faster motor execution. This finding aligns

but also during sensorimotor engagement through movement observation40,41, motor imagery42,43 or tactile

of the mu rhythm tends to be more pronounced during active motor tasks than other, more passive, tasks and/or
conditions. fMRI findings also converge with our results, showing that blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD)
responses in the sensorimotor cortex and higher-order motor areas, such as the supplementary motor area, are
negatively correlated with mu power29. Simultaneous EEG-fMRI studies further show that both alpha and mu

and modulating cortical rhythms47–49. This finding supports the concept that the thalamus is integral to the
generation and modulation of cortical rhythms50. However, a key distinction emerges: alpha-BOLD correlations
are linked to the visual cortex, whereas mu-BOLD correlations are associated with the sensorimotor cortex29. Future research should more directly examine whether the correlation between mu power and response time
is influenced by the duration and timing of mu rhythm activity, for example by testing whether slower trials
provide a longer ramping period. Addressing this question will require a larger number of trials per condition to
allow more robust within-participant analyses. When the presentation of a visual, auditory, or tactile target is preceded by a stimulus-relevant cue, participants
tend to respond more accurately and rapidly than when a stimulus is presented without a preparatory cue51,52. This finding suggests that prior to stimulus presentation, focused attention improves stimulus processing and
readiness for an action when it is required53. Such action preparation under threatening situations is crucial,
specifically if the threat is controllable or avoidable: a passive defense mode switches to an active fight or flight
behavioral strategy54. In the context of action preparation when encountering a threatening situation with the
possibility of avoiding the threat actively vs. the unavoidable or (safe) control conditions, studies have shown
that during anticipation of a threat and preparing to respond, physiological changes, including a decrease in
heart rate and heightened electrodermal activity, occur alongside restricted visual exploration, all of which are
part of the body’s preparation to react to the threat20. Moreover, anxious individuals often exhibit heightened

broader sensory and motor system engagement, potentially linked to mu rhythm modulation during such stress. These findings are in accord with our results showing a greater suppression of alpha in the visual cortex and the

cortices, differ when motor responses are required under alternative consequences (e.g., pleasant or neutral
outcomes). Conclusion
In summary, the present re-analysis highlights the distinct yet corresponding roles of mu and alpha rhythms,
which can be separated using ICA to distinguish between sensorimotor activity and visual activity. Moreover,
higher alpha power in occipital-parietal regions was associated with lower trait anxiety, while greater mu
suppression in the left central cortex (sensorimotor area) correlated with faster threat responses. Although
research on the mu rhythm in threat-related responses remains limited, our findings suggest that mu rhythm
activity contributes to how humans perceive and react to environmental threats, particularly when motor action
is required. This study contributes to our understanding of how visual and motor cortices interact from the
detection of threat to the execution of an avoidance response. Data availability
