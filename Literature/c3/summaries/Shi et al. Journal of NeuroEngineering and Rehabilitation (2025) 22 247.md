Abstract
Background  Action observation and imitation training (AOIT) is an evidence-based cognitive-motor rehabilitation
strategy for Parkinson’s disease (PD), particularly for the postural instability and gait disorder (PIGD) subtype. However,
its effectiveness may decline with disease-related impairments in neuroplasticity. Intermittent theta burst stimulation
(iTBS), a patterned repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation protocol, can induce LTP-like plasticity and may
enhance responsiveness to rehabilitation. This study investigated whether iTBS priming augments AOIT effects on gait
and cognition in early-stage PIGD and explored underlying neurophysiological mechanisms. Methods  Fifteen patients with early-stage PIGD participated in a randomized, double-blind, sham-controlled
crossover trial. Each phase included five consecutive days of AOIT preceded by either real or sham iTBS applied over
the bilateral leg region of the primary motor cortex, separated by a washout period of more than four weeks. Pre-
and post-intervention assessments included dual-task gait analysis, cognitive tests, clinical scales, neurophysiological
measures (motor evoked potentials, cortical silent period), and resting-state EEG power spectral density. Results  Both conditions improved balance and gait measures. However, real iTBS significantly enhanced dual-task
gait automaticity (F = 5.558, P = 0.026) and global cognition (F = 5.294, P = 0.026) compared to sham. Real iTBS also
increased cortical silent period (F = 4.655, P = 0.040) and MEP-based cortical plasticity response (F = 6.131, P = 0.020). Improvements in cortical plasticity were significantly correlated with better gait performance (r = − 0.429, P = 0.020)
and motor scores (r = − 0.463, P = 0.011). No adverse events were reported. Bilateral intermittent theta-burst stimulation
as a priming strategy to enhance action
observation and imitation training in early
parkinson’s disease: a proof-of-concept study

Background
Gait disturbance is a hallmark motor symptom of Par­
kinson’s disease (PD), particularly in individuals with the
postural instability and gait disorder (PIGD) subtype. These impairments are difficult to treat and often show
limited responsiveness to dopaminergic medications [1,
2]. As a result, non-pharmacological interventions have
become a critical component of disease management in
early-stage PD when patients retain functional reserve [3,
4]. Rehabilitation interventions that integrate both cogni­
tive and motor elements have shown particular prom­
ise in improving gait stability and motor control in PD
[5, 6]. Among current approaches, action observation
and imitation training (AOIT) has emerged as a prom­
ising method that simultaneously engages motor execu­
tion and cognitive domains such as attention, working
memory, and executive function [7, 8]. AOIT is believed
to activate the mirror neuron system and internal action
simulation pathways, facilitating motor learning and neu­
roplastic adaptions [9, 10]. While initially developed for
stroke rehabilitation [11], AOIT has shown great poten­
tial in PD treatment [12]. However, dopaminergic deple­
tion in PD leads to dysfunction in cortico-basal ganglia
circuits, resulting in impaired motor learning and syn­
aptic plasticity [13, 14]. In particular, the mechanisms
underlying long-term potentiation (LTP)- and long-
term depression (LTD), which represent experience-
dependent neural reorganization, were disrupted as the
disease progresses [15, 16]. These deficits may limit the
brain’s capability to benefit from conventional rehabilita­
tion. Therefore, there is an emergent need for alternative
methods that can enhance neuroplasticity and optimize
motor-cognitive training effects. Intermittent theta burst stimulation (iTBS) is a non-
invasive brain stimulation technique that employs a
high-frequency, excitatory pattern capable of enhancing
cortical excitability and inducing LTP-like plasticity [17–
19]. iTBS has been shown to improve motor performance
and promote synaptic reorganization in both healthy
individuals and patients with PD when applied to the pri­
mary motor cortex [20, 21]. Compared to high-frequency
repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS),
iTBS offers several advantages, including shorter stimula­
tion time, lower intensity, and greater efficacy in inducing
durable neurophysiological changes [17, 18]. Delivering
iTBS prior to rehabilitation may act as a neural priming
strategy, by shifting the brain into a more excitable and
plastic state, thereby enhancing responsiveness to subse­
quent training [22, 23]. Specifically, applying iTBS before
AOIT may create a more favorable neurophysiological
environment for motor learning, thereby amplifying the
therapeutic effects of AOIT on both motor and cognitive
domains in PD [24, 25]. Based on this rationale, we conducted a randomized,
double-blind, sham-controlled, crossover clinical trial
in early-stage PIGD patients to examine whether bilat­
eral iTBS applied to the leg region of M1 could enhance
the efficacy of AOIT. We hypothesized that iTBS would
facilitate cortical plasticity and improve cognitive-motor
integration, leading to superior outcomes in gait, motor
function, and cognition compared to AOIT alone. Methods
Participants
Eighteen patients with idiopathic PD were recruited
from the Department of Neurology at Tianjin Medical
University General Hospital, China. The inclusion cri­
teria were as follows: [1] age between 50 and 75 years;
[2] Hoehn and Yahr stage between 1.0 and 2.5; and [3] a
stable daily dosage of anti-parkinsonian medication for at
least 4 weeks prior to inclusion, with no changes permit­
ted throughout the observation period. Exclusion crite­
ria included: [1] a history of other neurological disorders
confirmed by MRI; [2] orthopedic conditions that could
impair mobility; [3] prominent resting head tremors; and
[4] any contraindications to transcranial magnetic stimu­
lation (TMS). Study design
A randomized, single-blind, sham-controlled, crossover
design was used in this study. Prior to the intervention
phase, all participants completed a TMS pre-adaptation
session. Individual structural MRI scans were used to
guide neuronavigation and scalp-based registration. Resting and active motor threshold (rMT and aMT)
were determined during this session. A four-week inter­
val between the pre-adaptation and the start of the first
intervention phase was maintained to minimize potential
carryover effects. Conclusions  Bilateral iTBS targeting the leg representation of the primary motor cortex can potentiate AOIT effects
in early-stage PIGD by enhancing cortical plasticity and motor learning. These findings support the integration of iTBS
as a priming strategy within cognitive-motor rehabilitation protocols for PD. Trial registration Chinese Clinical Trial Registry, ChiCTR2300067657. Registered January 17, 2023. Keywords  Parkinson’s disease, Gait normalcy index, Intermittent theta burst stimulation, Action observation and
imitation training

Participants were randomly assigned in a 1:1 ratio to
receive two intervention conditions: iTBS + AOIT and
Sham + AOIT. Each phase consisted of five consecutive
daily intervention sessions, separated by a more than
four-week washout period (Fig.  1A). After the washout
period, participants crossed over to the alternate inter­
vention condition. Outcome measures were collected at
four time points (T1-T4), representing the pre- and post-
intervention evaluation for both phases. The primary outcomes were predefined as clinical
evaluation scores, including the Movement Disorder
Society–Unified Parkinson’s Disease Rating Scale Part
III (MDS-UPDRS III), the Berg Balance Scale (BBS), the
Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA), and the Hop­
kins Verbal Learning Test–Revised (HVLT-R), as these
directly tested the main hypothesis regarding the clinical
efficacy of the intervention. Secondary outcomes were
defined as all gait parameters, including the Dual-Task
Gait Normalcy Index (DTGNI), derived from wearable
sensors, as well as neurophysiological measures such as
motor evoked potential (MEP) and cortical silent period
(CSP), which were used to explore the underlying neuro­
plastic mechanisms. The study protocol was approved by the Ethics Com­
mittees of Tianjin University (TJUE-2023-003) and
Tianjin Medical University General Hospital (IRB2022-
KY-300), and was registered with the Chinese Clinical
Trial Registry (ChiCTR2300067657). All participants
provided written informed consent in accordance with
the Declaration of Helsinki. Randomization, blinding and allocation
An independent physician used SPSS 25.0 (IBM, USA) to
generate a random allocation sequence. All iTBS sessions
were administered by licensed physical therapists trained
in TMS procedures, who were not involved in assess­
ments. Two attending neurologists, who were blinded to
the group allocation, performed all clinical evaluations. Throughout the study, participants, interventionists, and
assessors remained blinded to stimulation condition. The
assessors (neurologists, gait technicians, and EEG opera­
tors) were completely independent of the intervention­
ists (licensed physical therapists) and were not involved
in stimulation delivery or initial data processing. The
separation ensured that assessor blinding was maintained
throughout the trial. In addition, all data were coded and
anonymized by participant ID before analysis.

(TA), localized using a patient-specific TMS cap com­
bined with an optical neuronavigation system. Given
the predominance of gait and postural impairments in
the PIGD subtype of PD, and previous evidence dem­

site was localized to the leg area of the M1. The rMT was
defined as the minimum intensity that elicits peak-to-
peak MEPs of ≥ 50 µV in at least 5 of 10 trials with the
TA muscle at rest. The aMT was defined as the minimum
intensity that elicited MEPs of ≥ 200 µV in at least 5 of

Ag/AgCl electrodes in a belly–tendon configuration. The stimulation intensity was set at 65% of rMT. Each
session delivered 20 trains of burst (3 pulses at 50 Hz per
burst, 5 Hz train frequency). Each 2-second train was fol­
lowed by an 8-second inter-train interval, totaling 600
pulses per hemisphere per session. Bilateral M1 stimu­
lation was performed daily, beginning with the more
affected side, with a 15-minute interval between hemi­

selected based on prior evidence [26, 27] to further facili­
tate neuroplastic effect in accelerated iTBS protocols. A double-cone coil (V1320TC; 13 cm diameter, 20 cm
height) was used to target deeper lower-limb M1 repre­
sentation (approximately 3 ~ 4 cm deep under the scalp). For the sham condition, a sham coil (KY07C) was used,
which replicates the characteristic clicking sound of the
active coil but does not generate a significant magnetic
field at the scalp surface. All other procedure aspects,
including coil placement, positioning duration, stimula­
tion parameters (1,200 pulses), inter-session interval, and
the presence of the operators, were kept identical to the
active iTBS protocol to maintain effective participant
blinding. Stimulation was delivered via a magnetic-elec­
tric stimulator (Mag TD 60; Yiruide, Wuhan, China). AOIT protocol
Action Observation and Imitation Training (AOIT) was
administered immediately following each iTBS or sham
stimulation session. The training consisted of a ten-form
Tai Chi routine adapted from the standardized 24-form
sequence. Each form involved 2-minute instructional
video and 5 min of participant imitation. This observa­
tion–imitation cycle was repeated five times followed by
15 min of guided physical practice. This final 15-minute
guided phase involved the therapist providing real-time
verbal and physical cues to correct and refine Tai Chi
forms, with particular emphasis on posture, balance, and
sequence transitions, thereby consolidating the motor
learning achieved during observation and imitation. The AOIT was conducted in group sessions at the Intel­
ligent Rehabilitation Laboratory of Tianjin University. Distinct Tai Chi sequences were used in each phase to
prevent learning effect while matched for complexity and
intensity. All participants were in the “Medication-ON”
state, ensuring that the only variable between phases was
the stimulation type (iTBS vs. Sham). Details of the AOIT
intervention are available in the Supplementary Table 1. Behavioral data acquisition and analysis
All assessments were conducted in the “Medication-ON”
state. Clinical evaluations included the MDS-UPDRS III, BBS, MoCA, HVLT-R, STT, and Stroop test. The HVLT-
R included three subtests: Immediate Recall, Delayed
Recall, and Recognition, primarily designed to assess
memory performance. The STT assessed attention and
executive functioning using STT-A and STT-B. The
Stroop test measured inhibitory control and cognitive
flexibility. To minimize practice effects, alternate forms
were applied to the most susceptible tests (MoCA and
HVLT-R), with Version A administered at T1 and T3 and
Version B at T2 and T4. This counterbalancing approach
helped reduce item-specific learning. For the STT and
Stroop Test, alternate forms were not used given their
relatively low susceptibility to practice effects. Gait data were collected using a wearable inertial
motion capture system (myoMOTION, Noraxon, USA)
at 100  Hz. Seven inertial measurement units (IMUs)
were placed on the pelvis, bilateral thighs, shanks, and
feet. Every participant completed a dual-task paradigm
involving walking five laps along a 5-meter walkway with
or without simultaneously performing a serial-3 subtrac­
tion task. Gait spatiotemporal parameters and joint kinematic
parameters during turning were derived via our vali­
dated gait assessment approach [28–30]. Spatiotemporal
parameters included: stride length, stride time, stance
time, swing time, and walking speed, where the stride
length and walking speed were normalized to each par­
ticipant’s height in order to reduce inter-subject variabil­
ity. Kinematic parameters included the range of motion
(ROM) of the pelvis, hips, knees, and ankles. In addition,
a Dual-Task Gait Normalcy Index (DTGNI) was cal­
culated to provide an overall assessment of gait perfor­
mance under dual-task conditions [31, 32]. Neurophysiological data acquisition and analysis
To assess long-term potentiation (LTP)-like plasticity, MEPs were recorded before (Pre) and at 5, 10, 15, 20,

the more affected side. The MEP amplitude was calcu­

amplitude at each post-iTBS time point was expressed
as a ratio to the baseline amplitude. The mean ratio of
all post MEPs was calculated to represent the overall
induced plasticity level [33]. GABA-mediated intracortical inhibition was evaluated
via the CSP during a 20% maximal voluntary TA contrac­
tion. Fifteen single-pulse TMS stimuli (at 120% of the

Based on prior study by Chung et al. [24] showing an

Participants
Eighteen participants were initially screened for eligi­
bility. Two patients were excluded due to the inability
to localize the TA motor hotspot, and one did not meet
the inclusion criteria. Ultimately, fifteen participants
were enrolled and randomized in a 1:1 ratio to receive
either real or sham stimulation (Fig.  2). The mean age
of the participants was 66.47 ± 3.34 years, with an aver­
age disease duration of 4.17 ± 2.45 years. Demographic
and clinical characteristics are summarized in Table  1. All participants completed the study between August and
December 2024. No adverse events were reported, and all
collected data were included in the analysis. Effects on clinical outcomes
Group differences are shown in Fig.  3. Patients in the
iTBS + AOIT group demonstrated significant improve­
ments in overall cognitive function (MoCA score: F = 5.294, P = 0.026), which was not observed in the
Sham + AOIT group. Specifically, in the iTBS + AOIT
group, delayed recall memory showed significant
improvement (HVLT-R-DR: F = 4.987, P = 0.034) and
Stroop-C task completion time was significantly reduced
(F = 7.023, P = 0.013). Although similar trends were noted
in the Sham + AOIT group, the changes did not reach sta­
tistical significance. Both groups exhibited significant improvements in
immediate recall (HVLT-R-IR: iTBS + AOIT F = 7.853, P = 0.009; Sham + AOIT F = 10.754, P = 0.003) and bal­
ance function (BBS: iTBS + AOIT F = 7.552, P = 0.011; Sham + AOIT F = 5.224, P = 0.030). Although MDS-
UPDRS III scores showed a downward trend in both
groups after intervention, these changes did not reach
statistical significance. Additional details are provided in
the Supplementary Table 2. Effects on electrophysiological outcomes
Figures 4A–B illustrate changes in MEP ratios across
post-intervention time points. In the iTBS + AOIT group, MEP amplitudes increased across all post-intervention
time points, with a significant elevation observed at
15  min post-intervention (F = 6.131, P = 0.020). No sig­
nificant changes were observed in the Sham + AOIT
group. CSP duration significantly increased only in the
iTBS + AOIT group (F = 4.655, P = 0.040) as shown in

group. Between-group differences in band-specific power

This study is the first to demonstrate that applying
iTBS over the leg area of M1 can enhance the rehabili­
tative effects of AOIT in patients with PIGD. While a
randomized, double-blind, crossover design was used, we
showed that iTBS-induced cortical plasticity significantly
potentiates AOIT’s benefits, particularly in improving
gait automaticity, balance, and cognitive function.
iTBS improves gait automaticity in patients with
PIGD. Consistent with previous studies demonstrating
AOIT’s benefits in stroke and PD population [12, 36], we
observed significant improvements in balance function
following AOIT across both conditions. However, only
the iTBS + AOIT group showed significant gains in dual-
task gait automaticity, indicating that iTBS as a facilitator
of gait control under cognitively demanding conditions. This improvement may be attributed to targeted stimu­
lation of the leg motor cortex using a double-cone coil,
which enables deeper and more focal modulation of
lower-limb motor areas [37–39]. Additionally, given
the strong functional connectivity between M1 and the
supplementary motor area (SMA) which is a key region
integral to postural control and gait planning, our
results align with previous evidence showing that high-
frequency rTMS over M1 or SMA can alleviate motor
symptoms in PD [40–42]. It is likely that iTBS not only
enhances local excitability in M1 but also exerts related
effects on SMA, thereby improving movement coordina­
tion and gait automaticity. In addition to motor outcomes, iTBS also enhanced
the cognitive benefits of AOIT. While both groups exhib­
ited improvements in immediate recall, only the iTBS +
AOIT condition led to significant gains in executive func­
tion, delayed recall, and global cognition. This effect may
be related to the cognitive demands of observing and
imitating complex movement sequences, which engage
prefrontal processes such as attention, working memory,
and cognitive flexibility [12, 43–46]. Figure 4D shows
that iTBS increased cortical excitability and modulated
resting-state EEG oscillation toward higher frequencies,
especially the frontal alpha power change is significantly
correlated with delayed recall improvement. The results
indicate the role of iTBS in augmenting the memory-
enhancing effects of AOIT. While cognitive improvement
is commonly targeted via the dorsolateral prefrontal cor­
tex (DLPFC) stimulation [47], our findings suggest that
bilateral M1 stimulation may also modulate cognitive
networks via trans-synaptic propagation [48]. The frontal
longitudinal system which connects M1 with premotor
cortex and prefrontal areas, may serve as a conduit for
this motor-cognitive interactions [21, 49]. The observed benefits of iTBS appear to be mediated
by enhanced cortical plasticity and improved motor
learning. AOIT is regarded as activating the mirror
neuron system and facilitates motor learning [50, 51],
but its efficiency may be limited in PD due to impaired
LTP/LTD-like mechanisms caused by dopamine deple­
tion and cortico-basal ganglia dysfunctions [14, 52, 53]. Table 1  Demographic and clinical characteristics of participants

Our results demonstrated that five sessions of bilateral
M1 iTBS significantly increased MEP amplitude and
prolonged CSP durations (Fig. 4A-C), indicating both
increased excitability and improved GABAergic inhibi­
tion. These neurophysiological changes suggest a restora­
tion of plasticity, possibility through modulation of key
neurotransmitters such as dopamine, GABA, and gluta­
mate [54–58]. Furthermore, improvements in cortical plasticity were
significantly correlated with enhancements in dual-task
gait performance and MDS-UPDRS III, supporting the
hypothesis that iTBS enhances AOIT’s efficacy by facili­
tating motor learning. Compared to prior studies using
a figure-of-eight coil and applied 80% rMT over M1
[59], our use of a double-cone coil at 65% rMT provided
deeper, leg-specific stimulation, with greater tolerability,
which may explain the comparable or superior outcomes
at lower intensities in our study. Limitations
This study has several limitations. First, the sample size
was relatively small. Although a crossover design and
power analysis helped minimize inter-individual variabil­
ity, larger multicenter trials are needed to confirm gen­
eralizability. Second, despite improvements of cognition
and gait, changes in MDS-UPDRS III scores did not reach
statistical significance. This may be due to the relatively
short intervention duration in early-stage PIGD. Further
studies should explore optimal iTBS dosing, duration,
and personalization based on disease phenotype or neu­
rophysiological profiles. Third, while strict double-blind
procedures were implemented, the possibility of inadver­
tent unblinding cannot be entirely excluded and should
be carefully monitored in future trials. Finally, while we
identified correlations between plasticity and behavior,
causal mechanisms remain to be clarified through neuro­
imaging or pharmacological modulation. Conclusion
This study demonstrates that bilateral iTBS over the
leg motor cortex can effectively augment the impact of
AOIT on gait, balance, and cognitive function in early-
stage PIGD patients. By preconditioning the motor cor­
tex, iTBS may create a more conducive environment for
neuroplastic modulation by motor-cognitive interaction
training. These findings support the use of non-invasive
neuromodulation to augment neurorehabilitation in
PIGD. Future research should focus on optimizing stimu­
lation parameters, identifying biomarkers of responsive­
ness, and developing personalized protocols to enhance
