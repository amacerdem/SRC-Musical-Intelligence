Abstract
Chronic pain affects over 30% of the global population, yet non-pharmacological interventions with clear
neurophysiological mechanisms remain limited. While virtual reality (VR) and music therapy independently show
promise in pain management, the neural oscillatory underpinnings of rhythm-synchronized audiomotor integration
in VR therapy remain poorly understood. This study aimed to investigate whether music-synchronized virtual reality
(MSVR) enhances analgesia through distinct neural mechanisms compared to conventional distraction-based VR or
non-immersive interventions. 90 healthy adults (45 female, 22 ± 2 years) were randomized to a single session of: (1)
MSVR (rhythm-synchronized visuomotor tasks with music), (2) conventional VR (identical tasks with white noise), or
(3) non-immersive 2D control. Pressure pain thresholds (PPT) were measured at five movement-generating muscle
sites. Conditioned pain modulation (CPM) efficiency was assessed using PPT and cold-water stimulation. 64-channel
EEG recorded theta, alpha, beta, and gamma oscillations. Outcomes included PPT changes, CPM efficiency, cold
pain intensity/unpleasantness, and neural spectral power. MSVR significantly increased PPTs across all sites (15–25%
vs. control, P < 0.001; superior to VR in upper limbs, P < 0.05) and enhanced CPM efficiency by 18% (vs. control, P = 0.010; vs. VR, P = 0.046). Cold pain intensity decreased by 22% with MSVR (P < 0.05 vs. both groups). MSVR
uniquely enhanced parietal alpha oscillations during and post-intervention (P = 0.001). MSVR also induced greater
immersion and realism than VR (P < 0.001). MSVR significantly enhances endogenous pain inhibition and elevates
pain thresholds more effectively than conventional VR or 2D interventions, primarily through rhythmic audiomotor
integration modulating parietal alpha-oscillation modulation. These findings suggest MSVR as a scalable digital
therapeutic strategy for pain management. Keywords  Digital healthcare, Analgesia, Electroencephalography, Virtual reality, Music therapy

Introduction
Chronic pain affects more than 30% of the global popula­
tion and has become a serious public health issue given
its substantial individual and economic burden [1, 2]. As
an integrative phenomenon, pain emerges from dynamic
interactions among sensory, affective, and cognitive pro­
cesses, which are regulated by the body’s endogenous
pain modulation system [3]. This system governs the
balance between pain facilitation and inhibition. One
key paradigm for assessing the function of this system
is conditioned pain modulation (CPM), which serves as
a critical regulator of pain perception [4]. Neurophysi­
ologically, CPM efficacy reflects thalamocortical integra­
tion of nociceptive signals through frequency-specific
neural oscillations [5]. Spatiotemporal patterns of theta
(4–7 Hz), alpha (8–13 Hz), beta (14–29 Hz), and gamma
(30–100  Hz) oscillations have been implicated in both
acute pain perception and chronic maladaptive plasticity
in pain processing [6–9]. Virtual reality (VR) has emerged as a neuromodula­
tory adjunct in digital medicine, leveraging multisensory
immersion to engage motor cortical networks during
rehabilitation [10]. While conventional VR primarily uti­
lizes visual distraction mechanisms (e.g., nature scenes)
to reduce pain intensity in conditions like postopera­
tive recovery and burn care [11–13], emerging evidence
suggests rhythmically synchronized motor engagement
may amplify analgesia through sensorimotor integration
[14]. A recent study found that VR involves a top-down
pain modulation mechanism, and different VR scenes
are associated with distinct neural oscillations, particu­
larly involving alpha and gamma oscillations [15]. Music
therapy has also been utilized to optimize motor per­
formance, emotion and analgesia via rhythmic entrain­
ment [16–20]. However, the neurophysiological synergy
of music-synchronized VR (MSVR) remains unexplored
in physically active cohorts, despite its potential for pain
management. In this study, we investigate whether MSVR outper­
forms conventional VR and non-immersive interven­
tions in enhancing endogenous pain inhibition in healthy
adults, and elucidate the mediating role of frequency-
specific neural oscillations. Materials and methods
Study design and participants
Participants were randomly allocated to one of three
experimental conditions: (1) Music-Synchronized Virtual
Reality (MSVR): An immersive, interactive intervention
involving rhythm-synchronized visuomotor tasks with
music. (2) Conventional Virtual Reality (VR): An immer­
sive, interactive intervention involving identical visuomo­
tor tasks as the MSVR group, but with music replaced
by neutral white noise. (3) Non-Immersive Control (2D): A passive control condition involving viewing the same
visual sequence as the VR group on a 2D tablet, without
sound or interactivity. Analgesic effects associated with
distinct VR interventions were investigated for mediation
by neural oscillatory activity across specific frequency
bands in discrete brain regions (Fig.  1a). Additionally,
mediation analysis was employed to test whether the
analgesic effects of MSVR were mediated by changes in
specific neural oscillations. A total of 90 participants were included in this study
(45 females; mean age 22 ± 2 years). Inclusion criteria:
aged 18–35 years, right-handed, physically active (adher­
ence to WHO physical activity guidelines confirmed by
International Physical Activity Questionnaire Short Form
[IPAQ] [21]), with no history of chronic pain or neuro­
musculoskeletal injuries. Exclusion criteria: hearing
impairment, neurological disorders, use of analgesics or
psychotropic medications within 24 h, or failure to meet
WHO’s moderate-to-vigorous physical activity recom­
mendations (≥ 600 MET-min/week). All procedures were
approved by the Ethics Committee of Shanghai Univer­
sity of Sport and registered with the Chinese Clinical
Trial Registry (registry number: ChiCTR2300073582). This study adhered to the principles of the Declaration of
Helsinki. Experimental conditions
This experiment included three conditions, namely,
music-synchronized virtual reality (MSVR) intervention, VR intervention, and a control (2D) condition (Fig. 1b). The virtual environments were displayed on PICO 4
(Pico Immersive, Beijing, China). In the MSVR condition,
participants engaged in active music-guided visuomotor
tasks requiring precise synchronization of motor actions
with musical rhythms. Participants were instructed
to execute virtual target-hitting actions precisely syn­
chronized to musical beats, creating timing-dependent
visuomotor coordination. The technical implementa­
tion of this synchronization was achieved through a pro­
grammed unity between auditory and visual elements: all
musical selections were standardized to a tempo of 100–
120 beats per minute (BPM) using digital audio software
(Audacity, The Audacity Team), and the appearance of
each visual target was programmatically locked to occur
on the musical beat through custom development in the
Unity platform (Unity Technologies). This beat-synchro­
nized block-slashing paradigm operationalizes rhythmic
audiomotor integration, a core mechanism hypothesized
to underlie music-induced analgesia [22]. The virtual
scene for the MSVR intervention includes elements, such
as rocks, grass, blue sky, and white clouds. The music
selection included classical, electronic, and rock genres
to provide a rich auditory experience, and the volume
levels controlled between 45 and 60 dB, which has been

VR condition, identical visuomotor tasks were performed

motor/cognitive demands. The control group underwent
identical tasks presented on a 10.8-inch tablet, control­
ling for visual content. All interventions lasted 25  min
and had a 5-minute warm-up session. After the interven­
tion, participants in the MSVR and VR groups completed
the Gold–Rizzo Immersion and Presence (GRIP) Inven­
tory to assess their degree of immersion and realism of
the different VR experiences [24]. Pressure pain thresholds

analgesic effects: trapezius, deltoid, biceps brachii, fore­

sites were selected to represent a standard distribution of
upper and lower limb muscles commonly used in quan­
titative sensory testing. All measurements were con­
ducted using Wagner FPX-25 algometers (Wagner Force, USA) following uniform principles to ensure accuracy
and reproducibility, with PPT measured in Newtons and
the probe surface area standardized to 1  cm². Prior to
the test, the participants were clearly informed that the

site to ensure reliability. CPM
The efficiency of CPM serves as an indicator of a per­
son’s innate pain-inhibiting capabilities [5]. The efficiency
of CPM, particularly induced by cold water stimulation
and mechanical pressure, exhibits stability and high
reliability [28]. Consequently, this study utilized PPT
as the experimental stimulus and cold water stimula­
tion as the conditioned stimulus. This involved an initial
PPT measurement at the biceps brachii (T1), followed
by application of the conditioning stimulus—immer­
sion of the non-dominant hand in a cold-water bath. Immediately upon withdrawal from the cold water, the
PPT at the biceps brachii was reassessed (T2) to calcu­
late CPM efficiency using the standard formula: CPM =
(T2 - T1) / T1 × 100%. The conditioning stimulus con­
sisted of immersing the participant’s non-dominant hand
up to the wrist in a circulating cold-water bath main­
tained at 10  °C. Participants were instructed to keep
their hand immersed for 60 s, or until the pain became
intolerable (defined as a rating ≥ 7 on a 0–10 Numerical
Rating Scale [NRS]). Pain intensity and unpleasantness
of the cold stimulus were rated immediately after with­
drawal using the 0–10 NRS (0 = no pain/not unpleasant
at all; 10 = the worst pain imaginable/extremely unpleas­
ant). Pain modulation can be classified as pain inhibition
(CPM > 0), pain facilitation (CPM < 0), or no significant
effect (CPM = 0) [29]. Procedure
The experimental procedure, outlined in Fig.  1d, was
designed to systematically evaluate the intervention’s
effects on affective state, pain perception, and neu­
ral activity. Randomization used computer-generated
blocks, stratified by sex. Participants were blinded to
group hypotheses; testers were blinded during PPT and
CPM assessments. Following informed consent, par­
ticipants underwent a standardized sequence of base­
line assessments. First, the Positive and Negative Affect
Schedule (PANAS) was administered to quantify baseline
mood state. This was immediately followed by a 3-minute
resting-state electroencephalography (EEG) recording to
establish a baseline measure of neural oscillatory activity. Subsequently, baseline mechanical pain sensitivity was
assessed by measuring Pressure Pain Thresholds (PPT) at
all five predetermined muscle sites using a standardized
algometry protocol. To evaluate the efficiency of endog­
enous pain inhibitory pathways, the Conditioned Pain
Modulation (CPM) protocol was then administered. Par­
ticipants also rated the intensity and unpleasantness of
the cold-water stimulus. Following these baseline measurements, participants
were randomized to one of the three experimental inter­
ventions for a 25-minute session, during which EEG was
continuously recorded. EEG signals were also recorded
during all interventions. The analysis focused on data
from the 15-18-minute interval, during which neural
engagement may stabilize after initial adaptation. The
identical sequence of assessments was repeated immedi­
ately post-intervention. Finally, participants in the MSVR
and VR groups completed the Gold-Rizzo Immersion
and Presence (GRIP) Inventory to provide subjective
measures of their virtual reality experience. EEG data collection and processing
EEG signals were captured with a 64-channel Ag-AgCl
scalp electrodes (BrainAmp, Brain Products, Ger­
many; bandpass: 0.01–100  Hz; sampling rate: 1000  Hz)
arranged in accordance with the International 10–20
system. An additional electrode was positioned approxi­
mately 10  mm below the right eye to record electro­
oculographic signals and monitor eye movements. All
electrode impedances were maintained below 10 kΩ. EEG data were subjected to preprocessing utilizing the
open-source EEGLAB toolbox in MATLAB [30]. After
preprocessing, EEG data underwent Fourier transforms
for each trial by using Welch power spectral density
method with a Hamming window (pwelch function); the
data were used to calculate the power spectral densities
(PSD) of theta, alpha, beta, and gamma oscillations and
generate values for each region of interest (ROI) within
the 1–100 Hz range [31]. The spectral data of individual
participants were subsequently averaged to obtain group-
level EEG spectral distributions, resulting in frequency
plots for 1–100 Hz. After averaging across all trials under
the three conditions, the spectral information was fur­
ther averaged to create an overall average spectral map,
leading to topographic maps across multiple frequency
by defining specific frequency ranges. Corresponding
spectral power density values were extracted for statis­
tical analysis by defining specific frequency ranges. The
selected ROIs included the parietal cortex (Cz, C1, C2),
occipital cortex (P3, Pz, P4, O1, Oz, O2), left frontal cor­
tex (AF3, F3, F5, FC5), and right frontal cortex (AF4, F4, F6, FC6) synchronized on extensive evidence demon­
strating their correlation with pain perception [32]. Page 5 of 11

Statistical analysis
Statistical analyses were conducted using SPSS 26.0,
and the results were visualized using GraphPad Prism
8.0 and MATLAB (MathWorks, MA, USA). A priori
power analysis was conducted using G*Power 3.1. We
assumed an effect size f = 0.21 (based on a prior study)
[33]a statistical power of 0.8, and an α level of 0.05, which
yielded a minimum total sample size of 75. Randomiza­
tion was performed using computer-generated blocked
randomization with a fixed block size of 6, stratified by
sex. Allocation sequences were concealed via sequen­
tially numbered, opaque, sealed envelopes prepared by
an independent statistician. Envelopes were opened
sequentially after baseline assessments by a research
assistant uninvolved in outcome evaluation. Participants
and intervention administrators could not be blinded
due to the nature of VR exposure, but outcome asses­
sors remained blinded during PPT/CPM testing and
EEG analysis. For participants’ characteristics and pre-
intervention outcomes, categorical variables were evalu­
ated by Chi-square test, and continuous variables were
assessed with one-way ANOVA. The results are pre­
sented as number (percentage) or mean (SD). Baseline
differences between the three groups for all behavioral
measures (PPT, CPM, cold pain intensity, unpleasantness, PANAS) were assessed using one-way ANOVA. The pri­
mary analysis for the effect of the intervention on these
behavioral outcomes was then conducted on the change
scores (post- minus pre-) using a one-way ANOVA
across the three groups (MSVR, VR, Control). Post-hoc
multiple comparisons were conducted with Bonfer­
roni correction where appropriate. For the power spec­
tral density (PSD) values, which were obtained at three
time points (pre-, during- and post-), a two-way (group ×
time) repeated-measures ANOVA with between-subjects
factor group (MSVR, VR, Control) and within-subjects
factor time was performed to examine interaction and
main effects. The GRIP Inventory outcomes between the
MSVR and VR groups were compared using independent
sample t-test. Spearman correlation analysis was used to
explore relationships among emotional status, pain per­
ception, and neural oscillation. For significant correla­
tions, mediation analyses were performed to examine
the role of neural oscillation in various brain regions as
mediators between MSVR (independent variable) and
pain perception (dependent variable). Statistical signifi­
cance was set at P < 0.05. To test our hypothesis that the effect of the interven­
tion on pain outcomes is explained by changes in neu­
ral activity, mediation analyses were conducted using
the Multilevel Mediation and Moderation (M3) Toolbox
[34]. This approach allows us to determine if a significant
portion of MSVR’s effect on CPM (the outcome) oper­
ates through its ability to alter neural oscillations (the
mediator). The independent variable (X) represented the
different intervention groups, while the dependent vari­
able (Y) was the conditioned pain modulation (CPM). The total effect of X on Y, indicating the influence of
MSVR on CPM, was labeled as path c. The direct effect
of X on Y, controlling for mediator M, was referred to as
path c’. The remaining three paths of interest included the
relationship from X to M (path a), from M to Y (path b),
and the mediation effect (path a*b) [35]. This mediation
analysis enabled us to assess the impact of spontaneous
neural oscillations in different brain regions on the rela­
tionship between MSVR and perceived CPM efficiency. Results
This study included 90 participants, with 30 in each of
the MSVR, VR, and control groups. All participants
completed the assigned intervention and all post-test
assessments; there were no dropouts. Furthermore, the
research assistants conducting all PPT and CPM assess­
ments were blinded to group allocation. The characteris­
tics of participants did not significantly differ among the
three groups (Table 1). The analysis of pre-intervention
outcomes indicated the lack of statistically significant
intergroup differences in PPT at various sites, cold pain
intensity, cold pain unpleasantness, CPM, positive emo­
tions, and negative emotions (P > 0.05, eTable 1). At the
biceps site, both PPT and CPM changes showed sig­
nificant group differences (Fig.  2). The MSVR group
showed significant improvements over the control
group (P < 0.001) in the trapezius (1.31 ± 0.16), del­
toid (0.91 ± 0.17), biceps brachii (1.09 ± 0.16), forearm
(2.09 ± 0.19), and rectus femoris (1.33 ± 0.25). The VR
group also demonstrated significant effects over the
control in the deltoid (0.42 ± 0.17, P = 0.022), biceps bra­
chii (0.40 ± 0.16, P = 0.027), and forearm (0.88 ± 0.19, P < 0.001) but not in the trapezius or rectus femoris. Notably, MSVR outperformed VR in movement-gener­
ating muscles of upper limb (P < 0.05), not in the rectus
femoris, where the difference did not reach significance
(P = 0.297). Additionally, the differences in CPM among
the three groups were statistically significant (F = 5.185, P = 0.007). The MSVR group had significantly higher
CPM efficiency than the control (P = 0.010) and the VR
group (P = 0.046). Significant group differences in cold pain intensity were
observed (F = 3.993, P = 0.022), with the MSVR group

significantly enhanced following the MSVR intervention,
and beta oscillations in the left and right frontal cortex
significantly changed during the intervention (eFigs.  1,

emotional state were correlated with alterations in alpha
oscillations across different brain regions (P < 0.05). Notably, significantly stronger positive correlations were

oscillations in the parietal cortex significantly mediated
the relationship between the MSVR intervention and the
improvement of CPM (Fig. 3c). Additionally, the results
of the GRIP Inventory indicated that the MSVR inter­
vention significantly outperformed the VR intervention
in terms of immersion (p = 0.001) and realism (p < 0.001,
eTable 2). Discussion
This study found that the multisensory experience of
MSVR intervention-which combines rhythmic audio­
motor integration, physical activity, and immersive VR-
effectively increased PPT in healthy individuals. The
intervention demonstrated a hypoalgesic effect that
extended to both upper limb muscles, such as the trape­
zius and biceps brachii, and lower limb muscles, includ­
ing the rectus femoris. By contrast, the VR intervention
only improved the PPT of the upper limb muscles. The
extended analgesic effect to lower limb muscles in MSVR
may reflect the synergy of enhanced audiomotor integra­
tion, where rhythmic auditory cues synchronize motor
output across proximal and distal musculature, coupled
with the general effects of elevated motor engagement
and high immersion within the VR environment. This
differs from the passive control condition, which lacked
motor and immersive components. Thus, the superiority
of MSVR in modulating pain likely stems from a combi­
nation of specific (rhythmic synchronization) and non-
specific (motor activity, immersion) factors, rather than
music synchronization alone. By syncing motor output
to auditory cues, MSVR may recalibrate sensorimotor
timing disrupted in pain conditions, potentially explain­
ing its superiority over passive VR distraction in modu­
lating systemic pain. While our study focused on this
mechanism of rhythmic audiomotor integration, it is
important to note that music therapy exerts its benefits
through multiple pathways. Beyond synchronization, the
immersive VR environment and the music itself likely
contributed to the observed effects through well-estab­
lished mechanisms such as distraction and emotional
modulation, which are also fundamental to music ther­
apy [36]. For example, music can positively affect emo­
tions, relieve stress, and relax muscles, which may have
synergized with the rhythmic component to enhance
overall analgesia [37]. Research on the central mechanisms of VR analgesia is
currently limited, and this study focuses on the sponta­
neous generation of endogenous theta, alpha, beta, and
gamma oscillations during interventions. The respon­
siveness of different frequency bands can accurately
reflect neural activity and serves as a stable pain assess­
ment metric with minimal influencing factors [38]. VR
environments synchronized on mindfulness scene can
significantly enhance alpha oscillation activity; as such,
multisensory information provided by the MSVR inter­
vention, including its immersive and motor-engaging
properties, not only has a fundamental distracting func­
tion but may also induce a state similar to self-focused
mindfulness, which could be more effective in reduc­
ing pain sensitivity than distraction-synchronized VR
scene [15]. This finding may be related to the height­
ened arousal levels prompted by music because auditory
stimuli can enhance alpha oscillation activity. Previ­
ous research demonstrated that increased arousal can
influence cognitive processes and enhance attention to
visual stimuli [39]. Elevations in alpha oscillations after
MSVR intervention are typically linked to relaxation
and internal focus often induced by music and may also
enhance changes in emotional arousal [40]. Another
work explored alpha oscillations in the context of pain
stimuli among healthy subjects and provide evidence that
reductions in alpha oscillations correlate with increased
sensitivity to severe pain [41]. Furthermore, mediation
analysis revealed that parietal alpha oscillations signifi­
cantly mediated the relationship between MSVR and
CPM, suggesting a specific neural pathway for its analge­
sic effect. It is important to note that exploratory analy­
ses of other brain regions and frequency bands did not
yield significant mediation effects. While this mechanism
explains a portion of the benefit, other unmeasured fac­
tors such as emotional or cognitive changes likely also
contribute to the overall effect. Gamma oscillations are associated with sustained
attention to external stimuli and a sense of immersion
[42]. In this study, MSVR and VR conditions with stron­
ger immersion showed larger gamma oscillations than
the control. The enhanced gamma oscillations under
different VR conditions may be linked to the high level
of immersion, primarily driven by the distracting func­
tion of the virtual environment. Engagement with con­
tinuous multisensory VR stimuli occupies attentional
resources and potentially alters spontaneous gamma

oscillation activity, which in turn may impact neural
activity in pain-related brain regions and modify pain
perception [43]. The lack of post-intervention differ­
ences in gamma oscillations across the three conditions
suggests that MSVR and VR may enhance an immedi­
ate gamma oscillation response in real-time during VR
exposure. This finding highlights that different neural
oscillation frequency bands offer spatially and temporally
distinct, complementary insights into pain processing
[44]. Enhanced beta and theta oscillations in the frontal
cortex reflect increased cognitive and executive process­
ing in individuals exposed to a music-synchronized vir­
tual environment. This phenomenon may result from the
complex sensory stimulation of music and demands great
cognitive and emotional engagement [45]. Hence, MSVR
may enhance pain modulation capabilities effectively by
amplifying cognitive and emotional processing linked to
beta oscillations and by promoting relaxation and atten­
tion associated with theta oscillations [46]. The correlation between spontaneous alpha oscilla­
tions and subjective pain perception may reveal how
the brain modulates pain through higher cortical pro­
cesses. The MSVR intervention, which integrates
music-synchronized motor activity within an immersive
virtual reality, may enhance this regulatory mechanism,
thereby improving pain modulation. It is plausible that
the combined experience of rhythmic movement, musi­
cal engagement, and immersion during the MSVR inter­
vention leads to heightened engagement and emotional
pleasure, which in turn is associated with increased spon­
taneous alpha oscillations in the parietal cortex. This
increase in alpha oscillations could enhance endogenous
pain modulation pathways by affecting the dynamic flow
of pain-related information and ultimately improving
CPM effectiveness. Limitations
This study has several limitations. First, CPM efficiency
in healthy individuals may not generalize to chronic pain
populations, where CPM dysfunction is prevalent (e.g.,
fibromyalgia). Second, the use of only resting-state data
may have overlooked the effects of MSVR on cognitive
tasks or emotional states. Third, we focused on resting-
state EEG to capture sustained, intervention-induced
changes in spontaneous neural oscillatory activity. Future
studies could employ task-based EEG recordings during
the application of painful stimuli to also capture evoked
neural responses and their modulation by VR. Lastly, the
impact of music selection and individual differences on
pain modulation was not extensively examined. While
rhythmic movement in MSVR aligns with music therapy
paradigms, future studies should include an active con­
trol with non-musical motor tasks to disentangle exercise
effects. Conclusion
In this study, the multisensory MSVR intervention,
which integrates rhythmic audiomotor tasks within an
immersive environment, significantly enhanced pain
modulation. These effects were mechanistically linked
to alpha-oscillation-mediated sensorimotor integration,
suggesting MSVR’s potential as a novel neuromodulatory
approach for pain management. The synergy of rhyth­
mic auditory cues, motor engagement, and immersive
distraction provides a behaviorally grounded strategy to
mitigate pain while promoting positive emotion-a dual
benefit critical for recovery contexts. While the specific
contribution of rhythmic synchronization is a key find­
ing, the overall analgesic effect likely also benefited from
the general mechanisms of enhanced physical activity
and multisensory immersion. As an emerging, low-cost, and portable therapeutic
modality, this form of VR intervention that combines
motor activity with synchronized auditory stimuli can
be conveniently applied to pain management across
diverse cultural backgrounds. Understanding the anal­
gesic benefits of MSVR and its neural mechanisms can
guide the future development of VR therapy that incor­
porates quantitative multisensory inputs to improve pain
management. Supplementary Information
