Abstract
BackgroundвЂ‚ A biological model of auditory learning posits that the auditory cortex interacts with the cognitive,
sensorimotor, and reward systems to improve sound learning in real-world listening. Congenital amusia is a
neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by deficits in fine-grained pitch discrimination. Although previous
studies have investigated the auditory processing, sensorimotor and cognitive abilities of amusia, the characteristics
of musical reward in amusia remain unclear. MethodвЂ‚ We recruited 44 individuals with congenital amusia (22 females; 19.66вЂ‰В±вЂ‰1.92years), identified with
the Montreal Battery of Evaluation of Amusia (MBEA; cut-off в‰¤вЂ‰21.5) and 44 matched controls(23 females;
19.61вЂ‰В±вЂ‰1.65years). Participants completed the Barcelona Music Reward Questionnaire(BMRQ) to assess derived
pleasure from engagement in musical activities, and the Multiple Mood Scale (MMS) to evaluate emotional
experiences (heightened, tragic, romantic, blithe) elicited by minor- and major-key music. ResultsвЂ‚ The amusia group reported lower overall BMRQ scores and lower scores on all five BMRQ subscales (social
reward, mood regulation, emotional evocation, sensory-motor and musical seeking) compared to controls. For the
musical emotion task, both groups experienced similar musical emotions for major music, whereas the amusia group
rated minor-key music as significantly less tragic and more blithe. Additionally, overall BMRQ scores were negatively
associated with blithe and heightened ratings for major-key music, whereas these associations were positive in
controls. ConclusionsвЂ‚ These findings suggest individuals with amusia exhibit music-specific deficits in the pleasure derived
from music, together with an alteration in emotion experience to minor-key music. Overall, the findings indicate an
alteration in the use of pitch-derived cues when evaluating musical emotions and their relation to reward. From a
reward perspective, these findings guide neuroimaging studies toward clarifying how pitch-based cues influence
music-induced emotion experiences in congenital amusia. Altered music emotion experiences

Background
Congenital amusia is a neurodevelopmental disorder
characterized primarily by impairments in music pitch
discrimination, with an estimated prevalence of 1.5вЂ“4%
in the general population [1, 2]. Although pitch deficits
are the prominent feature of amusia, they may not fully
represent the underlying neural or cognitive mechaВ­
nisms. For example, individuals with amusia exhibit
deficits in the discrimination of small pitch differences in
music, whereas discrimination of large pitch differences
can remain preserved [3]. This suggests that amusia may
involve a specific difficulty in processing subtle pitch
variations, rather than a generalized auditory processing
disorder. Kraus and White-Schwoch [4] proposed a bioВ­
logical model of auditory learning that may help explain
these specific difficulties in amusia. According to their
model, auditory learning involves the interaction among
the auditory cortex and cognitive, sensorimotor and
reward systems, which collaborate to guide and improve
sound learning in real-world situations. Deficits in any
of these interacting systems could lead to the characterВ­
istic difficulties in pitch discrimination seen in amusia. While other hypotheses suggest that amusia may arise
from dysfunction in the auditory cortex [5], sensorimotor
disorder [6] or cognitive learning deficits [7], their model
provides a more integrated framework. It suggests that
these mechanisms may not be independent but rather
interact to impair auditory learning processes. A central component of musical reward is the abilВ­
ity of music to induce emotions. However, studies on
emotional processing in congenital amusia have showed
inconsistent results. On one hand, several studies suggest
that individuals with amusia exhibit reduced sensitivity
in recognizing and experiencing musical emotions. For
example, Jiang et al., [8] evaluated the emotional intenВ­
sity of Western and Indian melodies in both amusia and
control groups. While the control group rated Western
melodies as less emotionally intense than Indian meloВ­
dies, no such significant difference was found in the amuВ­
sia group. This suggests that the differences of emotional
sensitivity in amusia may be context-dependent, potenВ­
tially influenced by cultural factors or the type of music. McDonald and Stewart [1] observed that individuals
with amusia engaged in less musical activities and disВ­
played apathy towards music, a finding later supported
by Cousineau, who reported that most individuals with
amusia exhibited lower desire to participate in music
activities [9]. On the other hand, some researchers argued
that while congenital amusia primarily affects the recogВ­
nition of emotions, the emotional experience may remain
unaffected. Leveque et al. [10] examined the emotional
recognition and emotional experience using musical
stimuli and facial expressions. Their results showed that
while individuals with amusia were impaired in recognizВ­
ing emotion of musical stimuli, their emotional experiВ­
ence, specifically the emotional intensity they felt, was
similar to that of the control group. Furthermore, other
studies have found that, under laboratory conditions, the
recognition of music emotions in individuals with amusia
did not differ significantly from that of controls. Most of
them were able to identify four emotions such as happiВ­
ness, sadness, fear and peacefulness, conveyed through
unfamiliar music clips [11]. These results may indicate
changes in the musical reward processing systems of conВ­
genital amusia. The musical reward effects manifest in multiple
aspects, influencing emotional experiences, regulating
emotions, promoting sensorimotor coordination, serving
as a tool for social bonding, and stimulating individual
interest and motivation. These effects are primarily mediВ­
ated by dopaminergic pathways in the mesolimbic sysВ­
tem, including structures such as the nucleus accumbens
and ventral striatum. These regions release dopamine in
response to pleasurable music, contributing to feelings
of enjoyment and motivation further engagement with
music [12]. Emotional engagement with music plays a
crucial role in the reward process, as dopamine release
is triggered not only by sensory stimuli but also by emoВ­
tional experiences associated with music. For instance,
structural elements of music, such as the dichotomy
between major and minor keys, directly shape emotional
responses, thus modulating reward processing [13]. In
Western tonal music, the major key (characterized by a
bright tonic-third interval) is typically associated with
positive emotions such as joy and warmth, while the
minor key (featuring a lowered third) evokes negative
emotions like sadness or tension [13]. This emotional
contrast is based on the interplay between acoustic
properties and culturally learned associations, which
together activate fronto-striatal circuits, integrating the
prefrontal cortex and striatum to generate motivational
and affective responses [13вЂ“17]. However, disruptions
in emotional recognition, as seen in some individuals
with amusia, may impair this process, reducing intrinsic
reward value by diminishing sensitivity to structural-
emotional cues like keys [12, 18]. This study aimed to comprehensively investigate musiВ­
cal reward sensitivity and music-induced emotional
experiences in individuals with congenital amusia from
the perspective of reward. We assessed pleasure derived
from engagement in musical activities with the BarceВ­
lona Music Reward Questionnaire (BMRQ) across five

Musical reward and anhedonia measures
Participants completed the Chinese version of the BMRQ
[26], which measures overall music-reward sensitivity;
higher total scores indicate greater pleasure derived from
musical experiences. The BMRQ evaluates five dimenВ­
sions: social reward, which refers to the pleasure derived
from sharing music with others, such as group participaВ­
tion in musical activities or experiencing music in a social
context; mood regulation, which involves using music
to regulate oneвЂ™s emotions, including enhancing positive
emotions or alleviating negative feelings; emotion evoВ­
cation, which is experiencing strong emotions, such as
joy, sadness, or excitement, elicited by music; sensory-
motor, which involves physical responses to music, such
as dancing, tapping, or other forms of motor activity in
response to rhythm and melody, and musical seeking,
which refers to the intrinsic drive to seek out and engage
with musical experiences for personal enjoyment, emoВ­
tional fulfillment, or self-expression. The Chinese verВ­
sion of BMRQ demonstrated good internal consistency
(CronbachвЂ™s О±вЂ‰=вЂ‰0.84) [27]. To assess individual differences in anhedonia and
motivation, additional measures included the Chinese

tions of musical keys, whereby major keys are generally
perceived as bright, uplifting and associated with posiВ­
tive affect, while minor keys are associated with somber,
melancholic, or dramatic emotions, reflecting negative
valence and heighted tension [13, 31, 32]. However, group differences emerged for minor-key
music. Compared to controls, the amusia group reported
lower tragic and higher blithe ratings to minor excerpts,
with no group differences under major-key music. This
combination of findings indicates an altered processing
of negative emotional cues conveyed by the minor key. Rather than reflecting an absence of emotional experiВ­
ence, this pattern may point to an alternative appraisal in
congenital amusia [28]. When subtle pitch variations that
convey negative valence are hard to access, listeners may
rely on other musical feature leading to a more neutral
or positive interpretation. Specifically, the findings could
reflect a sequence in amusia have reduced access to fine
grained pitch information, the cue to valence mapping
become less reliable, and attention is reallocated toward
other features. Although romantic emotion ratings did not differ
between groups, participants experienced more romantic
emotions in response to major-key music, consistent with
prior studies on musical valence [33]. Major-key music
is typically associated with positive and pleasant affect,
including happiness and warmth, which aligns with the
core attributes of the romantic dimension such as affecВ­
tion, intimacy, and tenderness. The romantic dimension
may depend less on fine-grained pitch perception and
more on global musical features, such as tempo or harВ­
monic richness, as well as on listenersвЂ™ life experiences
[33]. In major-key contexts, the emotional clarity conВ­
veyed by harmonic structure and rhythmic organization
appears sufficient for listeners, including those with conВ­
genital amusia, to experience romantic emotions despite
reduced access to subtle pitch cues. Heightened emotions, which index general arousal or
excitement, showed no main effects of group or key, sugВ­
gesting that arousal-based responses to music remain
largely intact in amusia. From the perspective of cogniВ­
tive appraisal theory [34], emotions reflect evaluative
interpretations rather than direct readouts of acoustic
input. Impaired pitch perception in amusia may therefore
disrupt appraisals of emotional valence particularly for
minor-key music, where recognition relies more heavily
on subtle pitch cues. This account explains why the amuВ­
sia group diverged from controls in pitch-related dimenВ­
sions, such as tragic and blithe ratings under minor-key
excerpts, while showing preserved responses in romantic
and heightened dimensions. Dissociation between musical reward sensitivity and
emotional experience among individuals with amusia
Correlation analyses revealed additional differences
between groups. In the control group, higher BMRQ
scores were positively related to blithe and heightened
ratings for major-key music, which supports the view
that motivation to engage with music is closely linked to
positive emotional experiences in typical listeners [35]. In the amusia group, higher overall BMRQ scores were
negatively associated with blithe and romantic ratings
for major-key music. This inverse relationship indicates
that while some individuals with amusia retain a motivaВ­
tion to engage with music, they may not experience the
expected emotional pleasure or rewards from it. This
dissociation between music reward and emotional expeВ­
riences has been observed in music-specific anhedonia
[30, 36] and may reflect disruptions in affective appraisal
rather than a lack of musical interest. Specifically, indiВ­
viduals with amusia may have difficulty processing the
emotional cues in music, particularly those related to
pitch and tonal shifts. These impairments could disrupt
the interaction between the auditory cortex and subcorВ­
tical reward systems, such as the nucleus accumbens,
which plays a central role in music-induced pleasure. This reduced functional connectivity between auditory
regions and the reward network may explain why amusic
individuals report lower emotional rewards from music,
despite maintaining their motivation to engage with it. In addition, emotion states selectively influenced emoВ­
tional experiences in the amusia group. For minor-key
music, higher depression and anxiety scores were associВ­
ated with more tragic ratings and with lower blithe ratВ­
ings. These associations were absent in the control group. This result suggests that when external musical cues are
not adequately perceived because of pitch deficits, interВ­
nal stats exerts a greater influence on emotional appraisal
[13, 37, 38]. Reconsidering the consistent reduction in BMRQ
scores across all domains in the amusia group may
attributed to several factors. First, individuals with amuВ­
sia may show reduced sensitivity to emotional cues in
music, especially those associated with sadness and tenВ­
sion in minor-key music, which can limit their access to
emotionally rewarding experiences [39]. Second, early
auditory processing difficulties may discourage musiВ­
cal engagement over time, thereby reducing the opporВ­
tunities to develop emotionally meaningful associations
with music [9]. Third, even when motivation is retained,
the absence of corresponding emotional resonance, as
indicated by the negative correlations between reward
sensitivity and emotional ratings, suggests a disconnect

between the desire to engage with music and the capacВ­
ity to derive affective reward from it in individuals with
congenital amusia. The reduced musical reward sensitivity and the altered
emotional experiences observed in congenital amusia
may reflect disruptions in the interaction between cogВ­
nitive and reward systems. Prior neuroimaging research
has identified structural and functional anomalies in
frontotemporal networks in amusia [40вЂ“43], which may
affect connectivity with subcortical reward regions such
as the nucleus accumbens and putamen [12, 44вЂ“47]. As
a neurodevelopmental disorder, congenital amusia may
involve early alterations in frontal cortical development
that potentially weaken integration with limbic reward
circuits. Our behavioral data are consistent with this
account, although they do not directly demonstrate neuВ­
ral dysfunction. Limitations and future directions
The stimulus set was small and limited to Western tonal
music. Participants completed 62 emotion items after
each excerpt, which may induce fatigue or memory decay
for the just-heard music. Future researches should use
larger and more diverse stimulus sets (e.g., including
classical, popular, and non-western traditions), pre-valiВ­
date valence and arousal in the target population, assess
familiarity, and parametrically vary acoustic features
(e.g., tempo, loudness, harmonic density, timbre) while
incorporating computational acoustic descriptors as
predictors. A second limitation is the absence direct neural or
physiological measures. Further research should directly
test this framework by examining whether functional
connectivity between fronto-striatal and auditory netВ­
works relates to BMRQ scores at rest and during listenВ­
ing, and whether striatal responses to key (major/minor)
and pitch-cue manipulations are reduced in individuals
with congenital amusia. Finally, the sample was culturally homogeneous. All
participants were native Mandarin speakers. Linguistic
factors may influence music perception and emotion recВ­
ognition, so future studies should recruit more diverse
populations across languages and cultures to enhance
generalizability. Conclusions
This study found that individuals with congenital amusia
exhibit reduced musical reward sensitivity and altered
emotional experiences in response to minor-key music. Specifically, they reported lower tragic and higher blithe
ratings for minor-key music, while romantic and heightВ­
ened emotional experiences were preserved. In the amuВ­
sia group, higher music reward sensitivity was related to
less positive rating for major-key music, which points to
a weaker alignment between musical engagement and
positive emotion experiences induced by music. In conВ­
trols, higher musical reward sensitivity was related to
more positive rating for major-key music. Taken together,
these findings indicate a specific alteration in how pitch
cues shape affective evaluation and reward in congenital
amusia. Future neuroimaging studies are needed to clarВ­
ify these interactions in congenital amusia by examining
frontotemporal and striatal networks during the processВ­
