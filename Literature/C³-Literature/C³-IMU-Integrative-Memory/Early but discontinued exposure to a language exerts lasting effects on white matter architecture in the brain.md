Language experiences inﬂuence later cognitive development, but how they shape brain organization
remains unclear. We investigated four groups of participants who differed in their early linguistic
environments: International Adoptees (IA) from China, who discontinued their ﬁrst language (L1)
Mandarin in the ﬁrst two years of life and only speak French since adoption; L1-Mandarin speakers
with early acquisition and continued use of French; L1-French speakers; and English-French
bilinguals. We used diffusion-weighted imaging to explore group differences in two white-matter
tracts in the brain that play a role in language processing, the arcuate fasciculus and superior
longitudinal fasciculus. Our ﬁndings revealed that despite discontinuing Mandarin early in life, IA
participants exhibited structural features resembling those of native Mandarin speakers, including
smaller tract volumes in the left hemisphere and more balanced tract volumes across hemispheres. In
contrast, English-French bilingual and L1-French groups exhibited similar white-matter
characteristics, which were distinct from the two tone-language exposed groups, suggesting a
structural organization speciﬁc to tonal language experience, and not bilingualism in general. Afundamentalaspectofunravellingthepuzzleoflanguageacquisitionisthe
question of what is universal under all circumstances of language learning,
and how thebrainisimpactedbythespeciﬁccharacteristicsofthelanguages
being learned. The human brain undergoes signiﬁcant changes during
development, with white-matter tracts playing a critical role in connecting
different cortical regions and supporting cognitive functions. Among the
most important of these tracts is the arcuate fasciculus (AF)1,2. The AF
connects posterior temporal areas (i.e., Wernicke’s area) with the inferior
frontal areas (often referred to as Broca’s area) along the dorsal language
stream2. Especiallyinthelanguage-dominantlefthemisphere,therole of the
AF in language has been described as largely being important for mapping
sound to articulation but also, because of its bidirectionality, as being
involved in top-down attentional aspects of auditory processing1. Although left lateralization for language in the brain is already
present in early childhood3–6, language lateralization is thought to
develop from an almost equipotential state that exists in the ﬁrst two
years of life, with a progressive loss of this equipotentiality occurring as
linguistic skills come under the predominant control of one side of the
brain7. Developmental changes in lateralization of language networks
may occur partly because of brain maturation, but some changes in
lateralization are driven by speciﬁc language experiences. For example,
pitch processing is typically right-lateralized, but becomes left-lateralized
in native tonal language speakers8,9. In tonal languages, pitch contours are
integral to distinguishing word meanings10. Thus, for native-tonal lan-
guage speakers when pitch carries necessary linguistic information, it is
processed in the left hemisphere. However, when pitch carries primarily
paralinguistic contextual information (without any meaning/semantic
information), such as speaker gender or emotion, it is processed in the
right hemisphere. Research using functional neuroimaging has shown
that due to the importance of processing and integrating pitch with
linguistic information, speakers of tonal languages exhibit increased
activation in the right frontal and temporal auditory cortex compared to
speakers of non-tonal languages11–15, highlighting the bilateral engage-
ment of brain regions during speech perception of tonal languages16. The
AF’s involvement in phonological processing2,17–19 makes it a key player
in tone processing, particularly in the early phases of the acquisition of

which the developing child is exposed. Early developmental experiences play a crucial role in shaping later

diverse range of linguistic environments. It has long been suggested that the
most rapid pace of learning takes place during the ﬁrst years of life and that
during this time of heightened neuroplasticity, the brain is optimally pre-
disposed to collect and store basic information about the world31. Hearing a
language during early development helps infants’ brains become attuned to
the speciﬁc sounds of that language, forming neural representations of these
sounds32,33. These neural representations serve as a foundational base for
learning increasingly complex and structured aspects of the language34. This
suggests that there is a continuous connection between the neural repre-
sentations established early on and the higher-level language abilities that
develop over the following years. The adaptive importance of this rela-
tionship is that it enables the developing individual to build more advanced
language skills upon this early neural foundation. However, there is a lackof
substantial empirical evidence regarding how these early experiences
inﬂuence neural processing in later stages of development. In a recent
publication, Pierce et al.35 showed evidence for the maintenance of neural
templates for the processing of Chinese tones in a group of adolescent
international adoptees (IA) whose exposure to their birth language (Chi-
nese) was totally discontinued when they were 12.8 months of age, on
average. From that point on, the IAs were exposed to and spoke only French
and had no conscious recollection of their birth language when tested more
than a decade later. However, when these participants listened to a tone that
carried linguistic information present only in Chinese, and not in their
current language (French), their pattern of brain activation matched that
observed in native Chinese speakers who had spoken Chinese continuously
since birth and had acquired French as a second language at approximately
the same age as the IA. Importantly, the pattern of activation demonstrated
by the IA differed signiﬁcantly from that of French L1 speakers who had
never been exposed to Chinese, even though all participants heard identical
acoustic stimuli. The fact that the neurocognitive responses of the adopted
participants closely matched those of the native Chinese speakers provided
evidence that the neural representations supporting the processing of

later language processing of their adoptive language, French. The present paper builds on and extends this prior research that
examines the functional outcomes of early language exposure on brain
function by examining the impact of language exposure on brain anatomy,
speciﬁcally, structural differences in the white matter tracts involved in
processing language sounds. We examined in what way the functional
changes observed in temporal auditory and language processing areas are
underpinned by the white matter tracts connecting these regions to frontal
language areas—speciﬁcally the AF and the SLF III. We thus used anato-
mical diffusion imaging tractography to shed light on how linguistic
experience shapes the brain structurally. We compared four participant
groups that were exposed to tonal and non-tonal languages to shed light on
how variation in ﬁrst-language exposure shapes the brain: Chinese Inter-
national Adoptees (IA) who stopped speaking Mandarin in the ﬁrst two
years of life and now speak only French; Mandarin L1 speakers with early,
ongoing French exposure (i.e., Mandarin-French bilinguals; Mandarin
group); French monolinguals; and English-French bilinguals. We asked
whether brain structure is language-independent, in which case IA parti-
cipants—who ceased using Mandarin shortly after adoption- should con-
verge structurally with monolingual French speakers. Alternatively, if early
sensory and linguistic input largely shape neural architecture during a
sensitivedevelopmentalwindow37,38,thenthewhite-matterproﬁlesoftheIA
should be more aligned with those of tone-language speakers, i.e., the
Mandarin group. Results

Findings from animal work suggest that neural maps that are established
early have an ‘enduring trace’ (e.g., auditory system in barn owls39, visual
systeminmice40). Weknowthatinputfromtheoutsideworldcanshapeand
inﬂuence the human brain; a more contentious issue is whether such
changes endure without continued input41–43. Moreover, the speciﬁc prop-
erties of languages to which individuals are exposed during development
mayaffectbraindevelopment differently. Thepresentstudyaddressedthese
questions in a unique population of language learners--international
adoptees (IA)--and compared them to other language learners. Using dif-
fusion imaging tractography, we show that IA from China, exposed mainly
to French since adoption in the ﬁrst two years of life, maintained the neural
architecture of their birth language despite no ability to use that language
and no conscious recollection of it. Thus, the IA, who are functionally
monolingual French speakers, demonstrate white matter tracts connecting
thelanguageareasofthebrainthataremoresimilartoagroupofMandarin-
L1 speakers than to groups of French-L1 speakers or English/French (E/F)
bilinguals. Especiallyinthelanguage-dominantlefthemisphere, the IAhave
smaller arcuate fasciculus (AF) volumes than the French and E/F bilingual
groups but do not differ from the Mandarin group. The French and E/F
bilingual groups (i.e., participants who were never exposed to tonal lan-
guages)showtheclassicstrongleft-lateralizationforthistract1,44–46,whilethe
other two groupsdisplay a more bilateraldistribution with a shift away from
the left. Similar results are observed for the other language-related tract, the
superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF III), where the French group has larger
volumes than the two groups exposed to tonal languages in the left but not
the right hemisphere. Looking at relationships with age revealed patterns
speciﬁc to the development of the AF, which were not observed for the SLF
III. In the French group, only the left AF showed a signiﬁcant increase in
volume with age (in the age range 9–35 years), while both the left and right
AF volume increased with age in the IA group. Interestingly, the properties
oftheAFintheIAwerenotaffectedbytheageofadoption(orhowlongthey
were exposed to the tonal language), but rather by how long they were
exposed to their new primary language, French. The purpose of including an E/F bilingual group was to make sure that
the structural differences observed were indeed due to tonal language
exposure and not to bilingualism in general. This group showed a pattern of
brain anatomy that was very similar to the French-speaking group and
differed from the Mandarin and IA groups. Consistent with our results, Vaquero et al.47 observed that sequential English-French bilinguals showed
the same classic left-lateralization as in our E/F bilingual group and also
observed that enhanced left-ward lateralizationoftheAF volumewaslinked
with bilingualism (of two non-tonal languages) speciﬁcally in individuals
who learned their second language together with the ﬁrst, from birth. Moreover, Hämäläinen et al.48 observed, in bilinguals who spoke two non-
tonal languages, that the properties of the left AF were modulated by higher
phonological processing load associated with language learning early in life. However, Cui et al.49 did not ﬁnd any link between multilingual experience
speciﬁcally and the properties of the AF. On the other hand, early musical
experience and musical training, which can be related to tone perception in
Chinesespeakers,hasbeenpreviouslylinkedwithanenhancedrightAFand
reduced left-ward lateralisation47,49, which is suggested to be due to more
experience in auditory-motor adjustments associated with using auditory
feedback49. Similarly, Halwani and colleagues50 had previously shown an
enhanced right AF in musicians compared to non-musicians. The auditorycortex and surrounding areas in the lateral temporal lobe,
particularly in the left hemisphere,play a critical role in language processing
for both tonal and non-tonal languages. However, studies using functional
magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and magnetoencephalography (MEG)
have conﬁrmed that tonal language speakers recruit not only the left-
hemisphere language areas (such as Broca’s and Wernicke’s areas) but also
right-hemisphere regions involved in pitch perception12–15. This bilateral
activation reﬂects the brain’s ability to integrate pitch (a key feature of tonal
languages) with linguistic information. Even during a task of tone pro-
duction, Chang et al.11 found, with higher lexical tone processing require-
ments,enhanced activationinthe rightfrontaland temporallanguageareas,
which were linked by the authors with enhanced interactions through the
right AF. In a recent study, Qi et al.14 studied native English-speaking adults
enroled in a one-month Chinese course, and found that white-matter
structureintherighthemisphere predicted successatearlystagesofChinese
language learning. Consistent with this, Loui et al.51 found a relationship
between the volume of the right AF and behavioural performances on a
pitch-based artiﬁcial grammar learning task. Therefore, the right hemi-
sphere was associated with early success at learning Chinese. Thus, our ﬁndings are consistent with previous ﬁndings and support
the stance that bilingualism per se is not linked speciﬁcally to a rightward
asymmetry of the AF, but rather, signiﬁcant experience and exposure to
tonal languages and music are. The fact that the group differences we
observed for volume were not observed in terms of microstructural prop-
erties (i.e., fractional anisotropy (FA)) is also consistent with the study from
Vaqueroetal.47suggestingthattheeffectonmacrostructureonlymaybedue
tochangesintermsofmyelinpackingproperties(thickness,morphology)of
the AF without affecting microstructural properties47,52. Owing to our examination of multiple different early-language learner
groups, we were able to determine that smaller tract volumes in the left
hemisphere and a more bilateral organization are not attributable to bilin-
gualismorexposureto severallanguagesingeneralbutarespeciﬁctohaving
been exposed to a tonal language very early in life. We show that early
exposure to a tonal language, such as learning Mandarin from birth, is
associated with structural brain changes in the AF and SLF III. This struc-
tural adaptation reﬂects the brain’s early optimization of the connectivity
between regions involved in pitch perception, auditory processing, and

exposed to and use leave a trace that can be observed at the level of the
structural connectivity in the brain, and that stronger bilateral representa-

non-linear, experience-driven process55,56, our data point to the possibility
that brief but early exposure to a language, even if not maintained, may set
into motion neurodevelopmental processes that mirror or even amplify
those predicted by the DRM. Our ﬁndings underscore the need to incor-

duration or proﬁciency level, into updated frameworks of bilingual
neuroplasticity. Moreover, the results argue for a more nuanced
understanding of bilingualism in neurocognitive research, one that
takes into account the typological and phonological characteristics of
the languages involved. Broad conclusions about the neural effects of
bilingualism must therefore be interpreted in the context of speciﬁc
language experiences. Interestingly, we show that the neural architecture resulting from early
but discontinued exposure to tonal languages is not overwritten. The
similarity in white matter tracts between IA and Mandarin speakers clearly

maintained in the brain. This extends the ﬁndings of the unconscious
