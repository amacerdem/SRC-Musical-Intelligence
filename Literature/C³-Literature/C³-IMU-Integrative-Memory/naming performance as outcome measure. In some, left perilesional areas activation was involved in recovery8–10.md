Noelia Martínez-Molina1,2,3, Anni Pitkäniemi1,2, Sini-Tuuli Siponkoski1,2, Nella Moisseinen1,2, Linda Kuusela4,5, Sari Laitinen6, Essi-Reetta Särkämö7, Johanna Pekkola5, Susanna Melkas8, Gottfried Schlaug9, Boris Kleber10, Aleksi J. Sihvonen1,2,8,11 & Teppo Särkämö1,2
Group-based singing has been shown to improve language outcomes and induce structural
neuroplasticity in chronic post-stroke aphasia (PSA). However, the functional neuroplasticity changes
induced by such interventions remain unknown. Here our main aim was to determine these changes
using a cross-over randomised trial. Nineteen patients with PSA were randomly allocated to a 4-month
multicomponent singing-based intervention (singing group) or standard care (control group). With
a pre-post design, we pooled data from both groups and analysed verbal learning and task-based
fMRI activation of two novel songs (trained or untrained during intervention) at two time points (pre-
and post-intervention). Post-intervention, patients with PSA produced more correct syllables from
the trained song and for the trained relative to the untrained song. fMRI results revealed increased
activation when singing along to the trained song in the right postcentral gyrus, and in the right
posterior superior temporal gyrus (pSTG) when singing along to the trained vs. untrained song. Notably, right pSTG activation increases correlated with improved naming abilities. Collectively, these
findings indicate that group-based singing is associated with verbal learning and induces functional
neuroplasticity changes in the singing network, derived from demographically matched healthy

study (N = 2) with melodic intonation therapy (MIT), which uses musical elements to facilitate speech production,

and precentral gyrus) in patients with PSA21 as well as enhancing the functional connectivity between temporal
language regions and the default mode network associated with better language skills22. Beyond music listening, the production of linguistic and musical information via singing offers further
neural stimulation by engaging the spoken language network23 therefore promoting brain recovery through
experience-dependent plasticity24. In healthy participants, fMRI studies have revealed substantial overlap
between the neural networks involved in speech and singing, with singing engaging frontotemporal areas more
bilaterally25–27. In patients with PSA, recent studies on the neuroanatomical substrates for speech and singing
production align with the notion of overlapping but distinct neural circuits for both. For example, findings from
connectometry analysis suggest that while connected spoken language production efficacy relies on both left
dorsal and ventral stream pathways28–30as described in dual-pathway models of speech processing28connected
singing efficacy is predominantly associated with the left ventral stream31. Complementary results from voxel-
based morphometry (VBM) and lesion-symptom mapping in the same PSA cohort indicated that the ability
to produce words from familiar songs was linked solely to damage in left temporal regions, that is, the ventral
stream32. Taken together, this evidence suggests the existence of a neural circuitry for preserved singing abilities
in PSA that could be malleable to singing-based interventions. When implemented in a group setting, singing can enhance communication abilities as well as psychosocial
functioning by introducing social interaction and peer support components in the context of an expressive
and motivating activity33–36. Indeed, a recent randomised controlled trial (RCT) in patients with PSA in the
chronic stage has demonstrated the efficacy of a choir singing intervention to improve spoken language and
communication outcomes37. Compared to the control period with only standard care, group-based singing
improved everyday communication and responsive speech production, which was maintained long-term, in
addition to enhancing social participation and reducing caregiver burden. In a subset of patients with PSA

corpus callosum as well as in the frontal aslant tract, superior longitudinal fasciculus, and corticostriatal tract

frontal cortex. Crucially, the left frontal neuroplasticity effects correlated with improved WAB naming scores. However, the functional brain reorganisation pattern associated with group-based singing, and whether it taps
into additional right-hemispheric regions within the singing network20still remain poorly understood. Here, we examined verbal learning of new songs and functional neuroplasticity changes associated with
group-based singing from a subset of 19 patients with chronic PSA from the abovementioned RCT37. The patients
were assessed three times, that is, at baseline (time point 1, T1), 5-month (time point 2, T2), and 9-month (time
point 3, T3) stages, and received a 16-week multicomponent singing intervention comprising both group and
home training either between T1 and T2 (Group A; N = 9) or T2 and T3 (Group B, N = 10). The song material
in the intervention comprised familiar songs as well as two novel songs specifically written and composed for
the study, one of which was trained during the intervention period and the other not trained (counter-balanced
across groups). By pooling data together from both groups, we analysed both behavioural verbal learning
performance (production of correct syllables) and brain activation in the fMRI task involving the production
of the song phrases with an auditory model (sing along) and without an auditory model (sing from memory)
from the novel songs in order to identify brain activation changes after the intervention within a singing network
mask derived from demographically matched healthy participants. To the best of our knowledge, this is the
first longitudinal task-based fMRI study in aphasia to adopt a singing task that directly mirrors the one used
in the singing-based intervention. Based on previous neuroimaging studies of singing-based interventions in
aphasia20,38–40we hypothesised that the singing intervention would induce functional neuroplasticity changes
within the preserved parts of the singing network in left and/or right frontotemporal regions that would be
associated with improved verbal learning of the song material and with the enhanced language outcome induced

We analysed the functional neuroplasticity changes induced by the singing intervention within the premorbid
singing network derived from demographically matched healthy controls (HCs). To do so, we first performed
a one-sample T-test in the HCs to obtain a representative map of the singing network based on the ‘sing along
vs. rest’ contrast, which was the main contrast of interest in this study as it closely resembles the group-based
singing intervention where patients aim to sing uniformly and according to an auditory model (Fig. 3a). The
significant clusters encompassed the bilateral STG and cerebellum, the left SMA and brainstem. Additionally,
we obtained a mask from HCs based on the ‘sing from memory vs. rest’ contrast to explore activation changes in
brain structures related to learning and lyrics retrieval. This analysis revealed significant clusters in the bilateral
cerebellum, the left STG, and the right PreCG. For a detailed list of the regional labels and peak coordinates
of the significant clusters in the ‘sing along vs. rest’ and ‘sing from memory vs. rest’ masks please see Tables S1
and S2, respectively. Next, we thresholded both T-maps and used these binary masks as an explicit mask for the
second-level analyses including the patients with PSA. Then, we conducted a paired T-test pooling data from
Groups A and B to examine the post- vs. pre-intervention effect for: (i) the trained song, (ii) the untrained song
and (iii) the trained compared to the untrained song. Age, total intracranial volume (TIV), and lesion size were
added as nuisance covariates in all the analyses (for further details see Methods). Statistical thresholding used
a voxel-wise cluster forming threshold of p < 0.005 (uncorrected) and a cluster-level threshold of p < 0.05 after
familywise error (FWE) correction for both the explicit masks from HCs and the second level analyses from
patients with PSA. In the post- vs. pre-intervention analysis, higher activation was found in the right postcentral gyrus [PoCG;
k (cluster size) = 178, MNI (x, y,z) = 48 − 16 41, pFWE = 0.006, T(17) = 5.58, Fig. 3b] for the trained song in the sing
along vs. rest contrast. Likewise, higher activation was found in the right posterior superior temporal gyrus
[pSTG; k (cluster size) = 95, MNI (x, y,z) = 64 − 32 7, pFWE = 0.040, T(17) = 4.821, Fig. 3c] for the trained compared
to the untrained song. No suprathreshold clusters were found for the untrained song. For the ‘sing from memory
vs. rest’ contrast, we found no significant clusters in any of the songs or for the trained compared to the untrained
song. To provide a quantitative index of the intervention-related neuroplasticity effects, we computed within-
subject Cohen’s d values for the post vs. pre analysis in the two significant clusters. The right PoCG exhibited a
mean beta increase of 4.67 (SD = 3.43), corresponding to a Cohen’s d of 1.36, indicating a large effect size. The
right pSTG cluster showed a mean beta increase of 5.90 (SD = 4.95), yielding a Cohen’s d of 1.19, also reflecting
a large effect. These results highlight robust and spatially specific activation changes following the intervention,
consistent with functional plasticity in sensory-motor and auditory regions. Correlation between functional neuroplasticity changes and Language outcomes
To examine the relationship between treatment-induced functional neuroplasticity changes and clinical
outcomes, we first evaluated whether the improvement in WAB naming reported in the primary study37 was
maintained in the subset of patients included in the present study. Using a Wilcoxon signed-rank test, we found a
pre-post improvement in WAB naming (W(17) = 26.5, p = 0.019) but not in WAB repetition (W(18) = 45, p = 0.142)
in accordance with previous findings. Therefore, we conducted nonparametric partial Spearman’s correlations
between improved WAB naming subscale and the mean increased activation in the significant fMRI clusters. As
in the fMRI analysis, we controlled for the same nuisance variables, i.e., age, TIV and lesion size. We found that
increased activation in the right pSTG correlated with improved WAB naming (R = 0.490, p = 0.038. Figure 4). No significant correlations were found with improved verbal learning of the novel songs. Discussion
This work investigated the effect of a group-based multicomponent singing intervention on functional
neuroplasticity and verbal learning of novel song lyrics in chronic post-stroke aphasia (PSA). With respect
to behavioural measures, we found that patients with PSA were able to learn to produce the lyrics of novel
songs. The findings from the task-based fMRI data indicated that the singing-based intervention also induced
functional brain reorganisation within the singing network, particularly in the right hemisphere. Specifically,
patients showed increased activation in the right PoCG and right pSTG within the singing network (obtained
from HCs), with the latter correlating with better naming abilities. These results provide novel insights into
the functional neuroplasticity patterns associated with singing-induced treatment effects in chronic PSA and
contribute to the growing evidence supporting music-based interventions for stroke rehabilitation, including
melodic intonation therapy (MIT)20,39music listening21,41and group singing37. The current findings demonstrate that patients with chronic PSA were able to learn novel songs introduced
during the group-based singing intervention. Following the intervention, patients showed significant
improvement in producing correct syllables for the trained song, both in comparison to the untrained song and
when the trained song was evaluated in isolation. The observed improvements in verbal learning are potentially
attributable to the facilitative role of singing in verbal production, which slows the rate of word production
and strengthens the connectedness between syllables and words through continuous voicing and melodic
intonation42. These results challenge the findings of an earlier case study which suggested that sung presentation
does not facilitate the repetition of neither novel nor familiar lyrics compared to spoken presentation43. Although
it remains to be determined whether patients with PSA in this study can produce the songs’ lyrics better when
presented in sung compared to spoken format. Further, a case study by Straube et al.44 proposed that preserved
singing ability relies on the familiarity of the song, with melody and lyrics integrated in long-term memory. Scientific Reports | (2025) 15:27639

Straube et al. observed superior sung production over spoken production exclusively for familiar lyrics, not for
novel ones. However, our findings align with prior research demonstrating that patients with PSA repeated and
recalled more words from novel lyrics when singing in synchrony with an auditory model45 or from short stories
presented in a sung format46. Our study is the first RCT in chronic PSA to report that group-based singing interventions lead to significant
brain activation changes within the singing network, particularly in the right hemisphere. In the fMRI analysis,
increased activity was observed in the right PoCG during singing along with the trained song and in the pSTG
in the trained relative to the untrained song after the intervention. These singing-induced activation increases
in the right PoCG and pSTG align with prior neuroimaging evidence from healthy participants, emphasizing
the distinct roles of these regions in vocal production and sensory-motor integration25,26,47. While these earlier
findings were based on healthy young26,47 and middle-aged25 participants, our results extend this evidence by
confirming the existence of a largely overlapping neuroanatomical singing network in healthy controls matched
demographically to patients with PSA, observed both during singing along with an auditory model and when
singing from memory. The increased brain activation in the right PoCG, a region associated with the ventral representation of
the larynx48aligns with a prior fMRI study in healthy participants. In that study, professional opera singers
exhibited increased brain activity in the bilateral ventral primary somatosensory cortex (larynx S1, Area 3a, MNI
coordinates = 36 − 18 39), which was also correlated with total amount of singing practice. This region is in close
proximity to the cluster peak found in the present study (MNI coordinates = 49 − 16 41). As suggested by Kleber
et al.47this could reflect enhanced kinaesthetic motor control of the vocal tract for sound production after the
intervention. These findings suggest that extensive singing training enhances the somatosensory representation
of the larynx in the contralesional side of the damaged hemisphere in chronic patients with PSA. Remarkably,
the lesion map and the singing network greatly overlapped in the left hemisphere, including both the PoCG and
the pSTG. However, direct evidence of the right PoCG’s specific role in chronic PSA remains scarce. Therefore,
further studies are needed to elucidate its precise contribution to speech rehabilitation. The right pSTG plays a crucial role in processing complex auditory feedback during vocal production
tasks, particularly in singing. Notably, the right STG has been associated with a network responsible for
processing pitch and melodic phrases within sung passages49and lesions in this region have been linked to
acquired amusia50. Furthermore, Cogan et al.51 reported that sensory-motor transformations for speech occur
bilaterally, with significant activation in the right pSTG during both perception and production, suggesting
this region’s involvement in transforming auditory input into motor output during speech tasks. In addition to
pre-post effects, we observed a positive relationship between treatment-induced improvements in naming and
neuroplasticity in the right pSTG. This finding suggests that enhanced neural processing of the trained material
during a group-based singing intervention is associated with larger clinical gain in language function induced by
the intervention and provides support for the role of the right hemisphere aphasia recovery subsequent to left-
hemispheric damage52. On the other hand, it is important to note that increased engagement of right hemisphere
regions and networks has occasionally been associated also with maladaptive outcomes in naming and verbal
fluency53,54. While the role of the right temporal regions in speech production in aphasia is still largely unknown,
it has been related to improved outcomes in comprehension54–56. However, since we used a fMRI production
task, only WAB subscales involving production and with significant pre-post effect were considered for brain-
behaviour correlations to reduce the dimensionality of the analyses. Future fMRI studies may incorporate both
production and comprehension tasks to evaluate the specific role of associated right hemispheric functional
changes. In the context of current models of speech and singing production, the activation of the right PoCG and
pSTG can be integrated in the DIVA (Directions Into Velocities of Articulators) model57. The DIVA model
emphasizes the role of sensory-motor feedback loops in speech production, where the right hemisphere,
particularly the pSTG, plays a key role in auditory feedback control, especially for pitch regulation and melodic
elements in vocalisation that are more prominent in singing. Indeed, singing involves more prosodic and melodic
modulation than typical speech. The right pSTG appears to be crucial for comparing produced sounds with
target pitch, providing corrective feedback that aligns motor output with auditory goals58. The involvement of
the right PoCG reflects its role in the control of complex vocal motor patterns required in singing, by providing
kinaesthetic feedback, as highlighted in neuroimaging studies59​. Overall, within the context of the DIVA model,
our findings suggest that, in PSA rehabilitation, a singing-based intervention might be especially beneficial by
strengthening the right-hemispheric auditory-motor integration and feedback control networks that are crucial
for vocal production and, further, that their functional enhancement may be linked to the clinical efficacy of the
intervention. Although the different intervention protocols preclude a direct comparison, the observed contributions of
the right PoCG and pSTG found after the singing-based intervention align with a prior study on the engagement
of the contralesional hemisphere after MIT, involving patients with large left hemispheric lesions20. The authors
reported increased MIT-induced fMRI activation predominantly in the right hemisphere, including the pSTG,
in one of the two patients with nonfluent aphasia included. Conversely, other studies have shown deactivation of
right hemispheric regions after MIT38,60 or increased activation without associated recovery61. In addition, some
of these studies reported MIT-induced activity specifically in the perilesional left hemisphere38,61. Similarly, the
SIPARI protocol, a rhythmic-melodic voice training akin to singing, has been linked to increased activation in
perilesional frontal regions in another small group study62. Given this mixed evidence, future RCTs with larger
sample sizes are needed to elucidate the clinical —especially lesion location and extent63— and neurobiological
factors underlying the compensatory versus maladaptive role of right hemispheric regions in singing-induced

With regards to recently proposed mechanisms for language recovery in aphasia, the results from this fMRI
study lend support to the degeneracy principle, since we found a rightward increased activation in the pSTG
within the singing network associated with improved naming abilities. This suggests that experience-dependent
plasticity, induced by the singing intervention, might contribute to recovery of this language component7. This
is in line with previous evidence of the contralesional right hemisphere homologues to play a crucial role in
language recovery after left hemisphere injury, especially in patients with large lesions52. Previous studies have
also linked homotopic regions in the right hemisphere, particularly the inferior frontal and insular regions,
to improved naming abilities following targeted anomia treatments64. However, different speech and language
domains may exhibit very differential trajectories of recovery, in line with the notion that aphasia recovery is a
heterogeneous and multidimensional process52,65,66. Within this multidimensional framework, our contribution
suggests that singing-induced right hemispheric functional neuroplasticity may be associated with better
language production in patients with PSA at the chronic stage. We also detected some notable differences between our previous structural results, which showed increased
grey matter volume in the left frontal cortex and increased white matter connectivity in left and right frontal
(frontal aslant tract), frontotemporal (superior longitudinal fasciculus), and corticostriatal tracts after the
intervention36and the findings of this fMRI study, which are both based on a subset of patients from the original
RCT. A plausible explanation for the discrepancy is that, first of all, for methodological reasons, the subsets
of patients with PSA included in the structural and fMRI studies were different. Second, the structural and
functional results may be indicative of different types/levels of neuroplasticity mechanism involving more
general structural changes in the (i) core left frontal language regions and pathways and in the (ii) connectivity
of the broader bilateral language network and more specific functional changes in the (iii) right temporoparietal
regions linked to the vocal-motor learning aspects of the intervention. These changes are not mutually exclusive
and likely contribute together to the clinical efficacy of singing. Moreover, in the current fMRI study, we had
only a singing production task whereas the inclusion of a speech production task could have engaged spared
language-related regions in the left hemisphere, particularly within the dorsal stream, which is considered more
left-dominant28. Nevertheless, it is important to note that the right PoCG cluster identified in this study connects
with the right superior longitudinal fasciculus/arcuate fasciculus tract showing increased quantitative anisotropy
in the structural findings36suggesting at least some overlap between the results. Also previous studies of MIT
have reported changes in structural connectivity in right or bilateral white matter pathways67,68. While this work provides promising evidence for the efficacy of group-based singing interventions in PSA
recovery, several limitations should be noted. First, we acknowledge that the multifaceted singing intervention
does not allow us to disentangle the individual contributions of its different components (e.g., singing, group
interaction, home training) or the specific effects of the singing training from the more generic therapeutic
effects of social interaction and emotional support provided by the group setting. However, this holistic,
multicomponent treatment is well-suited to target the heterogenous profile of language impairments as well as
psychosocial deficits often found in patients with PSA. Second, the small sample size underscores the need for
future studies with larger cohorts to validate and potentially extend these results. In addition, our analysis was
focused on the singing network; future studies with larger sample sizes could help to understand functional
reorganisation patterns at the whole-brain level. However, to the best of our knowledge, this study still stands as
the largest multimodal RCT conducted to date on singing-induced functional neuroplasticity changes in chronic
patients with PSA. In conclusion, our study provides novel evidence that a group-based multicomponent singing intervention
in chronic patients with PSA was associated with verbal learning of novel songs and induced functional
neuroplasticity changes in the right hemisphere, particularly in the PoCG and pSTG, with the latter linked to
better naming abilities. These findings suggest the potential of group-based singing interventions to promote
speech production recovery by engaging preserved language-related networks homologues within the singing
network and potential compensatory mechanisms. Methods

We used a sparse sampling technique75 to acquire EPI volumes in between periods of singing production, thus
reducing motion artifacts. The timeline for a single trial is schematised in Fig. 2b. Patients sang overtly 6 short
phrases (2 bars in a 4/4 metre) of the 2 novel songs in Finnish (Uulaa and Tydyy) and with mean number of
words 4 ± 1.26 and 3.83 ± 1.17, respectively; see Table S3) that were specifically composed for the intervention
and whose lyrics related to the circumstances that involved being a stroke survivor. Each trial started with 0.5s of silence, followed by a period of inhalation (1.5) and the active condition (4s
duration), a new silence period (1.5s) and volume acquisition (1.5 s). The design involved 3 active conditions
for each phrase: ‘listen and exhale’, ‘sing along’ and ‘sing from memory’. The auditory stimuli were sung without
instrumental accompaniment by a professional singer and the gender (female or male) was matched to that
of the patient. Additionally, 2 baseline trials were included at the end of every three phrases as explicit ‘rest’
condition. To control for effects related to excessive inhalation, baseline measurements required subjects to
inhale at the same time points as in the singing condition, followed by slow exhalation. Thus, each TR contained
a delay of 7.5s after onset of the trial and a TA of 1.5s with a total trial length of 9s. Within the 7.5s delay, timings
of inhalation and task execution were systematically varied by ± 500ms to ensure that we included the putative
peak of the hemodynamic response for each trial, which is estimated to occur with a 4–7 s delay76,77. Three active conditions were implemented for each phrase: listen and exhale, sing along, and sing from
memory. Each block followed a structured sequence: a, b, c, a, b, c, a, b, c, d, d, a, b, c, a, b, c, a, b, c, d, d (where

fMRI first-level model
Main effects were estimated with the general linear model (GLM) framework where each condition was modeled
with a boxcar function convolved with the canonical hemodynamic response function (HRF) as this has been
shown to yield the best model fitting in sparse sampling acquisitions82. Based on previous literature47we opted
for a “slow” event-response analysis and set trials’ duration to 0s. Microtime resolution was adjusted to 125 ms
per bin corresponding to the standard value used in continuous fMRI. Accordingly, microtime onset was set to
6 with the middle slice as reference. The interleaved design of the task ensured that each active condition recurred every three trials, corresponding
to a fixed onset-to-onset interval of 27 s for each condition. Accordingly, the dominant task frequency was
approximately 0.037 Hz, consistent across all three active conditions. In a few instances, however, the insertion of
2 baseline trials midway through the block introduced slightly longer intervals between repetitions of the same
condition in an onset-to-onset interval of 27 s + 2 × 9 s = 45s. These extended intervals were infrequent, occurring
5 times per 30 repetitions for each condition. Their inclusion aimed to reduce task fatigue in patients with PSA
while minimally impacting the regularity of the condition timing. Based on the Nyquist criterion and standard
high-pass filter recommendations, the minimum cutoff period required to preserve these signals is 2 × 27 s = 54 s
(0.0185 Hz). We therefore retained the commonly used conservative high-pass filter of 128 s (0.0078 Hz), which
fully preserved task-related frequencies. Spectral analysis of the design matrix regressors confirmed strong
signal components around 0.04 Hz, indicating no attenuation of task-relevant signal by the filter. Temporal autocorrelations in the BOLD signal timeseries were accounted for by using an AR(1) model to
approximate the observed covariance of the functional data in the context of restricted maximum likelihood
estimation. We included the TPM for grey matter tissue thresholded at 15% probability as explicit mask. No
global scaling was applied. In addition to experimental condition effects, the design matrix included nuisance
regressors to control for the effect of head motion (6 realignment parameters), potential outlier scans, and one
regressor with incorrect trials in all conditions. The model was first estimated within each subject, and contrast-
of-interest volumes were generated comparing the appropriate conditional coefficients to assess three simple
main effects: ‘listen vs. rest’, ‘sing along vs. rest’ and ‘sing from memory vs. rest’. Each patient’s Uulaa and Tydyy
runs were modelled as separate sessions in the same first-level design matrix for each time point. Functional fMRI second-level model

group-level random effects GLM analyses. The main contrast of interest in this study was ‘sing along vs. rest’,
which represents the closest condition to the group-based singing intervention. However, we also explored the
group-level results from the other singing condition, i.e. from the “sing from memory vs. rest’ contrast. Given
the low sample size of the intervention groups, we performed a paired T-test by pooling data together from both
groups. To do so, SPM’s ImCalc was used to create a ‘post-pre activation change’ image for each song and patient. Specifically, the pre-treatment ‘sing along vs. rest’ contrast image was subtracted from the post-treatment ‘sing
along vs. rest’ contrast image. Then, we compared the trained vs. untrained song using a paired T-test with the
‘post-pre activation change’ image for each patient as input into the second-level analysis. For both second-level
analyses, age, TIV, and lesion size were entered as nuisance covariates. To restrict the analyses to the singing
network, the second-level analyses were explicitly masked with the clusters derived from the ‘sing along vs.
rest’ and ‘sing from memory vs. rest’ contrasts in the demographically matched healthy participants (see next
