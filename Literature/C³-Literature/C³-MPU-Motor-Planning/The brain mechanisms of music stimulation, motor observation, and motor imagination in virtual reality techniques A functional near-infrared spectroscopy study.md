Abstract 
15 
Virtual reality (VR) has gained popularity in recent years, integrating with 
16 
conventional music stimulation, action observation (AO), and motor 
17 
imagination (MI). It offers promising opportunities for developing innovative 
18 
rehabilitation treatments, though the mechanisms underlying these effects 
19 
remain unclear. This study aims to compare brain activation and network 
20 
mechanisms following the fusion of MS, AO, and MI with VR. 50 healthy 
21 
participants were recruited and underwent functional near-infrared 
22 
spectroscopy (fNIRS) synchronization with three virtual reality tasks: music 
23 
stimulation (VRMS), action observation (VRAO), and motor imagery (VRMI). 
24 
The results indicate that VRMS significantly enhances functional connectivity 
25 
of the bilateral primary sensory cortex (S1), pre-motor cortex and 
26 
supplementary motor area (PM&SMA) compared to VRAO and VRMI. 
27 
Furthermore, the interaction among the bilateral PM&SMA, right dorsolateral 
28 
prefrontal cortex (DLPFC), and right primary motor cortex (M1) regions is 
29 
notably stronger with VRMS than with the other VR tasks. These findings 
30 
elucidate the brain activation and network characteristics of the three VR 
31 
tasks, highlighting VRMS’s potential in boosting the functional interaction 
32

33 
broader diseased brain samples, and alternative brain-stimulation effects of 
34 
VRMS.

Significance statement 
39 
As established non-invasive neuromodulation modalities, music stimulation 
40 
(MS), action observation (AO), and motor imagery (MI) face two clinical 
41 
constraints: overly simplistic task design reducing therapeutic salience, and 
42 
compromised patient engagement. Virtual reality (VR) integration addresses 
43 
these limitations through enhanced immersive and multisensory stimulation. 
44 
This study investigates cortical activation dynamics and functional network 
45 
during VRMS, VRAO, and VRMI tasks. The functional near-infrared 
46 
spectroscopy (fNIRS) results demonstrate that VRMS induces superior 
47 
functional connectivity modulation compared to VRAO, and VRMI tasks, 
48 
particularly within sensorimotor integration. These finding may contribute to 
49 
optimizing VR rehabilitation paradigm selection and advancing VR technology 
50 
exploration.

1. Introduction 
53 
With aging populations and an increasing incidence of neurological 
54 
diseases, there is a growing demand for functional recovery and rehabilitation, 
55 
prompting research into new methods(Stinear et al., 2020; O'Dell, 2023). 
56 
Music stimulation, action observation (AO), and motor imagination (MI) are 
57 
non-invasive rehabilitation techniques widely used in clinical practice, 
58 
especially in community and family therapy settings. These techniques 
59 
achieve significant therapeutic outcomes by using sound and vision to 
60 
activate the cognitive and motor cortices. Moreover, virtual reality (VR) 
61 
technology, as an emerging advanced tool, has the potential to address the 
62 
limitations of traditional rehabilitation methods, such as monotony and patient 
63 
focus challenges. With the integration of VR, these techniques can address 
64 
these issues, allowing professionals to manage multiple patients more 
65 
efficiently and consequently reduce their workload. These rehabilitation 
66 
methods require minimal motor ability from patients and can be easily 
67 
eNeuro Accepted Manuscript

## Page 4

administered in a seated position. When combined with VR, they create an 
68 
immersive and multisensory stimulation without increasing the patients’ risk of 
69 
falls (Laver et al., 2017; Palma et al., 2017). 
70 
Musical stimuli utilize elements such as tone, rhythm, and melody to 
71 
activate specific brain areas, thereby increasing cortical activation in auditory 
72 
and motor regions. This mechanism facilitates brain-stimulation treatment 
73 
(Sihvonen et al., 2017; Buard et al., 2019; de Witte et al., 2020; Chen et al., 
74 
2022). Research demonstrates that music can stimulate bilateral brain 
75 
networks in subcortical regions, including the frontal, parietal, and temporal 
76 
lobes, which promotes neuroplasticity. Moreover, music may enhance 
77 
cognitive function, alleviate symptoms of anxiety and depression, and 
78 
potentially improve cognitive abilities (Chorna et al., 2019; Salihu et al., 2024). 
79 
AO involves perceiving and understanding motor features by watching 
80 
others’ movements through videos or other media, leading to passive imitation 
81 
and psychological-level movement execution(Wulf et al., 2010). This process 
82 
may activate the brain’s mirror neuron system and various regions associated 
83 
with visual processing, including the occipital, temporal, and parietal areas, as 
84 
well as specific parts of the inferior parietal lobe and frontal gyrus. Motor 
85 
Imagery (MI), building on AO, requires autonomously imagining motor actions, 
86 
actively inducing psychological imitation, and executing movements, thereby 
87 
activating the mirror neuron system. Both AO and MI involve internal mental 
88 
activities that do not result in physical motor output but stimulate the motor 
89 
cortex similarly to actual movement execution(Hardwick et al., 2018; Bruton et 
90 
al., 2020). 
91 
The prefrontal cortex (PFC) is a crucial brain region responsible for 
92 
coordinating higher cognitive functions, including memory, language, and 
93 
executive functioning(Siddiqui et al., 2008). The PFC is involved in music 
94 
therapy, movement observation, and motor imagery. For instance, music 
95 
therapy facilitates the integration and coordination of cognitive functions such 
96 
as attention, imagination, creativity, memory, and emotion through neural 
97 
plasticity(Speranza et al., 2022). When individuals engage in motor 
98 
observation and motor imagery, the primary motor area (M1), pre-motor 
99 
cortex (PM), and supplementary motor area (SMA) of the frontal lobe are 
100 
activated(Larsson et al., 1996; Bordoloi et al., 2024). Thus, during music 
101 
therapy, the unconscious processes of beat perception and prediction, as well 
102 
as the observation and imagination of regular movement, are closely linked to 
103

M1, PM, and SMA. The prefrontal cognitive area and motor area are essential 
104 
in regulating music therapy, motor observation, and motor imagery. 
105 
These techniques are frequently combined in clinical settings to enhance 
106 
motor relearning and adaptation(Rannaud Monany et al., 2022). Despite the 
107 
effectiveness of these rehabilitation technologies, several technical 
108 
shortcomings remain. These issues include reliance on a single training 
109 
mode, challenges in maintaining patient attention, and obstacles to the 
110 
popularization of technology, all of which hinder their efficacy and widespread 
111 
adoption. 
112 
VR can be employed as a presentation technique alongside conventional 
113 
rehabilitation methods. With the advancement of VR technology, its 
114 
integration into daily life, architecture, military training, entertainment, medical 
115 
research, and other fields has become increasingly common, establishing it 
116 
as a prominent approach for rehabilitation treatment(Tieri et al., 2018). In the 
117 
medical realm, Ewa Szczepocka et al. explored the integration of VR with 
118 
cognitive training, discovering that it could enhance the sustained attention of 
119 
the elderly, thereby suggesting the potential of VR in the cognitive domain. 
120 
Their research indicates that improvements in cognitive function are closely 
121 
linked to neuronal plasticity(Grealy et al., 1999; Szczepocka et al., 2024). 
122 
Compared to real stimulation, VR stimulation produces a greater effect on 
123 
neuroplasticity, evidenced by increased β-band power in the frontal 
124 
region(Gangemi et al., 2023). 
125

for (a) enhancing visualFiufiufiigur stimulation that targets the mirror neuron

129

130 
thereby facilitating applications within communities and households (Merians 
131 
et al., 2002; Kwakkel et al., 2004; Tieri et al., 2018). 
132 
Recently, functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) has rapidly 
133 
emerged as a powerful tool for detecting brain activity. It operates based on 
134 
the interaction between near-infrared light and human tissue, enabling the 
135 
real-time monitoring of hemodynamics in the cerebral cortex. Near-infrared 
136 
light (NIR) penetrates biological tissues, such as lipids and hemoglobin, which 
137 
exhibit distinct absorption characteristics at different wavelengths(Pinti et al., 
138 
2020). The most critical absorption chromophore is hemoglobin, whose 
139 
physiological state can be indicated by oxygenated hemoglobin (HBO), 
140 
eNeuro Accepted Manuscript

141 
HBT were used as analysis indices. When cognitive functions are engaged, 
142

143 
in cerebral blood flow and possess a good signal-to-noise ratio(Yan et al., 
144 
2024). Due to its advantages, including portability, exercise tolerance, and 
145 
safety, fNIRS has been widely adopted in neurological research. 
146 
In our study, we examined the effects of three tasks on brain activation 
147

148 
cortical effects while simplifying our experimental procedures because of its 
149

150 
mechanisms of brain-stimulation following the combination of VR with these 
151 
three rehabilitation technologies is needed, especially regarding the 
152 
differences in their effect sizes. This study, therefore, explored the effects of 
153 
integrating VR with MS (VRMS), action observation (VRAO), and motor 
154 
imagination (VRMI) on brain activation and networks. This research also 
155 
provides a theoretical basis for clinical application.

2. Methods 
158 
2.1. Participants 
159 
50 healthy participants were recruited for the study conducted at the Fifth 
160 
Affiliated Hospital of Guangzhou Medical University from July 2023 to March 
161 
2025. The participants had an average age of 20.3 ± 0.8 years and comprised 
162 
20 males and 30 females. None had cognitive or intellectual disabilities, had 
163 
undergone VRMS, or received formal training in music. All participants 
164 
provided written informed consent. The study was approved by the Ethics 
165 
Committee of Guangzhou Medical University (GYWY-L2023-58) and 
166 
registered with the International Clinical Trials Registry (ChiCTR2300071527). 
167 
 
168 
2.2. Experiment 
169 
Before the experiment, the testing environment was maintained in 
170 
silence, and lighting was controlled to minimize auditory and visual 
171 
interference during fNIRS data collection. Participants used Huawei (China) 
172 
head-mounted displays (HMDs), with brightness adjusted for clear visibility of 
173 
the experimental stimuli. They sat comfortably in a chair with a backrest while 
174 
wearing the fNIRS equipment and VR glasses. A researcher briefed them on 
175 
eNeuro Accepted Manuscript

## Page 7

the study protocol, emphasizing the importance of focusing on the task and 
176 
refraining from talking during the session. Preliminary fNIRS data were 
177 
collected to ensure a stable channel signal. 
178 
The study used fNIRS to synchronize VRMS, VRAO, and VRMI tasks 
179 
within a block design format. Each task consisted of three blocks that were 
180 
identical for all participants. Before each block, participants had 10 s to 
181 
prepare. Each block included a 30-s task period followed by a 30-s recovery 
182 
period, during which participants viewed a white crisscross pattern on a black 
183 
screen. There were no breaks between consecutive blocks or tasks. Baseline 
184 
data were collected for 10 s before the onset of each block, resulting in a total 
185 
trial duration of 190 s (Figure 1). 
186 
In the VRMS task, participants listened to the first verse of the song “Red 
187 
River Valley” in Mandarin. The piece was performed in 4/4 time, in the key of 
188 
F major, at a tempo of 120 beats per minute, and consisted of 90 notes in 
189 
total. The VRAO task involved observing a video of a walk from a third-person 
190 
perspective, requiring sustained, focused attention. Similarly, the VRMI task 
191 
featured the same walking video from a third-person perspective, but included 
192 
synchronized auditory cues designed to evoke kinesthetic imagery of walking 
193 
in the participants. Throughout the experiment, we collected hemodynamic 
194 
data from specified brain regions of interest (ROIs) using fNIRS. 
195 
 
196 
2.3. fNIRS settings 
197 
Data were collected using fNIRS technology (Nirsmart, Danyang 
198 
Huichuang Medical Equipment Co., Ltd., China), continuously measuring 
199 
changes in oxyhemoglobin and methemoglobin within the cerebral cortex. 
200 
This was achieved using 760- and 850-nm light wavelengths. The sampling 
201 
frequency was set at 11 Hz. The recordings utilized 24 channels, 
202 
incorporating ten detectors and 12 light sources, with each detector positioned 
203 
30 mm from its corresponding light source. This configuration aligns with the 
204 
international 10–20 standard for lead-system design (Figure 2a). 
205 
The ROIs identified in this study were the right and left dorsolateral 
206 
prefrontal cortex (RDLPFC and LDLPFC), the right and left front polar area 
207 
(RFPA and LFPA), the right and left primary sensory cortex (RS1 and LS1), 
208 
the right and left pre-motor cortex (RPM and LPM), the supplementary motor 
209 
cortex (SMA), and the right and left primary motor cortex (RM1 and LM1, 
210 
respectively). A FASTRAK 3D digitization instrument (Polhemus, Colchester, 
211 
eNeuro Accepted Manuscript

## Page 8

VT, USA) was used to position the ROIs on the brain. The instrument 
212 
sequentially tracked the 24 channels in the functional near-infrared 
213 
spectroscopy (fNIRS), collecting data on the X, Y, and Z axes of each light 
214 
source and detector with reference to Brodmann areas (BA) standards (Figure 
215 
2b). 
216 
Each ROI was mapped to a distinct BA. Channel assignments to ROIs 
217 
were determined by identifying the BA subregion with maximum spatial 
218 
overlap at each channel location. Channels co-localized within an ROI 
219 
collectively delineated the target region (Figure 2c). 
220 
 
221 
2.4. Signal processing 
222 
The fNIRS data collected were imported into the NirSpark software 
223 
(Danyang Huichuang Medical Equipment Co., Ltd., China) for preprocessing. 
224 
Prominent motion artifacts were manually corrected, and a bandpass filter 
225 
(0.01–0.2 Hz) was applied to remove residual motion artifacts and 
226 
physiological noise (de Witte et al., 2020; Sutoko et al., 2020). This study 
227 
investigates the activation effects induced by VR tasks, particularly focusing 
228 
on the task-related response values of HBO and HBT. The light signal was 
229 
converted into HBO and HBT according to the Lambert-Beer law, and the 
230 
average signal for ROIs within each block was calculated. 
231 
In the brain-network module, functional connectivity (FC) values quantify 
232 
the connections both between channels and between ROIs. Homologous 
233 
brain-network FC values assess the relationships among channels within the 
234 
same ROI, whereas heterologous brain-network FC values gauge the overall 
235 
connection strength between different ROIs. To ensure the reliability of the 
236 
statistical results, FC values were corrected for multiple comparisons across 
237 
ROIs using the false discovery rate (FDR) method. 
238 
 
239 
2.5. Statistical analysis 
240 
The data were analyzed using the Kolmogorov-Smirnov normality test via 
241 
SPSS version 22.0. For data adhering to a normal distribution, paired-sample 
242 
t-tests were conducted. Conversely, the non-parametric Wilcoxon signed-rank 
243 
test was employed for two related samples that did not satisfy normality 
244 
assumptions. A significance threshold of p < 0.05 was set for statistical 
245 
eNeuro Accepted Manuscript

## Page 9

interpretation. Image processing was carried out using NirSpark software and 
246 
GraphPad 9.0.

4. Discussion 
281 
This study examined the variations in brain activation and FC networks 
282 
during MS, AO, and motor imagery (MI) tasks within a VR context. The 
283 
objective was to investigate how VR integration influences cerebral cortex 
284 
hemodynamics by synchronizing the functional near-infrared spectroscopy 
285 
(fNIRS) during three distinct brain-stimulation tasks. Unlike AO and MI, MS 
286 
had unique effects on the brain due to various elements of music theory, 
287 
including tone, melody, rhythm, and music perception(Patterson et al., 2002). 
288 
In research on event-related potentials, Palmer et al. (Palmer et al., 
289 
2009)found that modifying musical elements such as timbre, stress, and 
290 
rhythm could alter the latency of mismatch negativity in listeners. This finding 
291 
suggests that musical elements can regulate brain processing. 
292 
We observed similar effects of music on cortical hemodynamics using 
293 
fNIRS monitoring. The comparisons indicated that the stimulating effects of 
294 
VRMS on the brain’s functional network connectivity were more pronounced 
295 
than those observed with the other two tasks. Specifically, the VRMS results 
296 
exhibited stronger functional connections in bilateral, heterogeneous, and 
297 
homologous brain networks, particularly in the left SMA and PM regions. 
298 
During VRMS stimulation, there was an enhancement in the functional 
299 
connections of the bilateral PM and SMA regions with the RM1, RS1, 
300 
RDLPFC, and bilateral FPA regions. However, the enhancement observed 
301 
with VRMS was evident only in the HBT signal and not in HBO, potentially 
302 
due to factors such as stability, effect size, or sample size. 
303 
This indicates that VRMS enhances the frequency of functional 
304 
interactions among different brain regions and improves the efficiency of FC 
305 
within these areas. Notably, the PM and the SMA are closely linked to both 
306 
movement and beat perception and prediction(Cannon & Patel, 2021; Guida 
307 
et al., 2023). This association motivated the investigation of the specific 
308 
impact of musical stimulation on the PM and SMA. 
309 
 
310 
4.1. Specific effects of MS on the functional networks of motor and cognitive-
311 
related cortical regions 
312 
In the non-motor state, MS facilitates the functional interaction between motor 
313 
and cognitive-related brain areas. MS can induce rhythm perception in the 
314 
brain, a complex activity requiring the collaboration of the auditory, cognitive, 
315 
and motor cortices. According to Merchant et al.(Merchant et al., 2015) study, 
316 
eNeuro Accepted Manuscript

## Page 11

beat perception is based on the motor cortico-basal-ganglia-thalamo-cortical 
317 
(mCBGT) circuit, which transmits information via delta and beta oscillatory 
318 
activities. During beat perception, the relevant effectors in motion-related 
319 
areas activate simultaneously, enabling interaction between auditory and 
320 
motion-related brain regions. 
321 
According to the auditory perception-induced action simulation for auditory 
322 
prediction (ASAP) theory, brain regions responsible for movement are 
323 
activated during beat perception, specifically the SMA and dorsal 
324 
striatum(Cannon & Patel, 2021). The term “groove” to describe the sensation 
325 
of movement elicited by music. Subsequent studies have shown that 
326 
predictive coding also applies to the phenomenon of “groove”(Vuust et al., 
327 
2022; Chen et al., 2025). This predictive coding helps minimize errors during 
328 
“groove”-related activities. Matthews et al. suggested that motor timing and 
329 
reward elements associated with the groove experience are linked to specific 
330 
cortex-striatal pathways. When exposed to music or rhythm, the putamen, 
331 
SMA, and PM develop internal representations of the beat that interact with 
332 
the caudate as well as prefrontal and parietal regions(Matthews et al., 2020). 
333 
Furthermore, Toiviainen et al. found that the functional connection strength 
334 
and interaction patterns of SMA and PM-related brain networks can be 
335 
influenced by varying beat levels. Low-beat musical stimulation increased the 
336 
coupling response between the SMA and PM, potentially relating to 
337 
movement occurrence or its prediction. In contrast, high-tempo stimulation 
338 
strengthened connectivity between the SMA and the cerebellum, causing a 
339 
shift in main network connections from the motor cortex to the cerebellum. 
340 
Thus, previous studies have indicated that the SMA is a crucial brain region 
341 
for the functional coupling of auditory and sensorimotor regions during rhythm 
342 
and beat perception(Toiviainen et al., 2020). 
343 
In this study, the FC between the SMA and associated brain regions was 
344 
enhanced under VRMS. This enhancement was particularly notable in regions 
345 
related to cognition, motor function, and sensation in the right hemisphere 
346 
(RM1, RS1, and RDLPFC). These findings suggest a coupling response of 
347 
multiple brain areas, indicating a tendency toward right-brain lateralization 
348 
during processing. Grossbach et al. similarly observed that bilateral frontal 
349 
and temporal brain regions exhibited sustained cortical activation during the 
350 
processing of musical rhythm and beat, with a right-brain processing 
351 
advantage. The right hemisphere is generally considered the primary region 
352 
involved in music processing. Furthermore, the auditory, cognitive, and motor-
353 
eNeuro Accepted Manuscript

## Page 12

related regions of the right hemisphere show certain processing advantages 
354 
during rhythm and beat processing(Kuck et al., 2003). 
355 
This study’s results, when combined with previous research, suggest that 
356 
VRMS may induce brain-network interactions with a right-lateralized 
357 
processing tendency. This primarily occurs through enhanced FC in SMA-
358 
related brain regions. This pattern differs from the brain-network configuration 
359 
stimulated by motor paradigms such as AO and MI and aligns more closely 
360 
with the characteristic changes induced by music. These findings may offer a 
361 
theoretical foundation for the clinical application of VRMS in treatment.

4.2. Advantages and prospects of VR technology 
364 
VR is a three-dimensional (3D) computer-generated simulation that allows 
365 
individuals to interact with a virtual environment in a seemingly realistic 
366 
manner through electronic devices(Emmelkamp & Meyerbröker, 2021). 
367 
Immersive VR provides users with a subjective sense of immersion. Its high 
368 
flexibility enables integration with various conventional cognitive and 
369 
behavioral therapy methods, fostering the development of innovative 
370 
treatment strategies and offering broad application prospects(Bruno et al., 
371 
2022). VR technology has the potential to induce neuroplasticity through 
372 
several specific mechanisms. These mechanisms include enhancing 
373 
interhemispheric balance, strengthening cortical functional connections, and 
374 
increasing the mapping between muscles and the cerebral cortex of an 
375 
affected limb. Additionally, VR can simultaneously improve neuroplasticity and 
376 
related behavioral indicators, activate frontal cortex regions, and potentially 
377 
involve mirror neurons(Hao et al., 2022). 
378 
VR has gained popularity as a technology in recent years. Whether used 
379 
as a primary treatment modality or as an assistive application, VR offers 
380 
excellent flexibility and controllability. It can be seamlessly integrated with 
381 
conventional rehabilitation therapy to address limitations of traditional 
382 
treatments and enhance therapeutic outcomes. For example, combining VR 
383 
with AO, motor imagery (MI), or mental simulation techniques can augment 
384 
the original therapeutic effects(Choi et al., 2020). 
385 
The combination of VR and AO effectively enhances functional integration 
386 
between mirror neurons and the sensorimotor cortex, as well as the activity of 
387 
associated neurons(Fan & Luo, 2022; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2023). VR, 
388 
when paired with walking tasks, offers diverse scenarios for gait training in 
389 
eNeuro Accepted Manuscript

## Page 13

individuals with hemiplegia following a stroke(Chen et al., 2016). Additionally, 
390 
the integration of VR and imagery therapy can significantly improve upper-
391 
limb function(Jo et al., 2024). Moreover, VR can be incorporated into 
392 
exposure therapy to simulate situations such as highly stressful scenes and 
393 
social interactions, benefiting individuals with anxiety disorders, post-traumatic 
394 
stress disorder, and other psychological issues(Natali et al., 2024). 
395 
In summary, integrating VR technology has the potential to revitalize 
396 
traditional rehabilitation methods by fostering a more diverse and adaptable 
397 
approach. This integration might decrease labor costs for hospitals and 
398 
rehabilitation institutions while addressing the limitations inherent in standard 
399 
rehabilitation practices. Additionally, it could overcome site constraints, offer 
400 
economic advantages, and ultimately contribute to technological 
401 
advancement(Stamm et al., 2020).

4.3. Limitations 
404 
A limitation of this study was its inclusion of only young participants aged 
405 
18–22 years, which resulted in an insufficient sample size and age 
406 
stratification. The fNIRS data collection process is vulnerable to various 
407 
factors, including environmental lighting, the subjects’ skull thickness, and 
408 
gender, which can cause unstable reception of the light source. Moreover, the 
409 
fNIRS technique is prone to optical interference from hair and scalp, 
410 
introducing uncertainty into the experimental data. Additionally, due to the 
411 
delay in hemodynamic response, the time resolution of fNIRS is low, 
412 
complicating the capture of the entire task effect. 
413 
This research necessitated complex data processing and analysis, 
414 
including noise removal and data correction, which increased the complexity 
415 
and duration of data processing, as well as the uncertainty of experimental 
416 
results. Our study was limited by the 24 channels of fNIRS, which can only 
417 
cover the cognitive and motor brain regions, without the ability to 
418 
simultaneously monitor the visual or auditory ROIs. Consequently, further 
419 
discussion on the effect of integrating VR with conventional rehabilitation 
420 
technology on brain-stimulation was not possible. Future studies should 
421 
consider using multichannel fNIRS that covers the entire brain region to 
422 
investigate VR’s effect comprehensively. 
423 
Moreover, the task sequence in our experiment could introduce temporal 
424 
sequence-induced fixed effects, posing another limitation. The order of tasks 
425 
eNeuro Accepted Manuscript

## Page 14

could influence experimental outcomes due to neural adaptation and resource 
426 
depletion mechanisms. Particular attention should be given to cumulative 
427 
neuroplastic effects, especially in VRAO and VRMI paradigms. In our study, 
428 
HBO and HBT signals served as primary data, demonstrating rapid baseline 
429 
recovery (5–7 s) under non-stimulated states. When integrated with a block-
430 
averaging paradigm methodology, these design features could reduce 
431 
temporal sequence-induced fixed effects. 
432

5. Conclusions 
434 
VR combined with conventional rehabilitation represents an innovative 
435 
approach in neural stimulation. The findings suggest that the stimulating 
436 
effects of VRMS may enhance brain networks, particularly in PM and SMA-
437 
related regions. These results highlight the potential of VR technology for 
438 
future clinical applications. Future research should explore the efficacy of 
439 
integrating VR with conventional rehabilitation therapies to advance both VR 
440 
technology exploration and its clinical implementation.
