THE AIM OF THIS SERIES OF experiments was to
determine whether consonant and dissonant chords
elicit similar or different electrophysiological effects out
of a musical context and whether these effects are simi-
lar or different for musicians and nonmusicians. To this
end, we recorded the event-related brain potentials
(ERPs) elicited by the different intervals of the chro-
matic scale that were classified into three categories:
perfect consonances, imperfect consonances, and dis-
sonances. Participants were to decide, on a six-point
scale, whether the intervals evoked pleasant or unpleas-
ant feelings. To test the hypothesis that the perception
of dissonance results from the superposition of the par-
tials of close frequencies (Helmholtz, 1877), two notes
were either played together (harmonic intervals) or suc-
cessively (melodic intervals). Since, in this latter case,
the two notes are played at different points in time, the
perception of roughness, if any, should be weaker
than for harmonic intervals. In line with Helmholtz’s
hypothesis, results showed larger differences for har-
monic than for melodic intervals, which were mainly
found on the N1-P2 complex for musicians, on the N2
component for nonmusicians, and on a later negative
component for both musicians and nonmusicians. However, these results also point to the influence of
expertise and cultural factors, since different results
were obtained when ERPs were averaged as a function
of music theory and according to the participants’
responses. Received January 22, 2002, accepted February 21, 2004

HEN
TWO
NOTES are played simultane-
ously, they are perceived differently and they
may evoke different sensations depending
on the musical interval between them. They can be
perceived as consonant and possibly evoke a pleasant
sensation, or they can be perceived as dissonant and
possibly evoke an unpleasant sensation. Of course,
these pleasant/unpleasant sensations may depend on
the listener’s expertise and familiarity with the type of
music and may therefore be highly subjective. Interestingly, however, several theories and hypotheses
have been proposed to explain the origin of consonance
and dissonance. In the 5th century B. C., Pythagoras was
one of the first to notice that two sounds bound by a
simple frequency ratio (1:1, 2:1, 3:2, 4:3) produced a
more consonant ensemble than more complex ratios
(e.g., 16:15). Later, in his well-known theory of conso-
nance, which still remains a reference nowadays, Helmholtz (1877) associated the feeling of dissonance
with the presence of beats between pure tones (or par-
tials of complex tones) of neighboring frequencies,
giving rise to a sensation of roughness, proportional
to the frequency of the beats. Thus, according to
Helmholtz, this sensation would have a physiological
origin, while still being influenced by other factors,
such as subjective and cultural differences. This con-
ception is in line with recent theories that distinguish
between sensory consonance and contextual conso-
nance (see Tenney, 1995). To our knowledge, most of the research on conso-
nance in psychomusicology has been aimed at studying
consonance in context, and less out of a musical con-
text (Butler & Daston, 1963; Plomp & Levelt, 1965). However, as Costa, Bitti, and Bonfiglioli (2000) point
out, the study of intervals in isolation is important for

that influence the perception of consonance (modality,
tempo, intensity, rhythm, timbre, etc.). Second, results
of certain studies (Balzano & Liesch, 1982; Cohen, Granot, Pratt, & Barneah, 1993) point to the fact that, to
a certain extent, the perception of intervals can be inde-
pendent of the particular context. This conclusion has,
however, been challenged by other results. For example,
when participants are asked to categorize, on a seman-
tic scale, the 12 possible bichords of a tempered scale, Bozzi (1985) found a clear correspondence between the
theoretical description and the results for the dissonant
bichords and the octave but not for the consonant
bichords. Maher (1980) also showed that, out of the 14
musical intervals used in his experiment, 7 gave no dif-
ference for the measure used in the experiment. Thus,
results did not clearly conform to music theory. Finally, Costa et al. (2000) studied the effect of theoretical con-
sonance on the expressive characteristics of the inter-
vals. Musicians and nonmusicians were required to
judge intervals on a semantic differential, made up of
30 bipolar adjective rating scales (e.g., pleasant/unpleas-
ant). These scales were organized along three factors:
emotional evaluation, activity, and potency. Results
showed that, on scales testing the emotional factor,
dissonant bichords were perceived as negatively polar-
ized, while consonant bichords spread out over the neu-
tral zone. The authors hypothesized that “emotional
characterization of these (consonant) intervals is more
influenced by the context in which they are arranged,
than by the interval per se” (Costa et al., 2000). Interestingly, results did not show any influence of
musical expertise. While few experiments have been conducted to
understand the perceptual, cognitive, and emotional
aspects of consonance, even fewer studies have been
aimed at analyzing its neurophysiological basis. Except
for the positron-emission tomography (PET) experi-
ment by Blood, Zatorre, Bermudez, and Evans (1999),
which studied the cerebral blood flow changes related
to affective responses to pieces of music that varied
systematically in degree of consonance, the other stud-
ies have used the event-related brain potential (ERP)
method. Because of its excellent temporal resolution,
the ERP method provides a unique way to study per-
ceptual, cognitive, and emotional processes as they
unfold in time. It then becomes possible to precisely
determine at which point in time the ERPs elicited
in two experimental conditions start to diverge, and
by inference, to determine when the two underlying
processes start to differ. The experiments conducted
so far with the ERP method mainly focused on
consonance and dissonance of chords or notes pre-
sented in a musical context. Levett and Martin (1992)
and Regnault, Bigand, and Besson (2001) showed that
the occurrence of dissonant chords at the end of har-
monic progressions is associated with a larger positive
component, peaking around 600 ms (P600), compared
to consonant chords. This P600 component has also
been found in response to out-of-key notes (Besson &
Macar, 1987; Besson & Faïta, 1995) or chords (Janata,
1995; Patel, 1998; but see also Koelsch, 2000, for other
music-related electrophysiological components) within
a musical context. Taken together, these results have
led to the hypothesis that the P600 reflects the difficulty
in integrating a note or a chord into a musical context,
rather than the processing of dissonance per se. In order
to exclude the potential influence of musical expec-
tancy, it is therefore of interest to study the effect of
consonance/dissonance out of a musical context. The aim of the present experiment is twofold. First,
in order to study the neurophysiological basis of conso-
nance, it is important to determine whether consonant
and dissonant chords are associated with different elec-
trophysiological components or modulation of a same
component when presented out of a musical context. To
this end, we recorded the ERPs elicited by the different
intervals of the chromatic scale. Participants had to
judge, on a six-point scale, whether the intervals evoked
a pleasant feeling or not. Based on intuitive knowledge
and on the results of Costa et al. (2000), we expected
dissonant intervals to be perceived as less pleasant than
consonant ones. Of interest was to determine whether
such a differential perception would be associated with
different ERP effects. Second, it was of interest to test
the hypothesis that, as proposed by Helmholtz (1877),
the perception of dissonance results from the super-
position of the partials of close frequencies. In order to do
so, two notes, with different intervals of the chromatic
scale, were played either together (harmonic intervals)
or successively as melodic intervals. Since in this latter
case the two notes are played at different points in time,
the perception of roughness, if any, should be weaker
than for harmonic intervals. Thus, the ERP effects asso-
ciated with the sensory component of dissonance, if
any, should be smaller for melodic than for harmonic
intervals. Moreover, the differences between intervals
should occur earlier, at a sensory stage of processing, for
harmonic intervals than for melodic intervals. To be
able to compare results for melodic and harmonic inter-
vals, we classified melodic intervals according to the
same rules as those typically used in music theory to
classify harmonic intervals. This choice is supported by
the theoretical work of Leman (1995), showing that the

showed that participants similarly categorized melodic

pure tones, the possibility remains that the absence of
harmonics might have artificially decreased the differ-
ences between the two types of intervals. As mentioned above, the perception of consonance
can result both from the physical characteristics of
the sounds, played simultaneously or successively, and
from the subjective “interpretation” of the sounds,
based on our implicit or explicit knowledge of the musi-
cal system that we are most familiar with. In order to
test these two possibilities, two types of analyses were
computed on the ERP data. First, ERPs elicited by the
different musical intervals were sorted as a function of
their physical characteristics that, according to some
music theories, distinguish three main categories of
intervals: perfect consonance, imperfect consonance,
and dissonance. Note, however, that results of psychoa-
coustic research have pointed out that the distinction
between consonant and dissonant intervals is better
considered as a continuum rather than as discrete cate-
gories. Thus, the classification of intervals that we used
may seem arbitrary. However, it was useful for method-
ological reasons, linked with the need to average several
trials from the same condition to obtain clear ERP data
while maintaining the duration of the experiment at a
reasonable length. Further, as mentioned above, it is
in agreement with some music theories. Second, ERPs
were sorted according to whether the intervals were
judged as pleasant or unpleasant by the participants,
independently of whether such categorization was in
line with music theory or not. Hence, an effect observed
only when data are sorted as a function of music theory
(based on the acoustic properties of intervals) should
reflect interval discrimination based on sensory pro-
cessing, that is, on the interaction between the acoustic
characteristics of the stimulus and the filtering pro-
perties of the auditory periphery. By contrast, an effect
observed only when data are sorted as a function of
the responses should reflect interval discrimination
based on perceived consonance including both sen-
sory and cognitive components. Taken together, results
from both analyses should therefore allow us to disen-
tangle bottom-up processes, linked with the basic
acoustic characteristics of the intervals, from top-down
processes responsible for the different feelings associ-
ated with the different intervals. Finally, as the percep-
tion of consonance may differ as a function of the
expertise of the participants (Van de Geer, Levelt, &
Plomp, 1962), we compared musicians, who had at least
10 years of music training, and nonmusicians, with no
specific training and who were not music lovers. Method
Participants
Twelve experienced musicians and 13 nonmusicians
(mean age: 26 years old) participated in this study. However, 5 participants were excluded from further
analyses due to a large number of artifacts (mainly
blinks and eye movements), so that each group com-
prised 10 participants. Stimuli
Stimuli consisted of series of two notes, composing the
different intervals of the chromatic scale: unisons (1),
minor and major seconds (2m and 2M), minor and
major thirds (3m and 3M), fourth (4), fifth (5), minor
and major sixths (6m and 6M), minor and major sev-
enths (7m and 7M), and octaves (8). As explained
above, these intervals were grouped into three cate-
gories: Perfect Consonances (PC: 1, 4, 5, 8), Imperfect
Consonances (IP: 3m, 3M, 6m, 6M), and Dissonances
(D: 2m, 2M, 7m, 7M). In one part of the study, the notes
were presented simultaneously (harmonic intervals),
and in the other part, they were presented successively
(melodic intervals). All participants listened to both
harmonic and melodic intervals. The order of presenta-
tion of melodic and harmonic intervals was the same
for all the participants, and the melodic intervals were
presented first.1
Procedure
Intervals were presented in 13 blocks, corresponding to
the 13 notes of the scale. Within each block, one note
was chosen as the base note of the interval (it could be
called the fundamental), and the 12 melodic intervals

Musicians tended to categorize harmonic and melodic
intervals according to music theory. Nevertheless, 44%
of the intervals were not categorized according to this
criterion. Moreover, nonmusicians did not follow the
rules of tonal music and showed a strong tendency to
categorize most of the intervals in the intermediate cat-
egory, that is, as quite pleasant. These results thus point
to the importance, for both musicians and nonmusi-
cians, of averaging ERPs according to not only music
theory but also participants’ responses. Comparison of the results for the melodic and har-
monic intervals showed that, for musicians, perfect
consonances were more often judged as very pleasant
for melodic (67%) than for harmonic intervals (54%,
2(1)  24.7, p .001). Note, however, that this is
mostly due to the fact that while perfect consonances in
melodic intervals also included the unison, the most
consonant interval, this was not the case for harmonic
perfect consonant intervals. Indeed, when the unison is
removed from the analyses, the percentage of melodic
intervals judged very pleasant drops to 61% (however,
the difference remains significant, 2(1)  9.2, p .005). Dissonant intervals were more often judged as disso-
nant for harmonic than for melodic intervals, and this
was found for both musicians (59% vs. 44%,
2(1)  9.6, p .005) and nonmusicians (46% vs. 33%,
2(1)  10.5, p .001). This result conforms to our
prediction, based on the theory of acoustic roughness,
following which the beating produced by the simultane-
ous emission of two sounds with close partials will pro-
duce a sensation of roughness, unpleasant to the ear. However, roughness alone cannot explain why the
melodic dissonant intervals were also judged as
unpleasant, since the notes were separated by a 750 ms
silence. It may be that musicians transpose their har-
monic knowledge to melodic intervals, so that they
apply the same rules in both cases. Whereas nonmusi-
cians have no explicit harmonic knowledge that would
help them correctly recognize and assign the intervals
to the appropriate theoretical category, an interval-by-
interval analysis (see Figure 3) suggests that nonmusi-
cians did use a different strategy. They seem to rely on a
principle of proximity: the farther away, the more
unpleasant. Thus, for instance, while musicians consid-

nonmusicians considered only 20% of the melodic

Discussion
When data are averaged as a function of music theory,
results do not show any significant effect of consonance. Thus, two notes separated by a silence seem to be
processed similarly whatever the interval between
them. By contrast, when data are averaged as a function
of participants’ responses, the effect of response type is
significant. However, the relatively late latency
(400-600 ms) of this effect suggests a cognitive rather
than a perceptual process. As mentioned above, the
decision to be made was probably more difficult for the
intervals in the intermediate category than for the inter-
vals in the other two categories. This is not surprising
insofar as the characteristics of perfect consonances and
dissonances are more salient. Thus, our results seem to
indicate that, in line with our hypotheses, perceived
consonance of melodic intervals did not influence sen-
sory/perceptual processing of notes but rather later cog-
nitive computations. Finally, it is interesting to note that
the effect of musical expertise was not significant in any
latency band considered for analysis. General Discussion and Conclusion
One major aim of our experiment was to investigate
whether the processing of consonance/dissonance would
be associated with specific ERP effects. Our results show
differences depending on the consonance of musical
intervals, for both musicians and nonmusicians. More-
over, the effect of consonance on the processing of musi-
cal intervals differs according to expertise and to the
mode of interval presentation (i.e., melodic vs. harmonic
intervals). Finally, the comparison of our results with
those of previous experiments clearly points to the strong
influence of musical context. These results add argu-
ments in favor of both a physiological origin and a psy-
chological origin (linked with the listener’s expertise and
cultural factors) to the sensation of consonance. It is,
however, difficult to propose one single model that could
account for the different aspects involved in the process-
ing of consonance. Indeed, several factors, such as musi-
cal expertise, the simultaneous or successive presentation
of notes, and the musical context, lead to qualitatively
different neural processing. Thus, even one of the most
basic perceptual characteristics of sounds, consonance,
does not involve a simple process. Our results point to
the complexity of the cerebral processes involved in
sound perception and, even more so, in music percep-
tion. In the following we will consider the effect of con-
sonance on the processing of intervals as a function of
three factors: expertise, simultaneity, and context. Expertise
Our results clearly show an influence of musical expert-
ise on the perception of consonance when intervals are
presented out of a musical context. While nonmusicians
process harmonic intervals differently as a function of
their acoustic characteristics, musicians discriminate
musical stimuli earlier as a function of perceived conso-
nance. These two types of categorization are reflected
by different variations of the electrophysiological com-
ponents elicited by perfect consonances: a larger N2
component for nonmusicians and a more positive
N1-P2 complex for musicians. Auditory cortex plastic-
ity may, at least to some extent, explain this effect. Functional reorganization of the auditory cortex after
cochlear lesions or repetitive auditory stimulation has
been shown in animals (see Rauschecker, 1999, for a
review). Moreover, Weinberger (1998) was able to pro-
duce conditioning in guinea pigs by associating the
presentation of a sound of given frequency with an
aversive stimulus. Results showed an increase of the size
of the receptor fields in the primary auditory cortex that
corresponded to the conditioned sounds, while the size
of receptor fields corresponding to nonconditioned
sounds decreased. The author interpreted these data in
terms of “physiological memory of auditory cortex,”
reflecting the “acquired meaning of a stimulus.” Thus,
some type of functional reorganization of the auditory
cortex, as found by Weinberger (1998) in animals, may
occur in humans. Music training, especially when
started at an early age, by favoring a long and sustained
associative learning between a given interval and an
emotion, may increase the cortical representations of
sounds for musicians. Such results have indeed been
reported in the literature (see for instance Schlaug, Jancke, Huang, Staiger, & Steinmetz, 1995; Pantev &
Lütkenhöner, 1998; Pantev, Roberts, Schulz, Engelien,
& Ross, 2001). Simultaneity
Results differed for harmonic and melodic intervals. Whereas for harmonic intervals the consonance effects
start as early as 100 ms (for musicians in the averages as
a function of the responses) or 200 ms (for nonmusi-
cians in the averages as a function of music theory) after
stimulus onset, no effect is found before 400 ms for
melodic intervals. Thus, as expected on the basis of
the theory of consonance, the sensation of roughness
seems intrinsically linked with the simultaneous pres-
entation of notes, out of musical context. In other
words, the effect of consonance, whether reflecting

Our results show that the consonance of intervals
presented out of musical context modulates the ampli-
tude of an N1/P2 complex, an N2 component, and a
negative component peaking between 400 and 600 ms
post-stimulus onset. These negative components differ
from the effect of consonance found when chords are
presented in a musical context. Indeed, as mentioned in
the introduction, dissonant chords ending musical
phrases elicit the occurrence of positive components,
with different latencies but often referred to as P600
components (Levett & Martin, 1992; Regnault et al.,
2001). Note, however, that a positive component, peak-
ing around 600 ms post-stimulus onset, can be seen in
response to both harmonic and melodic intervals in
both musicians and nonmusicians. Interestingly, the
amplitude of this positivity is larger in response to per-
fect consonance and dissonance than to imperfect con-
sonances when ERPs are averaged as a function of music
theory (see Figure 4). Similarly, its amplitude is larger for
very pleasant and unpleasant intervals when ERPs are
averaged as a function of the participants’ responses (see
Figures 5 and 6). Therefore, such an ordering does not
correspond to what would be expected based on the
literature related to the musical P600. Rather, one would
expect the dissonant (or unpleasant) intervals to be
associated with the largest positivity, the imperfect
consonance (or quite pleasant) intervals to be associ-
ated with intermediate positivity, and the perfect conso-
nance (or very pleasant) intervals to be associated with
the smallest positivity. Because we did not find such
relationships, we tend to consider that the interpreta-
tion proposed above, in terms of categorization diffi-
culty of imperfect consonance (or quite pleasant)
intervals compared to the other two categories, accounts
better for our pattern of results. Taken together, the
findings reported in the literature and the present
results underline the importance of a musical context in
generating specific expectancies regarding the acousti-
cal characteristics of chords. When no musical context
is presented, as in this experiment, the functional inter-
pretation of the late positivity may differ from the
current functional interpretation of the P600 in terms
of the integration cost of an unexpected event in a
musical context (Patel, 1998). Author Note
