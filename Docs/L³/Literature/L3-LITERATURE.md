## Part 1: Per-Group Citation Map

### β — Neuroscience (Variable D)

- **Salimpoor et al. (2011)**: Anatomically distinct dopamine release during anticipation and experience of peak emotion to music
  → Supports: β NAcc activation (β0), caudate anticipation (β1), VTA dopamine (β2)
  [Effect: NAcc release during peak pleasure, caudate during anticipation; PET + fMRI, N=8]
- **Salimpoor et al. (2013)**: Interactions between the nucleus accumbens and auditory cortices predict music reward value
  → Supports: β NAcc-auditory cortex connectivity, reward prediction
  [Effect: NAcc-STG connectivity predicts purchase price, r=0.38, N=19]
- **Blood & Zatorre (2001)**: Intensely pleasurable responses to music correlate with activity in brain regions implicated in reward and emotion
  → Supports: β reward region activation, β DA release proxy
  [Effect: rCBF increases in NAcc, VTA, insula during chills; PET, N=10]
- **Koelsch (2006)**: Significance of Broca's area and ventral premotor cortex for music-syntactic processing
  → Supports: β IFG activation (syntactic processing), motor cortex engagement
  [Effect: ERAN in IFG/BA44 for harmonic violations; fMRI, N=18]
- **Zatorre et al. (2007)**: When the brain plays music: Auditory-motor interactions in music perception and production
  → Supports: β motor region activation, auditory-motor coupling
- **Koelsch et al. (2006)**: Investigating emotion with music: An fMRI study
  → Supports: β amygdala, hippocampus, insula activation mapping
  [Effect: Amygdala response to dissonance; hippocampus to familiarity; fMRI, N=11]

### γ — Psychology (13D)

- **Berridge (2003)**: Pleasures of the brain

- **Huron (2006)**: Sweet Anticipation — Music and the Psychology of Expectation

- **de Fleurian & Pearce (2021)**: Chills in music: A systematic review

  [Meta-analysis: k=116 studies, d=0.85 for chill-related physiological changes]
- **Blood & Zatorre (2001)**: (see β section)

- **Huron (2006)**: (see γ section)

- **Berlyne (1971)**: Aesthetics and Psychobiology

- **Zajonc (1968)**: Attitudinal effects of mere exposure
